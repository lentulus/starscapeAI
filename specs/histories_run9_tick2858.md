# Starscape 5 — Histories of the First Fifty-Five Years
*Simulation Run 9 · Tick 2858 · April 2526 – January 2581*

---

## I. Earth and Its Inheritors

Three human polities departed the same homeworld. System 1157617 — a body of potential 7, orbiting a star whose coordinates place it in the outer rim of the simulation space — was the cradle of Oceania, Eurasia, and Eastasia simultaneously. They launched their first scouts in the same week of April 2526. Within fifteen ticks, they were at war with each other.

No account survives of what diplomatic contact, if any, preceded the first exchange of fire. The simulation records only outcomes: by tick 15, the three human polities had declared mutual hostilities, and the state of war that began that July has not ended. For fifty-five years, humans have been killing humans.

The wars are not equal. **Eurasia** emerged as the dominant human power, expanding to 22 presences — more than any other polity in the known galaxy — under a succession of three admirals. The first, **Julia**, held command from the opening ticks through the early expansion phase. Julia was followed by **Rutherford** (Task Force rating +3), who prosecuted the middle period of Eurasian growth, pushing scouts and fleets into systems that no other polity had yet mapped. Rutherford's successor, **Tillotson** (also TF+3), commands Eurasian forces at the time of this writing.

**Oceania** and **Eastasia** have expanded more modestly, constrained both by the three-way human war and by a fourth conflict that arrived from outside. In the early months of the simulation, **Nhaveth Court A** — one of three successor courts of the Nhaveth civilization, located far from the human sphere — declared war on all three human polities in a single tick. The motivation is not recorded. The effect was to add a fourth front to humanity's wars before the first decade had ended.

The most violent single event in human space occurred on **7 June 2548**, when Nhaveth A and Nhaveth Court C jointly bombarded **system 1130253** — a Eurasian presence. The bombardment is the only such event recorded in the entire simulation to date. Eurasia survived it; the presence at 1130253 persisted.

By January 2581, the three human polities together hold fewer presences than Eurasia alone. They share a homeworld, share a species, and have not stopped fighting for a single tick of the simulation.

## II. The Serial Wars of Nakhavi Reach

Of all the polities in the simulation, **Nakhavi Reach** has chosen the most consistently martial path. Where other species fought one war and sued for peace, or stumbled into conflict and retreated, Nakhavi has accumulated wars the way an empire accumulates territory — deliberately, serially, each new conflict beginning before the last has resolved.

The record stands at five simultaneous active wars, spanning four decades.

**The First War — vs. Golvhaan Reach (begun ~2529)**
The opening conflict came early, within the first few years of interstellar expansion. Golvhaan Reach, another polity of moderate ambition, found itself the first target of Nakhavi aggression. The war has continued for over fifty years without recorded resolution.

**The Second War — vs. Skharri Pride (begun ~2537)**
A decade into the Golvhaan conflict, Nakhavi opened a second front against **Skharri Pride**. Skharri, a polity whose name implies martial culture even in translation, became the second concurrent war. By this point Nakhavi was fighting on two fronts while still expanding.

**The Third War — vs. Nhaveth Court A (begun ~2544)**
Eighteen years into Nakhavi's interstellar history, war came with **Nhaveth Court A**. The Nhaveth courts — three separate successor polities from the same species — are themselves in conflict with the human polities, making Nhaveth A one of the more embattled civilizations in the simulation. Nakhavi's declaration added a third active war.

**The Fourth War — vs. Oceania (begun ~2548)**
At tick ~1100, corresponding to approximately 2548, Nakhavi declared war on **Oceania** — one of the three human polities. The human sphere was already in the middle of its own multi-front conflict; Nakhavi's entry added another pressure on the human position.

**The Fifth War — vs. Shekhari Exchange (begun ~2562)**
The most recent addition to the Nakhavi war ledger, declared approximately 36 years into the simulation, brought **Shekhari Exchange** into the conflict. At the time of writing, all five wars remain active.

The cost of this policy is legible in the fleet records. Nakhavi has logged **245 scrapping events** — destroyed or decommissioned hulls — the highest in the simulation. Against this loss must be weighed 5 presences, a modest territorial footprint for a polity that has fought so many enemies. The Nakhavi expansion rate is low; the attrition rate is high.

Whether the Serial Wars represent strategic aggression — a deliberate policy of keeping neighbours off-balance — or a pathological decision engine that cannot distinguish between provocation and opportunity is a question the simulation does not answer. The record shows only that Nakhavi fights, and fights, and fights again.

## III. Eurasia — The Furthest Reach

In the first fifty-five years of interstellar civilization, no polity has placed more flags than **Eurasia**. Twenty-two presences, spanning systems from the human homeworld region outward into space that no other sophont has surveyed — this is the Eurasian legacy at tick 2858.

The achievement is more remarkable set against its context. Eurasia has been at war with both of its sibling human polities since tick 15. It has fought Nhaveth Court A. It absorbed a bombardment. It has done all of this while maintaining the most aggressive expansion programme in the galaxy.

The instrument of this expansion has been three admirals, each effective, each handing off a going concern to a capable successor.

**Julia** held command in the opening phase, directing the first scouts outward from system 1157617. Julia's tenure established the pattern: aggressive scouting priority, presence-building in high-potential systems, fleet maintenance sufficient to deter or repel challengers. The tactical foundation Julia built allowed the admirals who followed to operate offensively rather than defensively.

**Rutherford** (Task Force +3) prosecuted the middle expansion, the period of fastest growth. The TF+3 rating — three levels above baseline task force competence — meant Eurasian fleets under Rutherford hit harder and absorbed more damage than their numerical equivalents. Systems that might have required two campaigns fell in one. The jump network extended. The 22-presence count is substantially Rutherford's achievement.

**Tillotson** (TF+3) commands today, inheriting a mature expansion with deep jump routes and a treasury position sufficient to maintain what Rutherford built. Whether Tillotson will push further, or whether Eurasia has reached the natural limit of manageable overextension, the simulation has not yet shown.

The runner-up deserves mention. **Nhaveth Court C** holds 20 presences — two fewer than Eurasia — but has accomplished this under a single admiral who has served for the entire fifty-five years of the simulation. **NHV-ADM-0001**, rated TF-2 (two levels below baseline), has compensated for tactical limitations with strategic persistence. Beginning from the Nhaveth homeworld in a region of the galaxy entirely separate from the human sphere, Court C pushed scouts steadily outward, eventually reaching systems near the **Kreeth Dominion** — at which point Kreeth declared war (approximately 2567) and the expansion slowed.

The contrast between the two leading polities is instructive. Eurasia: three admirals, all capable, high task force ratings, rapid expansion through decisive action. Nhaveth Court C: one admiral, below-average tactical rating, forty years in post, expansion through sheer operational continuity. Both reached approximately the same result. The simulation appears agnostic about method.

At the halfway point of Run 9, Eurasia leads the galaxy. The second fifty-five years will determine whether the lead holds.
