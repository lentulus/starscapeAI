## Systematic anomalies

### 1. Eccentricity cap at e = 0.97
Many bodies show exactly e=0.97, indicating a hard cap in the planet generator. Real orbits at e=0.97 are extremely elongated ‚ÄĒ periapsis is only 3% of SMA. Combined with large SMAs this often places periapsis inside the star or within the Hill sphere of another planet.

### 2. SMA cap at 50.0 AU
Multiple outer planets and moons sit at exactly 50.0 AU, indicating another generator cap. A planet at 50 AU with e=0.97 has periapsis at 1.5 AU and apoapsis at 98.5 AU ‚ÄĒ a wildly crossing orbit.

### 3. Periapsis inside the star
For extended stars (giants), the inner planets have computed periapsis distances shorter than the stellar radius:
- System 898454 (K1III, R‚ėÖ=0.0453 AU): body 401 peri = 0.0144 AU ‚Üí inside star
- System 918040 (K3IV, R‚ėÖ=0.00303 AU): body 434 peri = 0.00336 AU ‚Üí just outside but grazing
- System 1066934 (G5V, R‚ėÖ=0.00470 AU): body 415 peri = 0.00154 AU ‚Üí inside star

### 4. Moon orbital periods longer than planetary years
Moon orbital periods are computed from `a¬≥/M_planet` where M_planet is in solar masses. Rocky planets with mass ~1 M‚äē (~3√ó10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∂ M‚ėČ) generate extremely long moon periods ‚ÄĒ moons of body 421 have orbital periods of 30,000‚Äď100,000 days (82‚Äď282 years). These moons would be outside or near the Hill sphere and dynamically unstable.

Hill sphere radius: `r_H = a_planet √ó (M_planet / 3M‚ėÖ)^(1/3)`
Example: body 420 (0.230 M‚äē, 1.27 AU around 1.0 M‚ėČ star):
`r_H = 1.27 √ó (6.9√ó10‚ĀĽ‚Ā∑ / 3)^(1/3) = 1.27 √ó 0.00622 = 0.0079 AU`
Moon 421 has SMA = 0.001544 AU ‚ÄĒ inside Hill sphere, so technically bound, but period at 82 yr implies a very slow drift relative to planet motion around star. The Kozai-Lidov mechanism would destabilise it on timescales much shorter than the simulation.

### 5. High inclinations throughout
Inclinations are distributed broadly across 0‚Äď180¬į, including many retrograde orbits (inc > 90¬į). This is a result of random generation rather than disk-formation physics. The viewer's 2D ecliptic projection therefore shows many orbits as strongly foreshortened ellipses rather than near-circles.
