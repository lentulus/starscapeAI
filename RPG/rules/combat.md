# Combat

## Overview

Combat in this game is dangerous, fast, and consequential. Characters
are not action heroes — wounds accumulate quickly, the environment is
often as threatening as the enemy, and the decision to fight is rarely
without cost. The system is designed to resolve violence quickly and
return the table to the broader situation, not to model tactical
complexity for its own sake.

Most combats should resolve in three to five rounds. If a combat is
going longer than that, something interesting should be happening —
a changing environment, a tactical shift, an opportunity to end it
differently — not more of the same exchange.

> **Roleplay note:** The decision to fight is a character decision,
> not a table default. In a setting where killing a Vardhek attaché
> is a diplomatic incident, where a Skharri's honour-instinct can
> start a war, and where the nearest medical support may be three
> jumps away, violence has consequences that extend well beyond the
> combat scene. Encourage players to think about what they are
> starting before they start it.

---

## The Shape of a Combat

A combat is divided into **rounds**. Each round, both sides act —
players first or NPCs first depending on initiative — before the
next round begins. A round represents a few seconds of intense
activity, not a precise unit of time.

Within a round, each character takes **one action**. An action is
one meaningful thing: attack, move to cover, assist a crewmate,
attempt a technical task under fire, communicate a complex instruction.
Simple things — dropping prone, drawing a weapon, shouting a warning
— do not cost an action.

---

## Initiative

### The Opening Round

At the start of combat, one side acts first. To determine which:

**The player group nominates one character** to make a **Wits roll**.
This represents the group's collective readiness — their situational
awareness, their speed off the mark, their ability to react as a unit.

**The GM rolls for the NPC group** — a single Wits roll representing
the opposing side's readiness.

**The side with more sixes acts first** in the opening round. On a
tie, the players act first.

This is one roll, immediately resolved. There is no individual
initiative order within sides.

### Surprise

If one side has genuinely surprised the other — through successful
Stealth, through an ambush that was not detected, through a situation
where the other side had no warning — the surprising side acts first
in the opening round automatically. No roll is made.

The surprised side **loses their action in Round 1**. They may take
cover, move, or react in limited ways, but they cannot make a
meaningful offensive action until Round 2.

Partial surprise — the other side knew something was coming but not
when or where — gives the surprising side first action with no roll,
but the surprised side retains their Round 1 action at a penalty die.

### Subsequent Rounds

After the opening round, sides alternate: **players then NPCs**,
or **NPCs then players** if the NPCs won initiative, repeating until
the combat ends.

### Communication and Coordination

Within the player group's turn, the players decide amongst themselves
who does what and in what order. **Communication is assumed** — the
crew can coordinate, warn each other, and adjust plans in real time,
because that is what trained people in communication with each other
actually do.

This coordination benefit applies when the players can genuinely
communicate. It does not apply when:

- Characters are in hard vacuum without functioning suit comms
- Characters are physically separated beyond communication range
- Communications are being actively jammed or disrupted
- A character is incapacitated, panicking, or otherwise unable
  to receive or process information

When coordination is lost, each character acts individually without
the benefit of shared planning. The GM will indicate when this applies.

> **Note for the GM:** The initiative structure above handles most
> situations cleanly. The GM may vary it when the fiction warrants —
> a complex tactical environment, a dramatic reversal mid-round, or
> a moment where the sequence of events demands something different.
> This is not a rules exception; it is the GM reading the table.

### NPC Initiative

**Mooks** act as a bloc — the GM makes one decision for the group
and rolls once on their behalf. They move together, fire together,
and break together.

**Named NPCs** act individually within the NPC turn, in whatever
order makes narrative sense. A named NPC whose specific instinct
or agenda has been triggered — a Skharri whose honour has been
directly challenged, a Vardhek who has identified a specific threat
to their strategic position — acts on that trigger, not on tactical
logic.

## Actions

Each character takes one action per round. Common actions:

**Attack** — make a combat roll against a target in range.
The most common action and described in full below.

**Move** — change position significantly. Moving to a new
range band, reaching cover, boarding or leaving a vehicle,
crossing difficult terrain. A Move action combined with an
attack is possible but both rolls take a penalty die.

**Assist** — help another character's action. Adds one bonus
die to a crewmate's roll this round. The assisting character
must be in a position to actually help — adjacent, in comms
contact, with relevant capability. See Group Actions below.

**First aid** — attempt emergency medical treatment on an
adjacent character using Science (Medic specialty applies).
Cannot be performed while under direct fire without a penalty die.

**Technical action** — attempt a technical task under pressure.
Operate a system, bypass a lock, restore a damaged component.
Any Intellect skill roll made under fire takes a penalty die.

**Communicate** — convey complex information to someone not
already in the coordination loop. Simple warnings are free;
a full tactical briefing costs an action.

**Disengage** — attempt to exit the combat without being
attacked. Opposed Move roll against any enemy in Close range.
On success, the character is out of the immediate combat.
On failure, they take a free attack from that enemy before
leaving.

## Making an Attack

### The Attack Roll

Build a dice pool as normal:

**Attribute** — Strength for Fight (melee), Agility for Shoot (ranged)

**Skill** — Fight or Shoot as appropriate

**Gear** — the weapon's gear rating

Roll the pool. Count sixes.

### Attack Results

| Sixes | Result |
|-------|--------|
| 0 | Miss. No effect. |
| 1 | Hit. Target takes one condition. |
| 2 | Clean hit. Target takes one condition; attacker chooses the nature of the condition. |
| 3 | Strong hit. Target takes two conditions, or one condition plus a tactical effect. |
| 4+ | Devastating hit. Target takes two conditions plus a tactical effect, or three conditions. |

**Tactical effects** (attacker's choice on 3+ sixes):
- Target is knocked back or down (costs their next action to recover)
- Target's weapon is damaged (gear rating reduced by 1)
- Target is disarmed
- Target is pushed into a worse position (exposed, closer to hazard)
- Attacker gains a bonus die on their next action against this target

### Choosing Not to Kill

By default, combat damage is injury and incapacitation, not death.
A character reduced to four conditions is incapacitated — unable
to act, but alive.

**Killing** requires a deliberate choice. A character who wishes
to kill a downed opponent states this and it happens — no roll
required, but it is a conscious decision with the full weight
that implies. In a setting where killing a Vardhek diplomat is
a strategic incident, this matters.

**Subduing** — attempting to knock out rather than injure — imposes
a penalty die on the attack roll. On success, conditions are
psychological (stunned, dazed) rather than physical, and the
target can be restrained rather than killed.

> **Roleplay note:** The choice not to kill is as significant as
> the choice to kill. A Skharri who subdues an opponent when honour
> demanded a killing blow has done something that their culture
> will note. A human operative who kills when subduing was possible
> has made a choice that follows them. Keep these moments visible.

## Defence

Defence in this game is **passive by default**. There is no defence
roll against every attack — the attacker rolls and the result stands,
modified by cover and circumstances.

### Cover

Cover removes dice from the attacker's pool before the roll.

| Cover | Dice Removed |
|-------|-------------|
| Light cover (furniture, partial concealment) | 1 die |
| Solid cover (doorframe, vehicle hull, rock) | 2 dice |
| Hard cover (reinforced position, full concealment) | 3 dice |

Cover must be declared before the attack roll. A character behind
cover must expose themselves to attack — leaning out to shoot,
opening a door — which may reduce or eliminate the cover benefit
at the GM's discretion.

### Active Defence

A character may choose to **actively defend** rather than act
offensively. This costs their action for the round. An actively
defending character adds their relevant skill as bonus dice to
resist the incoming attack — Fight for melee defence, Move for
evading ranged fire.

Active defence is resolved as an **opposed roll**: attacker's
pool versus defender's pool. Most sixes wins. On a tie, the
attack succeeds with minimal effect (one condition, attacker's
choice of type removed).

Active defence is a meaningful sacrifice — giving up your
action to reduce damage. It is most valuable when a character
is already in a bad position and cannot afford another condition.

### Armour

Armour adds **green dice** to the defender's pool when actively
defending, representing the gear quality of the protective
equipment. Armour does not reduce damage passively — it only
applies when the character is actively defending.

| Armour | Gear Rating | Notes |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| Civilian clothing | 0 | No protection |
| Light armour | 1 | Concealed, minimal restriction |
| Combat armour | 2 | Standard military; restricts Move slightly |
| Heavy armour | 3 | Significant restriction; penalty die on Move |
| Vacc suit (armoured) | 2 | Adds vacuum protection; see Environmental Hazards |

Armour that has taken significant damage degrades — reduce the
gear rating by 1 after any combat in which the character took
three or more conditions. Armour can be repaired with Tech.

## Range

Combat range is divided into three bands. Each weapon has an
**optimal band** where it performs at full effectiveness.
Outside the optimal band, the attack takes a penalty die.

| Band | Distance | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| Close | Arm's reach to a few metres | Melee range; confined spaces |
| Short | Same room to adjacent compartment | Most personal weapons |
| Long | Open space, corridors, across a bay | Rifles, ship weapons |

**Switching bands** costs a Move action — moving from Long to
Short, or Short to Close.

**Melee weapons** (Fight) are only effective at Close range.
Used at Short range, they take two penalty dice and cannot
be used at Long range at all.

**Personal ranged weapons** (Shoot) are optimal at Short range.
At Close range they take a penalty die (unwieldy, risk of
grabbing). At Long range they take a penalty die (effective
range exceeded).

**Rifles and longarms** are optimal at Long range. At Short
range, no penalty. At Close range, one penalty die.

**Thrown weapons** are optimal at Short range. At Close and
Long range, one penalty die each.

## Size in Combat

The size differences between species create genuine mechanical
asymmetry in close combat. This is not balanced — a Golvhaan
and a Shekhari in melee are not equivalent combatants, and
they should not be.

### Attacking Across Size Categories

**Attacking a larger opponent (melee):** If the target is more
than twice your mass, your Fight roll takes a penalty die.
Hitting something that large is not hard; making the hit matter
is.

**Attacking a smaller opponent (melee):** If the target is less
than half your mass, your Fight roll gains a bonus die. However,
if the intent is to subdue rather than injure — to grab, restrain,
or incapacitate without serious harm — the roll takes a penalty
die instead. Precision against a small fast target is hard.

**Ranged combat** is not affected by size differences except
that larger targets are easier to hit: Large species targets
give the attacker one bonus die on Shoot rolls at Short and
Long range.

### Size and Conditions

Large species (Golvhaan, Kraathi, Skharri) have a higher
physical resilience that translates mechanically to their
Hit Capacity (derived from Strength + Endure, which is higher
for large species). There is no special large-species damage
reduction — the higher Hit Capacity is the representation.

Small species (Shekhari, Nakhavi) have lower Hit Capacity
for the same reason. Their advantages in combat come from
their specialties (Small Profile, Arboreal Agility) and
their ability to fight in environments where larger species
are restricted.

## Species in Combat

### Skharri — The Scream-and-Leap

When a Skharri character is genuinely insulted, directly
challenged, or witnesses a direct attack on someone under
their protection, the GM may call for a **Wits roll
(difficulty 2)** — meaning the player needs at least two
sixes.

On a failure, the honour-instinct fires. The Skharri acts
immediately and physically, regardless of the tactical
situation, the current initiative order, or the plan the
group was executing. This interrupts the normal round
structure — the Skharri acts now, before their turn.

The scream-and-leap is not a penalty. It is a character beat.
The action taken is the player's choice — the instinct demands
immediate physical response, not a specific one. A Skharri
who leaps at the source of the insult, who overturns the
negotiating table, who draws their weapon — all are valid.
The point is that they acted before they could choose not to.

After the instinct fires, the round continues normally.
The Skharri has used their action.

> **Roleplay note:** The scream-and-leap is one of the most
> interesting mechanics in the system when played honestly.
> A Skharri player who dreads the Wits roll because they know
> their character is at a critical moment — and who then has
> to decide what the instinct produces — is playing one of
> the most demanding and rewarding character arcs in the setting.

### Vaelkhi — Aerial Combat

A Vaelkhi in powered flight (open environment, 0.5 atm minimum)
operates in three dimensions that grounded opponents cannot
easily access. This translates mechanically as:

- Bonus die on Shoot and Move when attacking from an elevated
  position or manoeuvring in three dimensions (from the Aerial
  Hunter biological specialty)
- Grounded opponents attacking an airborne Vaelkhi take a
  penalty die — the target is moving in ways they cannot
  easily predict
- A Vaelkhi in flight cannot be engaged in melee by a grounded
  opponent unless that opponent has a means of reaching them

In enclosed spaces — standard ship corridors, most station
interiors — the Vaelkhi cannot fly and loses all aerial
advantages. Their ground movement penalty die applies.

### Vashori — Emotional Volatility

The Vashori emotional volatility mechanic (Wits roll to avoid
a visible uncontrolled reaction) can fire in combat if a Vashori
character is subjected to a significant betrayal or insult
during the fight — for example, discovering that an apparent
ally opened fire on them.

In combat, a failed volatility roll means the Vashori's action
this round is determined by the emotional reaction rather than
tactical choice. They are not out of control — they are fully
engaged with the source of the emotional trigger, to the
exclusion of the tactical situation.

### Golvhaan — Imposing Presence

A Golvhaan in combat who has not yet been provoked has a
specific quality: they are very difficult to make afraid. The
intimidation and morale mechanics that affect other species
do not apply to a Golvhaan unless the situation is genuinely
extraordinary. 700 kg of scaled centauroid has made its peace
with most threats.

A Golvhaan who is provoked — genuinely provoked, past the
long patience — does not use the scream-and-leap. They simply
act, deliberately and with full physical commitment. The GM
may note when a Golvhaan has been pushed past patience; it
is rare enough to be significant when it happens.

## Mooks and Named NPCs

### Mooks

Mooks are the anonymous opposition — guards, soldiers, hired
muscle, Kreeth warriors. They are defined by a single dice
pool and one behavioural note.

**Mook stats:**

| Quality | Pool | Behavioural note |
|---------|------|-----------------|
| Poor | 3 dice | Breaks on first serious casualty |
| Average | 5 dice | Holds until half their number are down |
| Trained | 7 dice | Holds until outflanked or officer down |
| Elite | 9 dice | Holds until the position is untenable |

Mooks do not have individual Hit Capacities. A mook hit by
an attack that scores one or more sixes is **taken out of the
fight** — incapacitated, fled, or dead depending on the nature
of the attack and the attacker's intent. They are not tracked
individually beyond this.

**Mook morale:** When a mook group loses half their number
in a single round, or when their behavioural trigger is met,
the GM makes a morale check — a flat 3-die roll. On zero
sixes, the group breaks. On one six, they fall back to a
defensible position. On two or more sixes, they hold.

### Named NPCs

Named NPCs have full stat blocks: attributes, skills, gear,
and at least one specialty that distinguishes them. They have
a Hit Capacity like player characters and are tracked individually.

Named NPCs also have a **motivation** — what they are actually
trying to achieve in this combat, which may not be the same as
winning. A Vardhek intelligence operative in a firefight may be
trying to retrieve something specific, not to defeat the players.
A Skharri with an honour-debt may be trying to kill one specific
character, not the group. Run named NPCs toward their motivation,
not toward optimal tactical play.

Named NPCs do not break from morale checks. They have their own
reasons for continuing or withdrawing.

## Environmental Hazards

### Vacuum and Hard Vacuum

Space is the most lethal environment in the setting. The rules
reflect this without requiring complex atmosphere tracking.

**Suited characters** in functioning vacc suits are protected
from vacuum. A suit that has taken damage may be compromised —
the GM introduces a consequence die at the start of each round
for a compromised suit. The D4 result determines what fails.

**Unsuited characters** in hard vacuum take one condition per
round from atmospheric decompression. After four rounds, they
are incapacitated. This is not a death sentence if they are
rescued and treated immediately — but the window is short.

**Hull breaches** in a combat environment introduce a consequence
die for all characters near the breach each round, regardless
of suits. The D4 result determines what the breach does this
round — debris, atmospheric venting, equipment damage, a
character being pulled toward the breach.

### Zero Gravity

Combat in zero gravity removes the intuitive positional logic
that most combatants rely on. Characters without the Zero-G
Movement specialty (all Spacers, and others with specific
training) take a penalty die on Fight and Move rolls in zero-G.

Characters with Zero-G Movement treat freefall as a normal
environment. They do not take the penalty die and may use
their spatial orientation to gain positional advantages that
grounded combatants cannot.

### Extreme Environments

Hostile temperatures, toxic atmospheres, high pressure, and
other environmental hazards impose conditions on unsuited
characters as described in the species entries and the
Conditions chapter. In combat, environmental conditions
accumulate alongside combat conditions — a character fighting
in extreme cold while already injured is stacking conditions
from multiple sources.

The GM should track environmental condition sources separately
from combat condition sources, since they resolve differently.

## The Consequence Die in Combat

The black D4 consequence die is placed on the table when the
combat situation has teeth beyond the exchange of attacks —
when the environment is actively hostile, when something
structural is happening that will affect everyone regardless
of their individual performance.

**When to introduce a consequence die:**

- Combat near or in a hull breach
- Fighting aboard a ship that is simultaneously under attack
- A Skharri whose scream-and-leap has changed the tactical
  situation for everyone
- A collapsing structure, failing life support, or detonation
  in progress
- Any situation where the GM wants to signal: *something will
  go wrong here regardless of how well you fight*

The consequence die is rolled at the **start of each round**
in which the condition applies. Its result determines what
the situation costs before attacks are resolved:

| D4 | Consequence |
|----|-------------|
| 1 | **Minimal cost.** A system degrades, a resource is spent, a position becomes slightly worse. |
| 2 | **Complication.** One character (GM's choice) must spend their action addressing the complication or accept a condition. |
| 3 | **Collateral.** Equipment is damaged, a bystander is affected, or the environment worsens for everyone. |
| 4 | **Full cost.** The thing the situation warned about happens. The breach vents. The structure collapses. The thing goes off. |

The consequence die is removed when the triggering condition
is resolved — the breach is sealed, the character gets the
situation under control, the structural failure is past.

## Ending a Combat

Combat ends when:

- One side is incapacitated, dead, or fled
- One side surrenders or is captured
- The situation changes so fundamentally that continuing the
  fight no longer makes sense

**Mooks** who break from a morale check are out of the fight —
whether they flee, surrender, or simply stop engaging is the
GM's call based on the fiction.

**Named NPCs** end the fight on their own terms when their
motivation is achieved, when the situation becomes untenable,
or when they are incapacitated. A named NPC who retreats is
not defeated — they will be encountered again under different
circumstances.

**Aftermath** — after every combat, the GM should briefly
describe the state of the scene. Who is down? What is damaged?
What has changed? What does the environment look like now?
Combat is not an interlude between scenes — it is a scene,
and its results change the situation that follows.

## Quick Reference — Combat

### Round Structure
1. Opening round: player group nominates one character for
   Wits roll vs GM's Wits roll. Most sixes acts first.
   Surprise: surprising side acts first, surprised side loses
   Round 1 action.
2. Subsequent rounds: alternating sides (players / NPCs).
3. Within player turn: free coordination if communication
   is possible. One action per character.

### Attack Roll
Attribute (white) + Skill (blue) + Gear (green)
0 sixes = miss / 1 = hit, one condition / 2 = clean hit /
3 = strong hit, two conditions or one + tactical effect /
4+ = devastating, two conditions + tactical effect

### Defence
Passive: cover removes attacker dice (1/2/3 for light/solid/hard)
Active: costs your action; opposed roll; armour adds green dice

### Range Bands
Close (melee) / Short (personal weapons) / Long (rifles, open space)
Outside optimal band: penalty die

### Size
Attacking larger opponent (melee): penalty die
Attacking smaller opponent (melee): bonus die
Attacking to subdue smaller opponent: penalty die
Large target (ranged): attacker gains bonus die

### Mooks
Single pool (3/5/7/9 dice by quality)
One hit = out of fight
Morale check when trigger met: 3 dice, 0 sixes = breaks

### Consequence Die (black D4)
Rolled at start of each applicable round
1 = minimal / 2 = complication / 3 = collateral / 4 = full cost

### Vacuum
Suited: protected (compromised suit = consequence die each round)
Unsuited: one condition per round, incapacitated after four
Hull breach: consequence die each round for nearby characters
