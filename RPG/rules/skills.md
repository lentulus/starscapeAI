# Skills

## Overview

Skills represent learned competencies that characters develop through career,
culture, and experience. Every skill roll in the game combines three elements:
**Attribute + Skill + Gear**, with each contributing a number of six-sided dice
to the pool. Any six is a success; additional sixes generate extra effect.

Skills are broad by design. The diversity of a character's background — their
species, career, and personal history — is expressed through **Specialties**
that refine how a skill is applied, not through multiplying the skill list.
A Belter and a planetary Marine may both have Shoot at the same rating, but
only the Belter has the *Zero-G Combat* specialty that removes the penalty for
firing in freefall.

There are fifteen skills, three under each of the five attributes. Every
character has access to every skill; the question is how well-developed it is.

---

## Skill Ratings

| Rating | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| 0 | Untrained. Roll attribute dice only. |
| 1 | Familiar. Basic competence. |
| 2 | Trained. Professional level. |
| 3 | Expert. Sought out for this. |
| 4 | Master. Among the best. |
| 5 | Legendary. Defines the field. |

Starting characters typically have ratings between 1 and 3, with most skills
at 0.

---

## Levels of Success and Failure

Count every six in the pool:

| Sixes | Outcome |
|-------|---------|
| 0 | Failure. Something goes wrong or simply doesn't work. |
| 1 | Bare success. You achieve the goal, but barely. |
| 2 | Clean success. No complications. |
| 3+ | Outstanding success. Extra effect, faster completion, or additional information. |

On a failure, the GM may offer success at a cost — you can achieve the goal,
but something else suffers. This is always the player's choice to accept.

> **Roleplay note:** Levels of success matter most in social and information
> skills. A bare success on Read means you caught something; three sixes means
> you read the room completely. Encourage players to describe *how* they
> succeeded, not just *that* they did.

---

## Pushing a Roll

Once per roll, after seeing the result, a character may **push** — rerolling
all dice that did not show a six or a one. Ones are locked out of the reroll.
Pushing represents digging deep: extra effort, taking risks, ignoring pain.

Pushing always has a narrative consequence. The GM describes what it costs —
a tool strained to its limit, a social situation made more tense, physical
exhaustion. On a pushed failure, something goes meaningfully wrong.

## The Skills

---

### Strength

*Raw physical power, endurance, and the ability to make things move.*

---

#### Fight

Close combat: unarmed strikes, blades, improvised weapons, and the controlled
violence of trained martial arts. Fight covers both offence and the instinctive
physical defence of someone who knows how to handle themselves.

**Typical uses:** Striking an opponent, wrestling or grappling, breaking free
of a hold, intimidating through demonstrated capability.

**Not used for:** Ranged weapons (Shoot), absorbing punishment (Endure), or
heavy physical work (Labor).

> **Roleplay note:** How a character fights says a great deal about them.
> A Skharri throws themselves in — honour demands presence and directness.
> A Shekhari fights efficiently and ends it fast; prolonged brawling is
> expensive. A trained human spacer fights dirty: elbows, knees, whatever ends
> it. Encourage players to describe their style, not just roll dice.
>
> Species note: Size matters here. A Shekhari (10 kg) attacking a Golvhaan
> (700 kg) is not the same contest as two humans. See the **Size** rules in
> the Combat chapter.

**Example specialties:** Unarmed Combat, Blade Fighting, Zero-G Brawling,
Skharri Honour Duelling, Crowd Fighting.

---

#### Endure

The capacity to keep going: sustained physical effort, resisting environmental
stress, absorbing punishment, and recovering from injury. Endure is what
separates someone who can sprint from someone who can march for three days.

**Typical uses:** Resisting fatigue, surviving hostile environments without
equipment, absorbing a hit that would drop a lesser person, recovering from
injury or toxin, operating in high or low gravity without penalty.

**Not used for:** Resisting psychological pressure (that is Wits or Empathy
depending on context), or recovering from psychological trauma.

> **Roleplay note:** Endure rolls are often silent heroism — the moment nobody
> else at the table notices because the character just *keeps going*. They
> matter most in extended sequences: a long EVA, a desert crossing, a firefight
> that goes on too long. High Endure characters are the ones still functional
> when everyone else is flagging.
>
> Species note: Vashori are cold-sensitive ectotherms — Endure rolls in cold
> environments carry an additional penalty die. Vaelkhi have very high
> metabolic demands; Endure rolls increase in difficulty if they have not
> eaten recently.

**Example specialties:** High-G Tolerance, Vacuum Exposure, Toxin Resistance,
Forced March, Pain Resistance.

---

#### Labor

Heavy physical work: lifting, carrying, forcing, constructing, operating
industrial machinery. Labor is the skill of the dockworker, the engineer
pulling cable in a crawlspace, and Ripley in the power loader.

**Typical uses:** Shifting cargo under time pressure, operating heavy
machinery, forcing a jammed airlock, field construction and repair requiring
brute physical effort, extracting something from wreckage.

**Not used for:** Fine mechanical repair (Tech), operating vehicles or ships
under pressure (Operate), or combat (Fight).

> **Roleplay note:** Labor is quietly one of the most important skills in a
> working-spacer setting. The universe is full of things that need moving,
> fixing with force, or building under time pressure. Characters with high
> Labor are invaluable in logistics situations — and those situations arise
> constantly when your strategic simulation is running in the background.
>
> There is also dignity in it. The 70s SF tradition this setting draws from
> respected competent working people. A character who can move cargo in a
> spinning habitat in microgravity while suited up is doing something genuinely
> difficult and skilled.

**Example specialties:** Zero-G Cargo Handling, Industrial Machinery,
Demolition, Salvage Work, Exosuit Operation.

---

### Agility

*Speed, coordination, and controlled movement through space.*

---

#### Shoot

All forms of ranged combat: slugthrowers, energy weapons, shipboard point
defence systems operated manually, thrown weapons. Shoot covers the full arc
from personal sidearm to crew-served weapons operated by a single character.

**Typical uses:** Firing a weapon at a target, suppressing an area, snap
shooting at a moving target, aimed fire for precision effect.

**Not used for:** Ship weapon systems operated through the command interface
(Operate), close combat (Fight).

> **Roleplay note:** Shoot is one place where species and background produce
> strikingly different characters without changing the skill rating. A Vaelkhi
> with Shoot fires differently from a Skharri — one is a patient aerial
> predator who waits for the clean shot, the other is pressing the attack
> before the tactical picture is complete. A Shekhari with high Shoot is
> unsettling to face because something that small should not be that accurate.
>
> The most important roleplay question around Shoot: *does this character
> want to fire?* Not every species or background produces someone comfortable
> with ranged killing.

**Example specialties:** Zero-G Combat, Sniping, Shipboard Weapons, Energy
Weapons, Suppression Fire.

---

#### Stealth

Moving unseen and unheard, concealing objects or people, operating without
leaving traces. Stealth also covers the passive side — blending into a crowd,
appearing to belong somewhere you do not.

**Typical uses:** Moving past a sensor sweep or guard, hiding a person or
object, tailing someone without being noticed, operating quietly in a hostile
environment.

**Not used for:** Electronic sensor spoofing (Tech), reading social situations
(Scan), actively deceiving someone face to face (Manipulate).

> **Roleplay note:** Stealth is highly species-dependent in practice, even at
> the same rating. A Shekhari is physically small and naturally quiet; their
> Stealth rolls have a different texture from a Golvhaan attempting the same
> thing. A 700 kg centauroid in a corridor is not stealthy in the same way as
> a cat-sized felinoid, and the table should feel that difference even if the
> dice are equivalent.
>
> Stealth also has an intelligence dimension — knowing *what* to hide from,
> not just how to hide. A character who understands sensor technology (Scan
> or Tech specialty) should be better at avoiding detection systems, not just
> biological observers.

**Example specialties:** Urban Concealment, Sensor Avoidance, Tailing,
Shipboard Infiltration, Species Camouflage.

---

#### Move

Athletics, agility, and controlled movement: running, climbing, swimming,
zero-G navigation, parkour, and any situation where getting from here to there
quickly and correctly matters. Move is also the skill for avoiding physical
danger that requires bodily response — diving clear, rolling, getting out of
the way.

**Typical uses:** Chasing or fleeing, climbing under pressure, navigating
zero-G environments, crossing difficult terrain, evading an area effect,
boarding actions across hull surfaces.

**Not used for:** Sustained effort over time (Endure), driving vehicles
(Operate), or combat movement specifically (that is handled within Fight and
Shoot actions).

> **Roleplay note:** Move is where the Vaelkhi's flight becomes mechanically
> relevant — in open environments they simply operate in three dimensions that
> other species cannot access without equipment. This is a significant
> advantage in the right context and a genuine limitation in ship corridors.
>
> Zero-G movement is a specialty worth taking seriously in this setting.
> Characters raised on stations or in the Belt move through freefall with the
> same comfort as a groundsider walks. Characters from high-G worlds find
> zero-G disorienting in ways that are both physical and psychological.

**Example specialties:** Zero-G Navigation, Free Running, Climbing, Swimming,
Low-G Acrobatics, Vaelkhi Flight Manoeuvring.

---

### Wits

*In-the-moment mental sharpness: situational awareness, rapid response,
and the ability to process and act under pressure.*

Wits is not raw intelligence — it is applied intelligence under time pressure.
The difference between knowing how to astrogate and being able to do it when
the drive is failing and three crew are shouting at you.

---

#### Navigate

Finding the way: astrogation, planetary navigation, reading charts and sensor
returns to determine position and plot course. Navigate covers the full scale
from threading an asteroid field to calculating a jump between systems.

**Typical uses:** Plotting an FTL course, finding a safe route through a
debris field, determining position from sensor returns, reading a star chart
under unfamiliar conditions, navigating a planetary surface without instruments.

**Not used for:** Actually flying the ship (Operate), interpreting what sensors
are detecting (Scan), or understanding the physics involved (Science).

> **Roleplay note:** Navigation is an ancient and respected skill in any
> spacefaring culture. A navigator who has made difficult jumps, who knows
> the routes that are not on the charts, is someone other spacers pay
> attention to. In the 70s SF tradition this setting draws from, the navigator
> is often the one who actually understands where the ship is in any deeper
> sense — not just geometrically but in terms of politics, hazard, and
> opportunity.
>
> Different species navigate differently. A Nakhavi navigator is processing
> spatial information through distributed cognition that has no human
> equivalent. A Vaelkhi has an instinctive three-dimensional spatial model
> from flight. These are roleplay textures, not mechanical modifiers, unless
> a specialty applies.

**Example specialties:** Astrogation, Deep Space Navigation, Planetary
Surveying, Hazard Route Planning, Dead Reckoning.

---

#### Operate

Controlling vehicles, ships, and complex systems under pressure. Operate is
the skill of the pilot, the helm officer, the shuttle driver threading through
a docking approach in bad weather. It covers hands-on control of any vehicle
or vessel when conditions make that control demanding.

**Typical uses:** Piloting a ship through difficult conditions, docking under
time pressure, combat manoeuvring, operating a ground vehicle in hostile
terrain, managing a damaged ship's systems in an emergency.

**Not used for:** Plotting the course (Navigate), repairing the systems (Tech),
or managing the crew's response (Command).

> **Roleplay note:** Operate is most interesting when things are going wrong.
> A routine transit does not need a roll; a roll happens when the situation
> has teeth. High Operate characters have a quality that other characters
> notice — a particular calm in a crisis, or conversely, a visible hunger for
> the difficult approach, the tight corridor, the manoeuvre that should not
> work.
>
> The relationship between a pilot and their ship is one of the richest
> roleplay seams in spacefaring fiction. Encourage players with high Operate
> to name their preferred vessels, develop opinions about ship handling, and
> have feelings about being asked to fly something they consider inferior.

**Example specialties:** Atmospheric Flight, Deep Space Manoeuvring, Combat
Piloting, Shuttle Operations, Damaged Vessel Handling.

---

#### Scan

Active awareness: reading sensors, perceiving what is happening in an
environment, noticing what does not fit, gathering information through
observation. Scan covers both technological sensor operation and the
biological equivalent — situational awareness, reading body language,
noticing that something is wrong before you can say why.

**Typical uses:** Operating ship sensors, noticing an ambush before it closes,
reading a social situation for threat or opportunity, detecting a concealed
object or person, understanding what sensor returns mean in context.

**Not used for:** Deeper analysis of what was found (Science or Tech),
actively manipulating or deceiving (Manipulate), or formally understanding
alien psychology (Read).

> **Roleplay note:** Scan is the skill that keeps characters alive before
> trouble starts. Characters with high Scan are the ones who say *something
> is wrong* before anyone else sees it. This is enormously valuable and
> should be rewarded with early information — not perfect information, but
> the sense that something needs attention.
>
> In a multi-species setting, Scan also includes the ability to read alien
> body language and environmental cues. A Vashori's emotional flare is
> obvious to anyone watching if they know what to look for. A Skharri
> honour-challenge has physical tells that precede violence. Characters with
> Scan specialties in specific species interactions have a real advantage in
> avoiding situations that escalate without warning.

**Example specialties:** Ship Sensors, Electronic Surveillance, Ambush
Detection, Species Body Language (specific species), Hazard Recognition.

---

### Empathy

*Understanding, influencing, and leading other people — including people
who are not human.*

Empathy in this context is not sentimentality. It is the ability to model
what another being is thinking and feeling, and to act on that model
effectively. A high-Empathy character is not necessarily kind; they are
accurate.

---

#### Manipulate

Influencing others through persuasion, negotiation, deception, or social
pressure. Manipulate is the skill of the trader closing a deal, the spy
extracting information, the diplomat finding the compromise that both sides
can accept. It is active — you are trying to move someone toward a specific
outcome.

**Typical uses:** Negotiating a price or treaty, lying convincingly, persuading
someone to take an action, social engineering past a checkpoint, extracting
information through conversation.

**Not used for:** Reading whether manipulation is working (Scan), understanding
the psychology behind a reaction (Read), or leading people in a structured
context (Command).

> **Roleplay note:** Manipulate should never be a substitute for roleplay —
> it should enhance it. The roll resolves whether the approach worked, not
> what the approach was. Players should describe *what* they are saying and
> *how* before rolling.
>
> Cross-species manipulation is genuinely difficult and interesting. Persuasion
> techniques that work on humans may fail badly on a Skharri (who reads
> indirect approaches as weakness or deception), a Vashori (who may react
> emotionally to the method rather than the content), or a Golvhaan (who is
> simply very hard to rush or pressure). A specialty in a specific species
> reflects hard-won understanding of how they are actually moved.

**Example specialties:** Negotiation, Deception, Alien Diplomacy (specific
species), Intimidation, Social Engineering.

---

#### Read

Understanding what others are actually thinking, feeling, and intending —
beneath what they are saying. Read is the prerequisite for effective
manipulation and the defence against it. In a multi-species setting it is also
the skill of genuine cross-cultural understanding: not just what an alien is
doing, but *why*.

**Typical uses:** Detecting deception, understanding an alien's emotional
state, sensing the real dynamic in a room, knowing whether a negotiation is
genuine, reading what a Skharri's body language means before they act.

**Not used for:** Active persuasion (Manipulate), operating sensors (Scan),
or formal xenopsychology research (Science).

> **Roleplay note:** Read is one of the most important skills in this setting
> and one of the most rewarding to play well. The difference between
> Manipulate and Read is the difference between talking and listening.
> Characters with high Read notice things: the Vashori diplomat is more
> agitated than the situation warrants; the Skharri's ears are back; the
> Shekhari has stopped negotiating and started calculating an exit.
>
> Encourage Read results to be delivered as information, not conclusions.
> "You notice the diplomat's tail is held very still — in Vardhek body
> language that indicates controlled tension" is more interesting than
> "they're lying." The player then decides what to do with that.
>
> Cross-species Read is genuinely hard without a specialty. A player who
> invested in Read (Vashori) has learned something real about how that
> species processes the world. Reward that investment.

**Example specialties:** Detect Deception, Alien Psychology (specific species),
Emotional State Reading, Political Subtext, Interrogation.

---

#### Command

Leading people effectively under pressure: issuing clear orders, maintaining
morale, coordinating a crew or team, and taking responsibility for collective
action in a crisis. Command is not about rank — it is about the ability to
make people function well together when it matters.

**Typical uses:** Coordinating crew in an emergency, maintaining morale under
sustained stress, issuing orders that are followed without hesitation,
organizing a group for a complex task, rallying people who are about to break.

**Not used for:** Persuading individuals one-on-one (Manipulate), understanding
what individuals are thinking (Read), or administering an organisation over
time (Administer).

> **Roleplay note:** Command is most visible in crisis. A character with high
> Command is the one the group turns to when things go wrong — not
> necessarily the most senior, but the one whose voice cuts through. This
> is a quality, not a rank.
>
> Command across species is demanding. Leading a mixed crew means different
> species respond to authority differently. A Kraathi cannot be commanded in
> any conventional sense — their herd structure has no provision for an
> outside authority. A Skharri respects command only from someone who has
> demonstrated personal superiority. A Golvhaan will follow sound leadership
> patiently but will not be rushed. A character who commands a multi-species
> crew well has genuinely earned something.
>
> Note that Command does not create loyalty — it directs it. Building the
> loyalty that makes Command effective is a roleplay task, not a skill roll.

**Example specialties:** Crisis Command, Ship's Crew, Mixed Species Teams,
Morale Under Fire, Small Unit Tactics.

---

### Intellect

*Applied knowledge: what you know, how deeply you understand it, and whether
you can reason through problems that have no precedent.*

Intellect skills are deliberate rather than reactive. They reward preparation,
study, and genuine understanding of underlying principles. An Intellect roll
is rarely urgent — but the results often determine whether the urgent situation
was survivable.

---

#### Tech

Understanding, operating, repairing, and modifying technology. Tech covers
the full range from field-stripping a weapon to diagnosing a failed jump drive
to modifying a piece of equipment for a purpose it was not designed for. Tech
is practical — it is about making things work, not understanding the theory
behind why they work.

**Typical uses:** Repairing damaged equipment, modifying a device for a new
purpose, diagnosing a technical fault, bypassing a security system, maintaining
equipment in the field.

**Not used for:** Understanding the underlying physics (Science), operating
equipment under pressure (Operate), or heavy physical construction (Labor).

> **Roleplay note:** Tech characters in a multi-species setting encounter
> technology designed for bodies and minds unlike their own. A human Tech
> specialist working on Shekhari-manufactured equipment is dealing with
> interfaces designed for a being a fifth their size. A Golvhaan technician
> working with human equipment needs to work around manipulator size mismatch.
> These are interesting problems, not just penalties.
>
> The relationship between a Tech character and their tools is worth
> developing. What do they carry? What do they refuse to be without? What
> piece of equipment have they nursed back to life so many times it has
> a name? These details cost nothing and add enormous texture.

**Example specialties:** Drive Systems, Weapons Maintenance, Electronic
Systems, Alien Technology, Life Support, Jury Rigging.

---

#### Science

Theoretical and applied scientific knowledge: physics, xenobiology, chemistry,
medicine, xenoarchaeology, and the ability to research and reason from
principles rather than from experience. Science is what you reach for when
the situation is genuinely novel and there is no established procedure.

**Typical uses:** Analysing an unknown substance or organism, understanding
the implications of sensor data, conducting field research, applying medical
knowledge beyond basic first aid, understanding the physics of an unusual
environment, reasoning about alien technology from first principles.

**Not used for:** Practical repair work (Tech), basic medical treatment in
the field (Medic — see below), or navigating using known principles (Navigate).

> **Roleplay note:** Science characters are the setting's sense-makers. In
> a universe full of a dozen sophont species and thousands of worlds, there
> is always something genuinely novel happening. The Science roll is often
> about what the character *understands* rather than what they *do* — and
> understanding changes the options available.
>
> Xenobiology is worth calling out specifically in this setting. Understanding
> alien biology — how a Nakhavi thinks, what a Vashori's ectothermic
> metabolism means for their decision-making in the cold, why a Kreeth
> warrior severed from its Queen becomes non-functional — is actionable
> intelligence, not academic interest. Characters who invest in this are
> genuinely better prepared for first contact situations.

**Example specialties:** Xenobiology, Physics, Medicine (advanced), Chemistry,
Xenoarchaeology, Astrogeology, Alien Technology Analysis.

---

#### Administer

Knowledge of how organisations, institutions, laws, and bureaucracies work —
and how to navigate them effectively. Administer covers trade regulations,
legal frameworks, faction protocols, institutional knowledge, and the
unglamorous but critical skill of knowing which form to file and which official
to find. In a multi-species interstellar setting, this includes the wildly
different administrative and legal structures of a dozen civilisations.

**Typical uses:** Navigating customs and port authority, understanding a
contract's implications, knowing which faction's jurisdiction applies,
managing ship finances and cargo manifests, understanding the legal status
of a situation, identifying the right person in an organisation to approach.

**Not used for:** Actively persuading officials (Manipulate), understanding
the political motivations behind decisions (Read), or commanding an
organisation (Command).

> **Roleplay note:** Administer is one of the most underrated skills in
> spacefaring fiction and one of the most realistic. The universe runs on
> paperwork, protocols, and knowing whose stamp you need. A character with
> high Administer is invaluable at any port of call — they know which queues
> to avoid, which regulations have exceptions, and which officials can be
> approached and how.
>
> In the 70s SF tradition this setting draws from, competent people deal with
> institutions as a matter of course. Van Rijn knew trade law as well as he
> knew people. Flandry understood the Imperial bureaucracy intimately and
> used that knowledge constantly. Administer characters carry that tradition.
>
> Cross-species administration is a genuine specialty. The Vardhek Roidhunate
> operates nothing like a human trade federation. Kraathi herd-authority has
> no provision for individual contracts. A character who understands multiple
> systems has a rare and valuable capability.

**Example specialties:** Trade Law, Port Authority Protocols, Faction
Administration (specific faction), Customs and Contraband, Ship Registration
and Licensing, Alien Legal Systems.

---

## A Note on Medic

Basic field medicine — stopping bleeding, treating wounds, administering
standard drugs — is handled as a **specialty of Science** rather than a
separate skill. Most characters can attempt basic first aid using Wits
(improvising) or Intellect (applying knowledge); a character with the Medic
specialty applies their Science skill and gets the benefit of trained
knowledge.

Advanced medicine, surgery, xenomedicine, and anything requiring genuine
medical expertise uses Science directly, with relevant specialties.

This reflects the setting's approach: competent spacers know basic first aid.
Actual medicine is a Science discipline.

---

## Specialties

Specialties are focused applications of a skill that reflect a character's
specific background, training, or experience. A specialty either removes a
penalty that would normally apply, adds a bonus die in specific circumstances,
or allows a skill to be used in a context that would otherwise be unavailable.

Each specialty is tied to a specific skill. Characters gain specialties through
their **Archetype** at character creation and may develop additional specialties
through play.

A character may not have more specialties in a single skill than their rating
in that skill.

> **Design note:** Specialties are the primary tool for differentiating
> characters who have the same skill rating. Two characters with Shoot 3 are
> both good shots; the one with Zero-G Combat and the one with Sniping are
> good in completely different situations. Specialties are also where species
> background and career history become mechanically visible without requiring
> separate species skill lists.

---

## Opposed Rolls

When two characters act against each other directly — one trying to deceive,
the other trying to detect the deception — both roll their relevant skill
pools. The character with more successes wins. On a tie, the acting character
wins (the attempt succeeds).

Common opposed pairs:

| Acting | Resisting |
|--------|-----------|
| Manipulate | Read |
| Stealth | Scan |
| Fight | Fight (defensive) |
| Shoot | Move (evading) |

## Extended Tasks

Some tasks cannot be resolved in a single roll. An extended task has a
**difficulty threshold** — a total number of successes needed across multiple
rolls. Each roll represents a period of work. Failures on extended tasks do
not subtract from accumulated successes but may introduce complications.

Extended tasks are appropriate for: repairs to a heavily damaged ship,
researching an unknown phenomenon, negotiating a complex treaty, constructing
something significant.

> **Roleplay note:** Extended tasks are an opportunity for the table to
> breathe. Each roll is a scene, not just a dice result. What is happening
> during the repair? What complications arise during the negotiation? The
> dice provide the structure; the players and GM provide the texture.
