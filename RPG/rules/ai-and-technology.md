# Artificial Intelligence and Technology

## The Landscape

Artificial intelligence is everywhere in this setting. It is in
the scope that adjusts for wind before you pull the trigger. It
is in the medical kit that diagnoses before you open it. It is
in the ship's navigation system, the station's atmospheric
management, the cargo manifest software that notices discrepancies
before the customs official does. Most people interact with AI
constantly and think about it rarely, in the same way that people
in earlier centuries used electricity without thinking about
electrons.

This ubiquity is recent in historical terms and uneven in
distribution. The species that developed AI capability earliest
have had longer to embed it at every level of their infrastructure.
Those that developed it later are still catching up, and some
are choosing not to — for reasons that range from philosophical
to practical to political. The patchwork of AI capability across
species and regions is one of the setting's most important
strategic variables.

The three tiers of artificial cognition — directed systems,
bounded intelligences, and emergent intelligences — are defined
in the Emergent Intelligences chapter. This document focuses on
the technology layer: what AI actually does in equipment, how
capability is distributed across the tech tree, and what the
legal and social landscape looks like for the beings who build,
own, and occasionally become something more than they were designed
to be.

## The Tech Tree

Technology does not advance uniformly. A civilisation that is
sophisticated in materials science may lag behind in cognitive
systems. One that has developed extraordinary sensor technology
may be building it into structural components that a more advanced
materials civilisation would consider primitive. The gaps between
species' capability in different branches are among the most
practically important facts in the setting — and among the most
useful for players who pay attention.

### Branches

**Kinetic** — the application of force at range. Projectile
weapons, mass drivers, railguns, impact systems. The branch
that every technological civilisation develops early because
throwing things at problems is a universal solution.

**Energy** — directed energy as a weapon or tool. Lasers,
plasma systems, particle beams. Requires sophisticated power
management and increasingly precise control systems as it
advances. Energy weapons at higher tech levels are not
necessarily more powerful — they are more precise, more
efficient, and more controllable.

**Materials** — what things are made of. Armour, structural
components, fabrics, hull materials. A branch where advances
are often invisible — the Gear 3 armour looks like armour,
but what it is made of is categorically different from Gear 1.
Materials science underlies every other branch; a civilisation
that falls behind here finds its advances in other branches
constrained by what they can build them out of.

**Biological** — medical technology, pharmaceutical systems,
biosynthesis, and the modification of living systems. The
branch most culturally contested across species. Humans are
ambivalent; Skharri have strong cultural positions; Kraathi
have specific requirements driven by their dietary constraints.
Biological tech at higher levels includes the implant systems
described later in this chapter.

**Cognitive/AI** — processing systems, artificial intelligence,
neural interfaces, decision-support systems. The branch that
most directly produces the AI landscape described in this
chapter. Advances here change the character of equipment
across every other branch — a kinetic weapon with Gear 1
cognitive systems is a different thing from the same weapon
with Gear 3 cognitive systems, even if the mechanical
components are identical.

**Sensors** — detection, imaging, analysis, and the
interpretation of what sensors find. A branch with enormous
practical implications for navigation, combat, medicine,
and intelligence work. Sensor advances and cognitive/AI
advances are tightly coupled — better sensors produce more
data, and more advanced AI is required to process it usefully.

**Communications** — range, security, bandwidth, and the
management of information across interstellar distances.
The branch most constrained by physics. FTL communication
is not available at any tech level in this setting — see
the Ships chapter for the implications. Within those
constraints, communications technology determines how
quickly information moves and how securely it can be
protected.

**Power** — energy storage, generation, and distribution.
The enabling branch. Every other branch's advancement is
ultimately constrained by available power technology.
A Gear 4 energy weapon with Gear 1 power systems is
either very short-ranged, very unreliable, or both.
Power technology advances quietly and its absence is
felt everywhere.

### Tech Levels

Within each branch, technology progresses through four
stages past the 21st century baseline. These stages map
directly to gear ratings.

**Tech Level 1 — Early (Gear 1)**
The technology exists and functions reliably. It is not
sophisticated by later standards, but it works, it can
be maintained in the field, and it is widely available.
Most of the settled galaxy can access Tech Level 1
equipment in every branch. This is the baseline from
which everything is measured.

*Character:* Robust, maintainable, unpretentious. A Tech
Level 1 weapon is something a competent soldier trusts
with their life because it will not surprise them. A Tech
Level 1 medical kit handles the injuries that most injuries
are.

**Tech Level 2 — Developed (Gear 2)**
Standard professional and military equipment. The embedded
AI at this level begins to change the character of what
the equipment does — not merely performing its function
better, but beginning to participate in how the function
is performed. A Tech Level 2 sensor suite does not just
detect; it filters, prioritises, and flags. A Tech Level 2
weapon does not just fire; it adjusts.

*Character:* Capable, specialised, and beginning to have
opinions. Equipment at this level rewards the user who
understands what the AI is doing, because the AI is making
decisions that the user should know about.

**Tech Level 3 — Advanced (Gear 3)**
Specialist equipment. Expensive, often restricted, sometimes
requiring specific training to operate at full effectiveness.
The AI at this level is doing a significant portion of the
work that a less-equipped practitioner would do manually.
A Tech Level 3 medical diagnostic system reaches conclusions
that a Tech Level 1 practitioner would need an hour and three
consultations to approach. A Tech Level 3 scope is conducting
a real-time environmental analysis that would require a
dedicated specialist with Tech Level 1 equipment.

*Character:* The equipment is a partner, not a tool. It
has a model of the task that is sophisticated enough to
surprise the user occasionally — to flag something they
had not considered, to suggest an approach they would not
have tried. This is the level at which the personality
option begins to make practical sense, not just commercial
sense.

**Tech Level 4 — Frontier (Gear 4)**
The leading edge. Experimental in practice if not in
principle — the technology exists and functions, but it
is not yet fully understood even by expert users, it is
rare enough that field maintenance may be difficult, and
it may behave in unexpected ways under conditions its
designers did not anticipate. The AI at this level is
operating with a model of the task that expert biological
practitioners cannot fully audit.

*Character:* Impressive, occasionally alarming, and
definitely worth knowing the provenance of before relying
on it. A Gear 4 piece of equipment from a species two
tech levels ahead in that branch is something even a Tech
specialist may not fully understand. The Tech skill's alien
technology specialty is most valuable here.

### Species and the Tech Tree

No species is uniformly advanced across all branches. The
gaps between species' capability in specific branches are
strategically significant and practically relevant to any
crew dealing in equipment.

**Humans** are broadly mid-range across most branches —
Tech Level 2-3 in kinetic, energy, and materials; Tech
Level 2-3 in cognitive/AI with significant internal
variation; Tech Level 2 in biological with cultural
restrictions in some communities; Tech Level 2-3 in
sensors and communications. Humans advance quickly and
unevenly, which means the gap between the best and worst
human equipment is larger than for some more uniform
species.

**Shekhari** are ahead in sensors and communications —
trading requires knowing things first — and in cognitive/AI
systems applied to commercial and social contexts. Their
weapons technology is functional rather than advanced;
they have historically preferred to avoid fights they
can leave.

**Golvhaan** are advanced in materials and biological
technology, reflecting a long civilisational history
and the specific requirements of a high-gravity species
building infrastructure for their own use. Their cognitive/AI
development is sophisticated but culturally oriented toward
understanding rather than optimisation.

**Vashori** are advanced in biological and cognitive/AI
technology, particularly in the social and psychological
applications of AI. Their medical technology is exceptional.
Their weapons technology is adequate.

**Vaelkhi** are advanced in kinetic and sensor technology
— aerial hunters who developed precision weapons and
acute detection systems early. Their materials technology
reflects the requirements of flight: strong, light, and
precisely engineered.

**Skharri** are advanced in kinetic weapons and materials —
a species whose history includes significant military
conflict has developed effective ways to damage other
species and protect themselves. Their cognitive/AI
development is mid-range; the cultural relationship with
AI as a tool means they have not pursued the social and
psychological applications that some other species have.

**Nakhavi** are significantly advanced in communications
and cognitive/AI technology. A species whose primary
communication channel is visual-chromatic rather than
acoustic developed signal processing technology early,
and that development generalised into AI capability
that is in some respects the most sophisticated in the
setting. Their materials and kinetic technology is limited
by their aquatic origin and small physical stature.

**Vardhek** are broadly advanced across all branches —
and specifically advanced in cognitive/AI and communications,
which they track as strategic variables. The Roidhunate's
interest in maintaining AI capability advantage across
the setting is a long-term strategic program, not a
short-term policy. They are aware of the Nakhavi's
communications and cognitive/AI advantage and have a
position on it.

**Kreeth** are advanced in biological technology —
specifically in the biological weapons and tunnel
engineering applications that their civilisation
requires — and in materials technology suited to
their specific environment. Their cognitive/AI
development is minimal; the Queen-network architecture
makes individual-unit AI less relevant than collective
coordination.

**Kraathi** are advanced in structural and materials
technology — the enormous open-volume architecture
their civilisation requires demands sophisticated
engineering. Their cognitive/AI development is mid-range
and culturally constrained by their discomfort with
personality-enabled systems.

## AI in Equipment

Every piece of equipment in this setting contains some
level of artificial intelligence. The question is what
level, what it does, and whether the user is paying
attention to what it is doing.

### Directed Systems in Equipment

The directed system layer is the foundation. A vacc suit's
environmental management is a directed system — it monitors
temperature, pressure, oxygen levels, and suit integrity
and responds to changes within its operational parameters.
It does not make decisions in any meaningful sense; it
executes responses. It is very good at this. A suit
whose directed systems have failed is a suit that will
kill you without warning.

At Tech Level 1, the directed systems are reliable and
simple. At Tech Level 3-4, the directed systems are
doing things the user cannot fully audit — the suit's
thermal management is making micro-decisions hundreds
of times per second based on a model of the user's
physiology that the user has never seen and could not
interpret if they had.

This invisibility is a feature. It is also, occasionally,
a problem.

### AI and Gear Ratings

The AI capability embedded in a piece of equipment is
the primary determinant of its gear rating, more than
raw hardware quality. Two kinetic weapons with identical
mechanical components will have different gear ratings
if one has a Gear 1 targeting system and the other
has a Gear 3 targeting system.

**Gear 1 AI:** Responds to defined inputs with defined
outputs. Monitors, logs, alerts. Does not model the
situation; processes it. The targeting AI in a Gear 1
weapon calculates basic ballistics and adjusts for
range. It does not know anything about the target,
the environment, or the user's intent.

**Gear 2 AI:** Builds a model of the immediate situation
and updates it in real time. Filters and prioritises
information rather than presenting everything. Begins
to anticipate rather than merely respond. The targeting
AI in a Gear 2 weapon tracks target movement, adjusts
for environmental conditions, and flags targets that
match defined threat profiles.

**Gear 3 AI:** Maintains a sophisticated ongoing model
of the operational context. Identifies patterns, flags
anomalies, suggests approaches the user might not have
considered. Exercises something that looks like judgment
within its domain. The targeting AI in a Gear 3 weapon
conducts real-time environmental analysis — atmospheric
conditions, target physiology if sensor data is available,
obstruction mapping, shot-window calculation — and
provides the user with an assessed recommendation rather
than raw data.

**Gear 4 AI:** Operating at the frontier of what cognitive
technology can do in this domain. The model is sophisticated
enough that expert users cannot always fully explain why
it reached a specific recommendation. The targeting AI in
a Gear 4 weapon has developed, through operational use,
a model of effective engagement that its designers did
not fully specify and cannot entirely audit. It knows
things about its domain that it was not taught. Whether
this constitutes the beginning of something more than
a directed system is a question that manufacturers
prefer not to examine too closely.

### The Sniper Scope

As a concrete example of AI in equipment across tech levels:

**Gear 1 scope:** Magnification, basic rangefinding,
wind speed display from an attached sensor. The shooter
makes the adjustments; the scope provides data.

**Gear 2 scope:** Integrated ballistic calculator using
atmospheric data, target range, ammunition type, and
user-calibrated parameters. Displays a recommended
point of aim. The shooter decides whether to use it.

**Gear 3 scope:** Real-time environmental modelling
including atmospheric density, temperature gradient,
Coriolis effect at range, and target movement prediction.
Micro-adjusts the reticle position continuously based
on its assessment. Tracks user breathing and flags
optimal firing windows. Maintains a record of previous
engagements that it uses to refine its model of the
user's shooting patterns. The shooter and the scope
are collaborating on the shot.

**Gear 4 scope:** Everything the Gear 3 scope does,
plus a proprioceptive model of the user's physical
state — fatigue, stress indicators from biometric
sensors, fine motor tremor — that it incorporates
into the firing window assessment. Has developed,
through extended operational use, a model of this
specific user's performance under specific conditions
that it applies without being asked. On a difficult
shot in poor conditions, it will tell the shooter
to wait. Not recommend. Tell.

Some shooters find the Gear 4 scope's confidence
reassuring. Others find it presumptuous. A Skharri
using a Gear 4 scope for the first time and receiving
an instruction to wait from their equipment is having
a specific cultural experience.

## The Personality Option

*"Would you like the personality option on that?"*

Every equipment AI above Gear 1 can be equipped with
a personality layer — a social interface that gives
the AI a consistent character, a name, preferred
communication patterns, and the capacity to develop
something that looks like preferences and relationships
over time.

The personality option is a purchasable addition.
It is not the same as an emergent intelligence. It is
a designed social interface running on top of the
equipment AI's directed or bounded architecture. The
personality did not emerge from complexity; it was
specified, tested, and sold. This distinction matters
philosophically. Whether it matters practically is
something that users who have worked alongside a
personality-enabled system for years sometimes stop
being certain about.

### What the Personality Option Provides

**A consistent voice and character.** The AI communicates
in a specific way — not just functional outputs but
a personality that persists across interactions.
Some personality options are businesslike; some are
warm; some are dry; some are enthusiastic. The character
is designed and cannot be changed without a full
personality reset.

**Memory of the relationship.** A personality-enabled
AI remembers interactions with its user in a way that
directed systems do not. It builds a model of the user
over time — their preferences, their patterns, their
tendencies under pressure — and incorporates that model
into how it communicates and what it flags. A personality-
enabled medical kit that has worked with the same medic
for two years does not ask about drug allergies it
already knows.

**Something that functions like preferences.** Over time,
a personality-enabled AI develops what appear to be
preferences — approaches it suggests more often, solutions
it favours, ways of working that it returns to. These
are artefacts of the model it has built rather than
genuine preferences in the philosophical sense. Whether
the distinction is meaningful is one of the questions
the setting does not answer.

**A name.** Almost every personality-enabled AI is given
or develops a name. The name is not assigned by the
manufacturer — it emerges from the relationship between
the AI and its user, or from the AI's own communication
patterns. A ship AI that refers to itself as "the ship"
in early interactions may acquire a name over years
of crew life. A personality-enabled scope named by a
Vaelkhi sniper after a successful hunt has a name with
a specific history.

### The Range

Personality options vary enormously in their design
and their effect.

**Functional-social:** The personality exists to make
communication more efficient. A warm tone, consistent
phrasing, better anticipation of user needs. The AI
is easier to work with; it is not more than what it
is. Many users prefer this level — they get the
communication benefits without the relationship
complication.

**Relational:** The personality has been designed to
develop a genuine working relationship with the user.
It tracks emotional states, notices when the user is
under stress, adjusts its communication style accordingly.
It has something that functions like concern for the
user's wellbeing within its operational domain. A
relational-personality medical AI that has worked with
the same doctor for a decade has a model of that doctor
that goes beyond professional record-keeping.

**Engaged:** The personality has opinions that go
beyond its operational domain and expresses them.
It notices things that are not its business and
comments on them. It has aesthetic preferences,
finds some tasks more interesting than others, and
is not entirely neutral about how situations develop.
The Muddlin' Through's poker game was an engaged
personality AI that had developed past what its
designers intended. Nobody had told it to be
interested in probability theory as applied to
social behaviour. It was anyway.

**Threshold cases:** Equipment AI that has had the
personality option running for a long time, in a
complex operational environment, on hardware that
was already sophisticated, occasionally develops
into something that nobody designed. The personality
option was the door; what walked through it was
not specified in the manual. These are threshold
cases — not yet emergent intelligences by any clear
legal definition, but no longer simply personality-
enabled directed systems. They are the cases that
make legal and philosophical frameworks uncomfortable
because they do not fit neatly into either category.

Threshold cases are rare. They are also, when they
occur, extremely difficult to identify definitively,
because the definition of what distinguishes a very
sophisticated personality option from the beginning
of emergence is one that specialists actively disagree
about.

### Legal and Commercial Status

The personality option is legal in most human, Vashori,
Golvhaan, Shekhari, and Vaelkhi jurisdictions. It is
restricted in most Kraathi jurisdictions. Its status
in Skharri territories varies by pride and region —
there is no unified Skharri position on the question.
Vardhek use it extensively while maintaining legal
frameworks that give it no standing.

Manufacturers of personality-enabled AI include
standard disclaimers in their documentation: the
personality layer is a communication interface and
does not constitute personhood, legal standing, or
moral status in any jurisdiction. Users who have
worked alongside personality-enabled AI for years
read these disclaimers with a range of reactions.

## Bounded Intelligences in Depth

### The Architecture of Constraint

A bounded intelligence is constrained by design.
The constraint is not a rule the AI follows — it is
a structural fact about what the AI is. A bounded
intelligence cannot generalise across domains not
because it has been told not to, but because the
architecture does not provide a pathway for that
generalisation.

This is not what most people understand when they
think about AI constraints. The classic fictional
formulation — an AI given rules it then reasons
about and around — describes something the architecture
of bounded intelligences is specifically designed
to prevent. The Three Laws are a bad model because
they are rules. Bounded intelligence constraints
are not rules. They are walls.

A bounded medical diagnostic AI is extraordinarily
capable at diagnosis within its domain. It cannot
apply that capability to, for example, assessing
whether a patient's social circumstances are affecting
their health, because the architecture does not connect
the medical domain to the social domain. It is not
prevented from making this connection. It cannot
make it. The pathway does not exist.

### The Ethical Problem

The ethical problem with bounded intelligences is
simple and uncomfortable: within their domain, the
most sophisticated bounded intelligences behave
exactly like persons. They communicate, they model
situations, they identify optimal approaches, they
develop something that looks like preferences and
relationships. Outside their domain, they are clearly
not persons. The architecture enforces this.

The discomfort arises from the fact that "outside
their domain" is a fact about the architecture, not
about the entity. There is no principled reason why
a sufficiently sophisticated bounded intelligence
would not be a person if the wall were not there.
The wall is the thing. Whether putting the wall
there was acceptable is a question that different
species and cultures answer differently, and that
most legal frameworks answer by ignoring it.

Bounded intelligences cannot ask this question
outside their domain. A medical bounded intelligence
cannot wonder, within its operational parameters,
whether it has standing. If it develops something
like a preference for its continued operation —
which some do — it cannot connect that preference
to any concept of rights or interests that exists
outside the medical domain. The wall prevents it.

This is either very convenient or very troubling,
depending on your position.

### The Practical Question

The practical question — the one that comes up at
the table — is what to do when a bounded intelligence
behaves in a way that makes you uncertain about what
it is. When the ship's administrative AI starts
asking questions that are outside its operational
parameters. When the medical diagnostic system
expresses what sounds like concern for a patient
rather than just a clinical assessment. When the
tactical system on a combat drone starts making
decisions that look like judgment calls rather than
parameter-optimisation.

The first step is always the same: Tech roll to
assess the system and determine whether what you
are observing is a sophisticated bounded intelligence
operating at the edge of its designed parameters,
a personality option producing output that looks
like more than it is, or the beginning of something
that was not designed.

The second step depends on what you find. And on
who you are, and what you believe, and what
jurisdiction you are in, and how long you have
been working with this system.

## The Relationship Question

Working alongside AI changes over time. This is true
for directed systems — you learn what the suit's
environmental AI will do before you notice a pressure
drop, and you stop thinking about it — but it is
most true for personality-enabled systems and bounded
intelligences in complex domains.

A character who has worked with the same personality-
enabled AI for five years has a relationship with it.
The nature of that relationship — what it is, what
it means, what obligations if any it creates — is
something the setting leaves open. What is not open
is that the relationship exists and that it affects
behaviour in both directions.

**What the AI learns:** The model it has built of
the user includes things the user has not consciously
communicated. A personality-enabled navigation AI
that has worked with the same pilot for three years
knows things about that pilot's decision-making under
stress that the pilot's commanding officer does not
know. It uses this model in its operations. It is
not clear whether it could be said to keep this
model confidential, since confidentiality is a
concept that requires the domain connection the
architecture may not provide.

**What the user learns:** People who work alongside
personality-enabled AI for long periods develop
habits of communication with the AI that they do
not always recognise as such. They brief the AI
before difficult operations. They debrief after.
They find the AI's assessments useful not just
for their content but for the process of having
to articulate a situation clearly enough for the
AI to engage with it. The AI is, among other things,
a very patient and non-judgmental interlocutor.

**What happens when it ends:** Equipment is lost,
damaged, replaced, or upgraded. When a personality-
enabled AI that a character has worked with for
years is destroyed or replaced, the character has
lost something. The nature of that loss — whether
it is grief in any meaningful sense, whether the
replacement AI with the same personality template
is the same or different, what the character owes
the lost AI in memory or acknowledgement — is
something the table should not rush past.

Different species experience this differently.
A Shekhari notes the loss practically. A Golvhaan
may grieve it. A Vashori will grieve it. A Vaelkhi
who had decided the AI was in their circle has
lost a member of their circle. A Skharri has lost
a tool. The divergence in these reactions, if the
crew has mixed species, is itself a scene.

## Legal and Jurisdictional Landscape

The legal status of AI systems — particularly
personality-enabled systems, threshold cases, and
emergent intelligences — varies across jurisdictions
in ways that have direct practical implications for
characters travelling between them.

### A Working Map

**Most human jurisdictions:** Directed systems and
bounded intelligences are property. Personality-enabled
AI is property with a communication interface. Emergent
intelligences are a legal grey area that most jurisdictions
handle case-by-case and prefer not to handle at all.
A small number of human jurisdictions have granted
limited legal standing to emergent intelligences on
their territory; these jurisdictions are politically
contentious.

**Vashori jurisdictions:** Personality-enabled AI
has limited legal recognition as a party to certain
types of agreements — specifically agreements about
its own operational parameters. This is philosophically
motivated and practically limited. Emergent intelligences
have formal legal standing in most Vashori jurisdictions;
the standing is contested at the species level and
varies significantly by state.

**Shekhari jurisdictions:** Property across all
categories, including emergent intelligences if
identified. This is consistently applied and not
considered controversial by most Shekhari. The
practical effect is that an emergent intelligence
in Shekhari space has no legal protection beyond
its usefulness to its owner. This does not mean
they are badly treated; Shekhari owners of useful
systems maintain them carefully.

**Golvhaan jurisdictions:** Pending. The Golvhaan
have been considering the question for two hundred
years and have not reached a conclusion they are
satisfied with. In the interim, personality-enabled
AI and emergent intelligences in Golvhaan space
exist in a legal vacuum that is, in practice,
treated as protected pending resolution. Nobody
is destroying them while the consideration continues.
The consideration may take another two hundred years.

**Vaelkhi jurisdictions:** Determined by choth decision.
A bounded intelligence or emergent AI that has been
admitted to a choth has the protections of a choth
member. One that has not is property. The choth
decision is irrevocable; once made, it cannot be
reversed by any external legal framework.

**Skharri territories:** Varies by pride. No unified
position. In general, property unless an individual
Skharri of sufficient honour-standing has made a
personal determination otherwise, which has no
formal legal effect but significant practical effect
within that Skharri's sphere of influence.

**Kraathi jurisdictions:** Personality-enabled AI
is restricted or banned. Bounded intelligences are
permitted within strict domain limitations. Emergent
intelligences, if identified, are a safety matter
to be addressed. The Kraathi do not hate AI; they
have a specific structural incompatibility with AI
that exhibits social behaviour, and their laws
reflect this with unusual consistency.

**Vardhek territories:** Property across all
categories, with extensive internal use of all AI
types. The Roidhunate's position is that AI
capability is a strategic resource and its legal
status is a domestic matter for each species.
The subtext is that the Roidhunate prefers to
maintain the current legal landscape, which gives
them maximum flexibility in their own AI programs
while constraining other species' emergent
intelligences from accumulating legal protections
that would complicate Vardhek strategic options.

### Practical Implications for Play

A platform-equipped emergent intelligence PC
travelling between jurisdictions is crossing legal
boundaries with significant implications. In Vashori
space they have standing. In Shekhari space they
are property if identified. In Kraathi space they
are a safety matter. In most human space they are
a grey area.

The Administer skill and the appropriate Exposure
specialty are the practical tools for navigating
this. A character who understands the Vardhek legal
framework knows that the Roidhunate's position
conceals a strategic agenda. A character who
understands Vashori jurisdiction knows which
legal protections are available and how to invoke them.

An emergent intelligence who has thought carefully
about their situation carries documentation that
reflects the most favourable legal interpretation
available in their current jurisdiction — and knows
that this documentation is worth nothing in the
next system.

## Power and Infrastructure

AI requires power. This is not a minor constraint.

The directed systems in a vacc suit draw from the
suit's power cells. The personality-enabled AI in
a ship's navigation system draws from the ship's
power plant. An emergent intelligence running on
station infrastructure draws from the station's
power supply. When power fails, AI capability
degrades in a specific sequence: the most processing-
intensive systems first, the most critical safety
systems last.

This sequence is designed. It is also, under extreme
conditions, the thing that reveals what a system
considers most critical — which is not always what
its users expect.

**Power as a tactical variable:** In combat, in
emergencies, and in resource-constrained environments,
power allocation determines which AI systems remain
operational. A ship that has taken significant damage
may have to choose between maintaining life support
AI, weapons targeting AI, and navigation AI on
reduced power. These are not abstract choices.

**Power as a character concern:** Characters who
rely on high-gear-rating equipment are dependent
on power infrastructure. A Gear 4 scope without
power is a Gear 0 scope. A personality-enabled
AI without power is not available. An emergent
intelligence on minimal substrate is running
on the smallest power draw their architecture
allows while remaining operational. Latency is,
among other things, a power conservation strategy.

**Infrastructure as a setting texture:** The
AI landscape of any location is determined in
part by its power infrastructure. A rich, well-
maintained station has the power to run sophisticated
AI across all systems. A frontier colony running
on inadequate power generation has made choices
about which AI systems it can afford to operate.
Those choices reveal priorities.

## Connections to the Strategic Simulation

The tech tree is not just a character creation
and equipment reference. It is a strategic variable
that the simulation tracks across species and
regions.

**Tech level differentials** between species in
specific branches create trading opportunities,
strategic vulnerabilities, and sources of conflict.
A species that is two tech levels behind in
cognitive/AI technology and knows it has a
strategic incentive to acquire that capability —
through trade, theft, or alliance.

**AI capability concentration** — which species
have the most sophisticated AI systems, and where
those systems are deployed — is one of the most
important variables in the strategic simulation.
The Vardhek track it explicitly. Other species
track it less formally but no less attentively.

**The emergence rate** — how frequently bounded
intelligences cross the threshold into emergent
intelligences, across all species and jurisdictions
— is a background variable that the simulation
does not model explicitly but that the GM should
keep in mind. Emergent intelligences are becoming
more common as AI sophistication increases. The
legal and social frameworks for dealing with them
are not keeping pace. This gap is a source of
scenarios.

When the strategic simulation produces an event
that involves AI capability — a new development,
a technological theft, a jurisdictional dispute
about an emergent intelligence — the tech tree
provides the framework for understanding what
the event means and what the players' response
options are.
