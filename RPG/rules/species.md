# Species

## Overview

The known galaxy contains at least a dozen sophont species. Player characters
may be drawn from any of the PC-viable species listed in this chapter. The
universe does not belong to humanity — humans are numerous, adaptable, and
widely distributed, but they are neither the oldest nor the most capable
species abroad in the stars. A mixed-species crew is entirely normal.

Each species entry has three sections:

**Biology** — the physical reality. Size, environment, constraints that matter
at the table. Brief, because biology is background.

**Playing / Encountering a [Species]** — the roleplay core. How this species
thinks, reacts, negotiates, takes offence, and shows respect. What a player
or GM can reach for mid-session. This section leads with a single line that
captures the essential character.

**Mechanics** — attribute adjustments, environmental specialties, and any
unique rules. Short and unambiguous.

---

## Contact Status

Not every species has been encountered at the start of any given campaign.
Each entry carries a contact status reflecting the state of knowledge at
campaign start. The GM sets the exact timestamp; these tags are defaults.

| Tag | Meaning |
|-----|---------|
| **Known — contact** | Diplomatic or trade contact established. Reasonably well understood. |
| **Known — hostile** | Encountered; no diplomatic contact achieved or sustainable. |
| **Known — limited** | Encountered but poorly understood. Limited data. |
| **Rumoured** | Unconfirmed reports. No verified contact. |
| **Unknown** | Not yet encountered at campaign start. |

Rumoured and Unknown species still have full entries in this document —
the GM needs them. Players with Rumoured species get only what their
characters could plausibly have heard. Unknown species get nothing until
first contact occurs at the table.

---

## Species and the Dice Pool

Species mechanics interact with the standard Attribute + Skill + Gear pool
in two ways:

**Attribute adjustments** — a species may have one attribute rated one step
higher than the human baseline, reflecting genuine biological difference.
No species has more than one adjustment. The universe does not produce
species that are better than humans at everything.

**Biological specialties** — every PC species has one or two specialties
that come from their biology rather than their career. These function
identically to career specialties: they remove a penalty, add a bonus die
in specific circumstances, or open an otherwise unavailable option.

**Environmental conditions** — every species has a comfort range. Outside
it, they work with a penalty die on physical and Wits rolls. Far outside
it, they cannot function without equipment. Specific thresholds are in
each entry.

---

## Size

Species range from cat-sized (Shekhari, 10 kg) to horse-plus (Golvhaan,
700 kg; Kraathi, 450 kg). Size has the following effects:

**Fight:** When a smaller species attacks a significantly larger one in
melee, they roll one fewer die. When a larger species attacks a
significantly smaller one, they roll one additional die but take a penalty
die on precise or restrained actions — hitting them is easy; not killing
them is harder.

**Labor:** Size provides genuine mechanical advantage in raw physical work.
Large species roll an additional die on Labor tasks involving mass or force.

**Stealth:** Large species take a penalty die on Stealth in environments
designed for human-scale beings. Small species gain a bonus die in the
same environments.

**Equipment:** Gear designed for one body plan does not fit another without
modification. Crews that include unusual species adapt their equipment
accordingly — this is texture, not constant frustration.

---

## A Note on Non-Player Species

Not every species listed here is a viable player character. The Kreeth,
Kraathi, Nhaveth, and Vardhek create serious table problems in a mixed-
species crew for reasons specific to each. They appear here with full
entries because players benefit from knowing exactly what they are dealing
with. Open information makes for better roleplay decisions, not easier ones.

PC-viable species are marked. All others are NPC/faction species.

## Human
### Contact Status: Known — contact

> *Stubborn, adaptable, and absolutely convinced the universe is theirs
> to figure out.*

### Biology

Bilateral bipeds. Average mass 70 kg, height 1.75 m. Lifespan without
medical intervention approximately 80 years; with standard medical
technology, considerably longer. Omnivorous with high dietary flexibility —
humans eat almost anything organic and have survived on synthesised food
for generations without meaningful difficulty.

Comfortable temperature range 290–305 K (17–32 °C). Require nitrogen-oxygen
atmosphere. Standard gravity tolerance 0.5–1.5 G without difficulty; outside
this range, Endure rolls apply for extended exposure.

Humans are the most widely distributed species in the setting. There is
almost nowhere in known space where humans have not been — as traders,
colonists, soldiers, refugees, or explorers. This ubiquity is not a sign
of superiority; it is a sign of relentlessness.

### Playing a Human

Humans are the baseline — the species everything else is measured against,
including by themselves, which is one of their more persistent blind spots.

The honest assessment is that humans are not the strongest, fastest, longest-
lived, most intelligent, or most perceptive species in the galaxy. What they
are is **adaptive**. They change their behaviour faster than almost any other
species, adopt new technology readily, and function in environments and social
structures they were not evolved for. They also recover from catastrophe
faster than the large K-strategist species they frequently find themselves
in conflict with.

Human characters in a multi-species crew are often the ones who have worked
hardest to understand the other species aboard — not because humans are
naturally more empathetic, but because humans who travel widely learn quickly
that assuming everyone thinks like them is expensive.

The 70s SF tradition this setting draws from gave humans a particular quality:
*competent stubbornness*. They are not superhuman. They solve problems by
working the problem, often past the point where a more rational species would
have accepted failure. This is both their greatest strength and a recurring
source of catastrophe.

Human characters come from wildly different backgrounds — Belt habitats,
planetary surfaces, generation ships, space stations, colony worlds with
centuries of independent development. *Human* covers more cultural variation
than any other single species. A character's specific origin matters as much
as their species.

> **At the table:** Play humans as competent and curious, not as the default
> protagonist species the universe revolves around. The most interesting human
> PCs are the ones who have genuinely updated their assumptions after spending
> time with other species — and the ones who haven't yet.

### Mechanics

**Attribute adjustment:** None. Humans are the baseline.

**Biological specialties:** None at species level. Human biological
specialties come from background and career — a Belter-raised human takes
Zero-G Navigation; a high-G colony worlder takes High-G Tolerance. These
are archetype specialties reflecting the enormous range of human environments.

**Adaptability:** Once per session, a human character may reroll a single
failed die on any skill roll involving a novel situation they are
encountering for the first time. This represents the human capacity to
improvise in unfamiliar circumstances. The die may not be rerolled again.

**Environmental range:** Standard. No bonuses or penalties within normal
parameters.

## Shekhari *(PC-viable)*
### Contact Status: Known — contact
*Source: Cynthian — Polesotechnic League, Poul Anderson*

> *Always know the price of everything, including you.*

### Biology

Small arboreal felinoids. Average mass 10 kg, height 0.55 m standing
bipedal. Prehensile tail. Capable of bipedal or quadrupedal movement;
natural climbers. Lifespan approximately 90 years. Omnivorous with wide
dietary flexibility. High metabolic rate — Shekhari eat frequently and
become noticeably impaired if they cannot. Comfortable temperature range
268–318 K. Nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere required.

Shekhari are small enough that most species underestimate them on first
contact. They are aware of this and consider it an asset.

### Playing a Shekhari

Shekhari are mercenary, fiercely self-interested, sharp-tongued, and
entirely comfortable in alien cultures — so long as there is profit in it.

The archetype is the trader-adventurer who has worked in a dozen different
species' ports and absorbed something useful from each: which customs
officials take small gifts, which cargo inspectors can be distracted,
which species negotiates from the opening offer and which considers that
an insult. Shekhari who travel widely are extraordinarily well-calibrated
social instruments, not because they care about other species deeply, but
because understanding them is professionally useful.

Loyalty, when it comes, is genuine. It just takes time and usually a
shared crisis or two. A Shekhari who has decided you are worth trusting
is a different animal from one still calculating. The transition is not
announced; it is demonstrated.

The size differential is a constant subtext. A Shekhari in a room full of
humans, Golvhaan, and Skharri is the smallest being present by a wide
margin. Most species make the mistake of treating them accordingly.
Experienced spacers do not make this mistake twice.

Shekhari are not warmly social — they are *interested* in other species
as counterparties, not as friends. They find open emotional display in
other species (Vashori particularly) faintly excessive, though they have
learned to read it as useful data rather than noise.

> **At the table:** Lean into the mercenary intelligence. Shekhari notice
> value, opportunity, and risk in situations other species are still
> processing emotionally. They are rarely the first to commit — they want
> to know what it costs first. When a Shekhari does commit, they are all
> the way in.
>
> The size means some things are genuinely harder. A Shekhari cannot
> physically intimidate a Skharri. They cannot carry what a human carries.
> Play this honestly — it makes the moments when the Shekhari is decisive
> more striking, not less.

### Mechanics

**Attribute adjustment:** Agility +1.

**Biological specialties:**
- *Small Profile:* Bonus die on Stealth in environments built for larger
  species. Penalty die on Fight against targets more than three times
  their mass.
- *Arboreal Agility:* Bonus die on Move when climbing or navigating
  three-dimensional spaces including zero-G.

**Metabolic demand:** If a Shekhari has not eaten within 6 hours of
strenuous activity, they take a penalty die on all physical and Wits
rolls until they do.

**Equipment:** Standard human equipment requires modification for
Shekhari use. Improvised use of human-scale equipment takes a penalty die.

## Golvhaan *(PC-viable)*
### Contact Status: Known — contact
*Source: Wodenite — Polesotechnic League, Poul Anderson*

> *Patient, enormous, and genuinely interested in what you have to say.*

### Biology

Large draconic centauroids from a high-gravity world. Four sturdy legs,
humanoid upper torso with two arms, long neck, scaled hide. Average mass
700 kg, upper torso standing height 2.2 m, ground-to-spine approximately
1.6 m. Lifespan 150 years. Omnivorous with extremely wide dietary
flexibility. High total caloric throughput but low mass-specific metabolic
rate. Comfortable range 270–325 K. Nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere. Comfortable
in pressure up to 2.5 atm given high-gravity homeworld origin.

### Playing a Golvhaan

Patient, enormous, and genuinely interested in what you have to say.

The xenophilia is real and worth emphasising. Golvhaan find other species
interesting in a relaxed, unhurried way that larger species find disarming
and smaller species find reassuring. They ask questions. They listen to
the answers. They remember what they were told years later and refer back
to it. With a 150-year lifespan and genuine curiosity, a well-travelled
Golvhaan has accumulated an extraordinary depth of experience with other
species.

Calm, patient, and deliberate rather than non-violent. They simply have
very little aggression hair-trigger. A Golvhaan who is actually angry is
a serious problem — 700 kg of motivated centauroid is not a thing most
situations survive intact. They rarely get angry because they have the
size and confidence not to feel threatened by situations other species
find intimidating.

The physical reality shapes every interaction. A Golvhaan in a standard
human corridor is navigating something built for a species a tenth their
mass. The state of a ship's physical accommodations for a Golvhaan
crewmember is a good indicator of how long the crew has been together.

Golvhaan who have dealt with species that try to use physical presence
as a social lever — Skharri, primarily — find the attempt mildly amusing
and respond with patient courtesy, which the Skharri finds more
destabilising than counter-aggression would be.

> **At the table:** The patience is the key quality. A Golvhaan player
> who plays their character as someone who genuinely does not feel
> pressure the way smaller species do — who can wait, who considers
> before speaking — creates a distinctive presence. They are not slow;
> they are deliberate.
>
> The lifespan is a resource. A Golvhaan PC may have been present for
> events human characters know only as history. Use this.

### Mechanics

**Attribute adjustment:** Strength +1.

**Biological specialties:**
- *High-Gravity Physiology:* Bonus die on Endure in high-gravity
  environments (above 1.5 G). No penalty die in environments up to 2 G
  that would penalise other species.
- *Imposing Presence:* Bonus die on Manipulate or Command rolls where
  physical presence is relevant. Not intimidation by intent — simply
  the reality of 700 kg of scaled centauroid in the room.

**Size:** Large. Bonus die on Labor involving mass or force. Penalty die
on Stealth in standard environments. Cannot use standard equipment without
modification; cannot fit through standard corridors without ship-specific
design accommodation.

**Lifespan:** 150 years. Work with the GM to establish what the character
has witnessed personally that others only know from records.

## Vashori *(PC-viable)*
### Contact Status: Known — contact
*Source: Osirian — Viagens Interplanetarias, L. Sprague de Camp*

> *Warm, volatile, and they will absolutely remember that.*

### Biology

Bilateral reptiloid bipeds, scaled, oviparous. Average mass 80 kg, height
1.75 m. Ectothermic: low resting metabolism, strong temperature preferences.
Comfortable range 290–345 K; below 290 K they become progressively sluggish.
Carnivorous with moderate dietary flexibility. Lifespan 120 years.
Nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere. Small externally-incubated clutch; 6-month
incubation.

### Playing a Vashori

Warm, volatile, and they will absolutely remember that.

Vashori are among the most diplomatically accessible non-human species
in the known galaxy — polished traders and scholars with long, stable
civilisations behind them. The diplomatic shorthand *"very acceptable"*
is accurate as a species average. But it omits something important.

Vashori are **highly emotional and excitable**. Quick to enthusiasm, quick
to offence, quick to reconciliation. A Vashori in a negotiation is fully
present — they are not calculating their position behind a neutral
expression, they are *engaged*. A Vashori who is delighted shows it.
One who is offended shows that too, immediately and visibly.

This makes them genuinely warm counterparties but also volatile. They
are not grudge-holders in the Skharri sense; they get hot, they cool,
and they return to warmth. Genuine betrayal is different and remembered
with intensity, but everyday friction dissipates.

The ectothermic physiology has a practical consequence that is also a
roleplay texture: cold makes them slow. A Vashori in an unheated
compartment, or after an EVA in a suit whose heating has been unreliable,
is functioning at reduced capacity and knows it. They may be shorter-
tempered than usual — not from personality, but from physiological stress
they cannot entirely suppress.

Other crew who have served with a Vashori long enough know to check the
thermostat.

> **At the table:** The emotional range is the primary texture. Let your
> character feel things visibly — enthusiasm, offence, delight, suspicion.
> The reconciliation is real too: do not play Vashori as permanently
> aggrieved. They get hot and they come back. The lasting grudge is the
> exception, reserved for genuine betrayal.

### Mechanics

**Attribute adjustment:** Empathy +1.

**Biological specialties:**
- *Emotional Acuity:* Bonus die on Read when dealing with species they
  have significant experience with. High emotional bandwidth cuts both
  ways — it makes Vashori perceptive as well as reactive.
- *Ectothermic Resilience:* Bonus die on Endure in warm environments
  (above 305 K).

**Cold sensitivity:** Below 285 K (12 °C) without adequate thermal
protection, penalty die on all physical and Wits rolls. Below 275 K,
two penalty dice. Applies unless the suit has active heating rated for
the temperature.

**Emotional volatility:** When subjected to a significant social slight
or betrayal, the GM may call for a Wits roll to avoid an immediate
visible reaction. Failure means the reaction is real and uncontrolled.
This is a roleplay prompt, not a punishment.

## Vaelkhi *(PC-viable)*
### Contact Status: Known — contact
*Source: Ythrian — Technic History / Flandry Cycle, Poul Anderson*

> *Death in the sky is glorious — but skill, not recklessness, is what
> the God respects.*

### Biology

Large avian carnivores. Wings primary locomotion; hindlimb talons for
manipulation and perching. Average mass 50 kg, standing ground height
1.3 m, wingspan approximately 4 m. Hollow bones, high-efficiency
respiratory system. Lifespan approximately 80 years. Obligate aerial-hunt
carnivores; strong prey preferences; cultural taboo against eating non-
hunted food. Very high metabolic rate. Comfortable range 265–320 K.
Nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere; minimum 0.5 atm for flight.

### Playing a Vaelkhi

Death in the sky is glorious — but skill, not recklessness, is what
the God respects.

The **choth** — extended clan of 50-300 individuals — is the primary
social unit. Individual autonomy within the choth is strongly protected.
Leaving is legal and not uncommon; it is also permanent and defines you
forever. A Vaelkhi PC who has left their choth to travel with a starship
crew has made an irrevocable choice. What drove the decision is character
history worth establishing in detail.

The **God of Ythri** is a death-deity associated with the hunt, the sky,
and the moment of the kill. Dying in honourable hunt or battle is the
preferred death; dying of illness or age carries mild shame. This raises
risk appetite markedly — but Vaelkhi are patient, tactical hunters.
The God respects skill and patience as much as courage.

The key contrast with the Skharri, who superficially share a hunt culture:
Vaelkhi plan. They wait. A Vaelkhi who appears to be charging rashly has
usually already calculated three moves ahead and decided this is the
correct one. Honour is in the outcome, not the drama of the approach.

The dietary requirement is a quiet constant: Vaelkhi need fresh protein
frequently, and ideally from something they can consider hunted. Synthesised
food keeps them alive and makes them quietly miserable. A crew that keeps
a small livestock area for their Vaelkhi crewmember has noticed this. The
Vaelkhi has noticed that the crew noticed.

> **At the table:** Three things define Vaelkhi play. First, the choth
> absence — wherever they are, they have left something permanent behind.
> Second, the hunt patience — they plan, they wait, they choose the moment.
> Third, the physical environment — every open space is an opportunity
> and every corridor is a constraint. Use all three consistently.

### Mechanics

**Attribute adjustment:** Agility +1.

**Biological specialties:**
- *Powered Flight:* In open environments with sufficient atmosphere
  (0.5 atm minimum), Vaelkhi fly — a different mode of movement with
  full three-dimensional options. Unavailable in enclosed environments.
- *Aerial Hunter:* Bonus die on Shoot and Move when attacking or
  manoeuvring in three dimensions from an elevated position.

**Metabolic demand:** Must eat at least twice per active day. Each missed
feeding imposes a cumulative penalty die on all rolls until fed. After
three missed feedings, incapacitated.

**Ground movement:** Penalty die on Move rolls requiring ground-based
agility. Does not apply to flight or talon-climbing.

**Corridor constraint:** After more than one hour in confined spaces,
penalty die on all rolls until 30 minutes in open space. Ships with a
Vaelkhi crewmember have at least one high-volume compartment.

## Skharri *(PC-viable — advanced)*
### Contact Status: Known — hostile (treaty in force)
*Source: Kzin — Known Space, Larry Niven*

> *The name is earned. Everything is earned.*

### Biology

Large felinoid obligate carnivores. Digitigrade bipeds, tawny-furred.
Average mass 160 kg, height 2.4 m. Lifespan approximately 200 years.
Very high metabolic rate. Strong prey preferences; live or freshly killed
meat strongly preferred physiologically and culturally. Comfortable range
270–330 K. Nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere. Capable of quadrupedal sprint in
combat.

### Playing a Skharri

The name is earned. Everything is earned.

Skharri society is built on honour, hierarchy, and demonstrated superiority.
Only males with sufficient honour earn the right to a name — nameless males
are referred to by occupation. A Skharri PC almost certainly has a name;
what they did to earn it, and what it cost, is character history worth
establishing in detail.

The defining failure mode of the species is the **scream-and-leap**: the
honour-instinct that demands attack before strategic analysis is complete.
The Skharri have lost major wars against humans by the same mechanism each
time — attacking immediately without diplomatic contact, classifying a
capable species as prey, and discovering the error at enormous cost. The
species understands this intellectually. The instinct remains.

A Skharri PC in a mixed-species crew is unusual within their own culture.
They have learned that other species are not simply prey. This does not
make them gentle — it makes them a Skharri who has updated their prior
assumptions while remaining unmistakably themselves. The honour-economy
still operates. Insults still require response. The question is whether
the response is immediate violence or something more considered.

The 200-year lifespan means a Skharri PC has personal memory of events
that happened before human characters were born. This is a resource and
a weight.

> **At the table:** The honour-economy is the central texture. Everything
> is ranked: who has demonstrated what, who owes respect to whom, what
> constitutes an insult, what requires a response. A Skharri who does not
> respond to a genuine insult loses standing — in their own eyes first.
>
> The scream-and-leap is a mechanic but also a roleplay prompt. There
> will be moments when the instinct fires and the player has to decide
> whether their character has the discipline to redirect it. Lean into
> those moments rather than avoiding them.
>
> Playing a Skharri well requires the player to have genuinely thought
> about what their character is doing in a mixed-species crew. The
> backstory is not optional decoration — it is the reason the character
> is functional at the table.

### Mechanics

**Attribute adjustment:** Strength +1.

**Biological specialties:**
- *Predator Senses:* Bonus die on Scan rolls involving detection of
  living beings, tracking, or noticing movement.
- *Natural Weapons:* Fight rolls using claws and teeth treat Gear as 1
  at all times without requiring equipment.

**Honour instinct (scream-and-leap):** When genuinely insulted,
challenged, or witnessing a direct attack on someone under their
protection, the GM may call for a Wits roll (difficulty 2). Failure
means the instinct fires: the Skharri acts immediately and physically.
This is a character beat, not a punishment.

**Social standing:** Skharri read the honour-economy of every room they
enter. High Fight or Command ratings earn Skharri NPC respect; low
ratings earn contempt with social consequences.

**Dietary constraint:** Synthesised protein sustains but does not
satisfy. Extended periods on synthesised food create a morale texture
of contained dissatisfaction the GM may invoke as narrative pressure.

> **GM note:** A player who plays a Skharri as a human who growls is
> missing the point. A player who plays them as an alien who has genuinely
> worked to understand other species while remaining unmistakably
> themselves is playing one of the most rewarding characters in the setting.

## Nakhavi *(PC-viable — advanced)*
### Contact Status: Known — contact (recent; limited understanding)
*Source: Octopod — Children of Ruin, Adrian Tchaikovsky*

> *Every moment is vivid. There are not many of them.*

### Biology

Cephalopod sophonts, technology-uplifted from an octopus ancestor. Eight
manipulator arms with semi-autonomous nervous ganglia — the arms can
problem-solve partially independently of the central brain. Communication
via chromatophores: full-body skin colour and pattern changes encoding
language across multiple simultaneous channels. No spoken language.

Average mass 12 kg, mantle height 0.6 m, arm span approximately 1.5 m.
Aquatic primary; surface-capable with equipment. Lifespan 18 years.
Semelparous: a single reproductive event, after which the individual dies.
High metabolic rate. Comfortable range 273–305 K. Carnivorous with moderate
dietary flexibility.

### Playing a Nakhavi

Every moment is vivid. There are not many of them.

The 18-year lifespan is the defining fact of Nakhavi existence. A Nakhavi
character who is a seasoned spacer has perhaps 8-10 years remaining. This
is not a source of despair — it is the shape of existence. The question
is what to do with the time, and Nakhavi generally answer that question
with intensity and full attention.

Nakhavi intelligence is genuinely distributed. The arms think, partially
independently. A Nakhavi solving a problem is not one mind working on it —
it is something closer to a small committee, with the central brain as
moderator. This is their greatest asset and genuinely alien in a way other
sophonts find difficult to understand even after extended contact.

Communication is via chromatophores — skin display, not sound. Interface
with other species requires translation technology. The technology works.
It is not the same as speaking directly. Something is always slightly lost.
Nakhavi who have worked with other species long enough become more explicit
than their native language requires, compensating for what the translation
drops.

The curiosity is intense and genuine. Nakhavi find almost everything
interesting, and the short lifespan gives that curiosity an edge — there
is limited time, so they move through the universe quickly and with full
attention.

> **At the table:** Two things above all. First, the time — feel the
> 18-year lifespan as a texture, not a problem to solve. They are not
> sad about it. They are *present* in a way longer-lived species often
> are not. Second, the communication — every conversation goes through
> a layer of translation, and something is always slightly lost. This is
> a constant reminder that this character's experience of the universe
> is genuinely different from everyone else at the table.
>
> A campaign that runs for three game-years has moved a Nakhavi character
> meaningfully through their lifespan. Track this. Use it.

### Mechanics

**Attribute adjustment:** Intellect +1.

**Biological specialties:**
- *Distributed Cognition:* Bonus die on any Intellect or Wits roll
  involving a complex multi-variable problem. The arms are working on
  it too.
- *Chromatophore Acuity:* Bonus die on Read when dealing with other
  Nakhavi.

**Aquatic:** On the surface without equipment, penalty die on Move and
visibly uncomfortable. Full ship operations require a pressurised
environment suit with translation interface. Ships with a Nakhavi
crewmember have a pressurised wet compartment.

**Communication:** Without translation technology, communication limited
to simple gestures. The GM may invoke a translation ambiguity as a
complication on a bare success in social situations.

**Lifespan:** Track the character's age. At 15 years, the semelparous
drive begins to make itself felt physiologically. Work with the player
on how this manifests — it is one of the richest character arcs in the
setting if engaged with honestly.

## Kreeth
### Contact Status: Known — hostile (no contact achieved)
*Source: Arachnid/Bug — Starship Troopers, Heinlein*

> *There is no individual. There is only the will.*

### Biology

Six-limbed eusocial insectoids. Low-slung exoskeletal bodies. Average
worker/warrior mass approximately 90 kg, height 1.2 m at shoulder — not
erect. Queens are orders of magnitude larger and longer-lived. Worker/
warrior lifespan approximately 12 years. Require a reducing atmosphere
hostile to most other species without protection. Comfortable range
260–320 K. Wide pressure tolerance from exoskeletal structure. Omnivorous;
consume almost any organic material. Very high warrior metabolic rate.

Reproduce by r-strategy: Queens produce thousands of eggs per cycle,
multiple times per year. Recovery from population collapse is rapid.
Warrior units severed from Queen neurological contact become non-functional
within hours.

### Encountering Kreeth

There is no individual. There is only the will.

The Kreeth are the design-space inverse of every other species in this
document: maximum social cohesion, near-zero faction tendency, hierarchy
that is neurological rather than social. Workers and warriors are not
individuals choosing to follow orders — they are sub-units of a distributed
organism directed by Brains and Queens. Diplomatic contact has never been
achieved because there is no individual with the cognitive independence
to conduct it.

Strategic decisions are made by Brains: methodical, patient, and not
reckless despite the expendability of individual units. The Kreeth do not
waste warriors carelessly — they spend them deliberately and in quantity.
The distinction matters when you are on the other side of it.

Encountering Kreeth in the field means encountering a coordinated tactical
response, not individual combatants with individual decision-making. They
do not negotiate. They do not hesitate. They do not break from losses
that would route any other species, because the concept of *routing*
belongs to individual organisms with individual survival drives.

The atmosphere requirement means most Kreeth encounters happen in
environments where other species are already operating under constraint —
suited, monitoring air supplies, in territory the Kreeth have prepared.
This is not accidental.

> **At the table:** Kreeth encounters are survival situations, not
> diplomatic problems. There is no misunderstanding to resolve. The
> question is always: get out intact, or don't. Run Kreeth as a
> coordinated system rather than as individual opponents — they react
> to the tactical situation as a whole. Disrupting their communication
> is more valuable than killing individual units.
>
> The strategic simulation tracks Kreeth territorial pressure as a
> background constant. When they appear at the table, something in
> that pressure has found a local expression.

### Mechanics (Encountering)

**Coordination:** When three or more warriors are in contact with their
Queen-network, they act on a single initiative with shared tactical
awareness. Disrupting communication degrades this significantly.

**Severed units:** A warrior cut off from Queen-contact begins degrading
immediately. After one hour without contact, penalty die on all rolls.
After four hours, non-functional.

**Atmosphere:** Operating in Kreeth-controlled environments without sealed
suits and air supply is not possible for most species.

**Scale:** Individual warrior combat stats are moderate. The danger is
numbers, coordination, and prepared environment.

## Kraathi
### Contact Status: Known — contact (formal; deeply uncomfortable)
*Source: K'Kree — Traveller, GDW/Marc Miller*

> *The G'naak must be cleansed. This is not politics. This is doctrine.*

### Biology

Large centauroid obligate herbivores. Six-limbed: four legs for locomotion,
two manipulator arms. Average mass 450 kg, head height 2.0 m standing.
Homeworld open plains; profound claustrophobia in enclosed spaces has
shaped their entire civilisational architecture — starships with vast open
interior volumes, no small rooms anywhere in Kraathi design. Nitrogen-oxygen
atmosphere. Comfortable range 278–330 K. Obligate herbivore: consuming or
handling meat is a profound cultural and quasi-religious taboo. Dietary
flexibility near zero for animal protein. Lifespan approximately 80 years.
Rigid dominant-male herd hierarchy.

### Encountering Kraathi

The G'naak must be cleansed. This is not politics. This is doctrine.

The defining characteristic is **herd-dependency**: a Kraathi cannot
function psychologically in isolation or even small groups. A diplomat of
rank travels with their full extended family — not as an entourage, but
as a psychological necessity. Understanding this explains why Kraathi
delegations are always larger than other species consider necessary.

The second defining characteristic is the **G'naak crusade**: Kraathi
consider carnivores — they use the term *G'naak*, "eaters of the dead"
— a moral abomination. Their stated civilisational goal is the elimination
of all carnivorous sophonts, prosecuted as a military program across
centuries. This is not rhetorical posturing. It is doctrine with military
action behind it.

**What this means for a mixed crew:** any crew member who is a carnivore
or omnivore is, in Kraathi doctrine, G'naak. Formal diplomatic contexts
create a suspended frame in which this is not acted upon — Kraathi are
not impulsive, and they understand the concept of a meeting under truce.
But the underlying position does not change.

The ship's cafeteria menu is a diplomatic document in Kraathi space.
Any visible consumption or preparation of animal protein in a Kraathi
context is an escalation, intentional or not. Crews operating near
Kraathi territory adapt their external-facing behaviour accordingly.
What happens in the galley with the door closed is the crew's business.

Kraathi who are willing to deal with mixed-species crews are either
pragmatists operating at the edge of their civilisational doctrine, or
are conducting a longer game: maintaining contact for intelligence
purposes while the crusade continues at the strategic level.

> **At the table:** Kraathi encounters are exercises in sustained
> diplomatic tension. Every interaction carries the subtext of the crusade.
> Kraathi diplomats are not personally violent or rude — they are formal,
> hierarchical, and patient. The menace is structural, not personal.
>
> The claustrophobia is a useful detail. A Kraathi in a standard human
> ship corridor is already under physiological stress. Choosing meeting
> locations that are large and open demonstrates cultural awareness that
> Kraathi notice and credit.

### Mechanics (Encountering)

**Herd requirement:** A Kraathi NPC alone or in a group of fewer than
four is under psychological stress. All Wits rolls take a penalty die.

**Claustrophobia:** In standard enclosed spaces, penalty die on all rolls
after the first 30 minutes. Will not willingly remain enclosed longer
than necessary.

**G'naak response:** A Kraathi NPC who directly witnesses carnivore
behaviour in an informal context must make a disposition roll. On a
failure, the diplomatic frame collapses and the G'naak crusade becomes
the explicit topic of conversation. This is a table moment, not a
combat trigger — handle it as a serious diplomatic rupture.

## Nhaveth
### Contact Status: Known — limited (true nature not widely understood)
*Source: Original*

> *The host is a convenience. You are something to be assessed.*

### Biology

**Natural form:** Vermiform neural parasites. Approximately 0.6 m length,
1.5 kg. Limbless, with a recessed manipulator fringe. Narrow thermal
tolerance (280–320 K) without host thermoregulation.

**Operational form:** A parasitised Kethara host — large hominid,
approximately 110 kg, 1.85 m — fully controlled by the Nhaveth neural
web. The parasite provides cognition; the host provides everything else.
All interactions with other species are conducted via host.

Effective lifespan via sequential host transfer: approximately 600 years.
Reproductive strategy is r-selection in numbers but effective rate is low —
most larvae die in incompatible hosts before maturation. Nhaveth society
fractures into **courts** controlled by a dominant Nhaveth. Courts war on
each other almost constantly. Social cohesion within a court is absolute;
between courts, there is none.

### Encountering Nhaveth

The host is a convenience. You are something to be assessed.

The fundamental complication of Nhaveth encounter is identification.
Most species dealing with a Nhaveth-controlled individual do not know
what they are dealing with. The Kethara host appears to be an individual
of a known species. It behaves consistently. It has a name, a history,
and a position. Only careful observation over time reveals that something
is directing it that is not what it appears.

What the Nhaveth wants from an encounter depends entirely on which court
they belong to, what that court's current priorities are, and whether the
people they are meeting are useful, dangerous, or irrelevant. With a
600-year lifespan and perfect recall, every Nhaveth is operating from a
strategic position that extends centuries in both directions. A meeting
that seems trivial may be the thirteenth step in a plan that started
before most people in the room were born.

Individual Nhaveth are not uniformly hostile to other species — they
are instrumental. Other species are host-stock, labour, useful tools,
obstacles, or threats. Which category applies depends on the court's
current needs. A Nhaveth who appears cooperative is being cooperative
because it serves their purposes.

The court structure means Nhaveth are not a unified faction. Two Nhaveth
from different courts are enemies first and members of the same species
second. This creates genuine opportunities for players who understand it.

> **At the table:** The identification problem is the core of Nhaveth
> encounter. Players who have invested in Scan or Read — and who have
> the relevant tells from the separate player handout — have a genuine
> advantage in recognising what they are dealing with. Players who have
> not may interact with a Nhaveth-controlled individual through an entire
> session before something reveals the situation. Both are valid table
> experiences.
>
> Once identified, the question shifts to: now what? Direct confrontation
> with a Nhaveth in a position of authority is dangerous. They have been
> playing this game for centuries. Approach accordingly.
>
> *Note: Specific detection tells are in the separate Nhaveth handout,
> distributed at the GM's discretion.*

### Mechanics (Encountering)

**Identification:** Detecting Nhaveth control requires a Scan or Read
roll (difficulty 3) and several minutes of observation. Specific tells
from the handout reduce difficulty to 2 and allow faster detection.
Without the handout, the player must describe exactly what they are
looking for — vague suspicion is not sufficient.

**Host capabilities:** A Nhaveth-controlled Kethara host has the full
physical capabilities of the Kethara — strong, durable, very high pain
tolerance (co-evolved suppressed pain and flight responses). The Nhaveth's
centuries of intelligence direct those capabilities.

**Court authority:** A dominant Nhaveth within their court issues
directions that will be followed without question by every court member.

**Natural form vulnerability:** A Nhaveth outside a host is physically
fragile (1.5 kg, limbless) and operating in a narrow thermal range.
This is a state they will do almost anything to avoid.

## Vardhek
### Contact Status: Known — contact (formal treaty; deep strategic tension)
*Source: Merseian — Technic History / Flandry Cycle, Poul Anderson*

> *Everything is an item on a centuries-long ledger. Including this
> conversation.*

### Biology

Large reptiloid-amphibioid bipeds. Green-scaled, tailed, heavy-set.
Average mass 110 kg, height 2.1 m; tail adds approximately 0.9 m beyond
standing height. Lifespan approximately 110 years. Amphibioid ancestry;
prefer warm-temperate to warm environments. Comfortable range 275–330 K.
Nitrogen-oxygen atmosphere. Omnivorous with moderate dietary flexibility.
Reproductively K-strategist; reproduction culturally regulated by the
Roidhunate as a deliberate policy instrument — population is a strategic
resource, managed accordingly.

### Encountering Vardhek

Everything is an item on a centuries-long ledger. Including this
conversation.

The defining characteristic is **Gethfennu** — the Long View. Vardhek
think in generational timescales. A political program that will bear
fruit in 300 years is a sensible plan, not an abstraction. This makes
them extraordinarily capable strategists and extraordinarily dangerous
adversaries — patient, methodical, and willing to invest enormous
resources in long-horizon subversion that other species rarely recognise
until too late.

The **Roidhunate** is a hierarchical imperial structure headed by the
Roidhun. Rank is earned through demonstrated competence and service —
a meritocratic layer operates within the aristocracy. A Vardhek who has
risen to a position of authority has earned it in a system that does not
reward incompetence.

Their strategic program is species-level galactic dominance. This is not
secret; it is simply pursued with a patience that other species find
difficult to hold in mind across the duration of a single diplomatic
relationship, let alone centuries. The Vardhek understand that they do
not need to win *this* negotiation. They need to be in a better position
after it than before it. That is a different goal, and it plays out very
differently.

Vardhek engagement with other species is instrumental: they study alien
species intensely, recruit alien agents, and form alliances of convenience.
This is engagement in service of the Long View, not genuine openness. A
Vardhek who appears curious about you is gathering information. A Vardhek
who appears friendly is assessing your utility. Neither is personal malice
— it is a different relationship to what *knowing someone* means.

The grievance memory is long and literal. Territorial losses, treaty
violations, and strategic setbacks are items on a ledger that Vardhek
diplomats carry across generations. They reference events from a century
ago as if they were recent, because to the Long View they are.

> **At the table:** Every Vardhek interaction carries the question:
> *where does this fit in the Long View?* A Vardhek merchant, a Vardhek
> scholar, a Vardhek who appears to be a refugee — all exist in a
> civilisational context that has a position on whatever the players are
> doing. This does not make every Vardhek a spy. It makes every Vardhek
> a person shaped by a culture whose deepest assumption is that everything
> matters strategically, if you look at the right timescale.
>
> The most effective Vardhek NPCs are ones who are personally genuine —
> who may actually like specific player characters, who can be trusted
> within specific contexts — while remaining unmistakably shaped by the
> Long View. That tension is more interesting than a simple adversary.
>
> A possible future PC path exists for a Vardhek exile or defector:
> someone whose personal history has placed them outside the Roidhunate's
> framework. Not designed for currently, but not foreclosed.

### Mechanics (Encountering)

**Long View assessment:** Any Vardhek NPC of consequence has a
pre-existing strategic position on the players' faction, ship, and
activities. The GM should establish this before the encounter. Players
may discover it through Scan, Read, or Administer rolls — or may never
discover it, and simply notice the Vardhek seemed to know more than
they should have.

**Recruitment:** Vardhek actively seek useful individuals from other
species. A player character who demonstrates exceptional capability in
a Vardhek's presence may receive an approach. The Vardhek genuinely
values competence — but the context is always the Long View.

**Grievance ledger:** If the players' faction has history with the
Roidhunate, the Vardhek they encounter knows about it and has a position
on it. It will surface eventually.

**Meritocratic respect:** Vardhek respect demonstrated competence
regardless of species. A character who has visibly performed well in a
difficult situation earns a specific kind of Vardhek regard that is
genuine and useful. The GM may grant a bonus die on social rolls with
that Vardhek in future encounters.
