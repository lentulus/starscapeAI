# Resolution

## Overview

This chapter contains the complete dice mechanics of the game. Everything
at the table that requires a roll — combat, social interaction, technical
work, navigation, survival — uses the same core system. Learn this chapter
and you have learned the mechanical foundation of the game.

The system uses standard six-sided dice (D6) in pools. Dice are
distinguished by colour, not type. A consequence die uses a four-sided
die (D4) and is a separate mechanic with a specific purpose.

---

## The Dice

Coloured dice distinguish the source of each die in the pool at a glance.
This matters when pushing a roll and when reading results quickly at the table.

| Colour | Source | Notes |
|--------|--------|-------|
| White | Attribute | The character's raw capability |
| Blue | Skill | Trained competence |
| Green | Gear | Equipment quality and condition |
| Red | Penalty | Removed before rolling; reduces pool |
| Black D4 | Consequence | Always rolled; always costs something |

A typical pool is a handful of white, blue, and green dice rolled together.
Count the sixes. That is your result.

---

## Building the Pool

Every roll begins by building a dice pool from three sources:

**Attribute** (white dice) — the relevant attribute rating. Between 1
and 5 dice.

**Skill** (blue dice) — the relevant skill rating. Between 0 and 5 dice.
A character with no rating in the relevant skill contributes 0 blue dice —
they roll attribute only.

**Gear** (green dice) — the rating of relevant equipment, if any. Between
0 and 4 dice. Many rolls involve no gear.

Add the dice together. This is your base pool.

**Example:** A character with Agility 3, Shoot 2, and a sidearm rated
Gear 2 rolls 3 white + 2 blue + 2 green = 7 dice.

### Penalty Dice

Some conditions, environments, and situations impose **penalty dice**.
Each penalty die removes one die from the pool before rolling, starting
with gear dice, then skill dice, then attribute dice.

Penalty dice are represented by red dice placed beside the pool. Count
them, remove that many dice from the pool (in the order above), then
set the red dice aside. They are not rolled.

A pool reduced to zero dice cannot be rolled. The action automatically
fails. If a pool would be reduced below zero, the situation is beyond
the character's current capability.

**Example:** The same character (7 dice) is firing in poor visibility
(1 penalty die). Remove 1 green die. Roll 6 dice instead.

### Bonus Dice

Some specialties, conditions, and situations add **bonus dice** to the
pool. Bonus dice are additional dice of the relevant colour — usually
white (attribute) or blue (skill) — added before rolling.

Bonus dice are noted as "+1 die" in specialty descriptions. They
represent the mechanical expression of training, advantage, or
circumstance that goes beyond the base pool.

## Rolling and Reading Results

Roll all dice in the pool simultaneously. Count every die showing a **6**.

| Sixes | Result |
|-------|--------|
| 0 | **Failure.** The attempt does not succeed. Something goes wrong, or simply does not work. |
| 1 | **Bare success.** The goal is achieved, but barely. Complications are possible. |
| 2 | **Clean success.** The goal is achieved without significant complication. |
| 3+ | **Outstanding success.** The goal is achieved with extra effect, speed, or information. |

### What Failure Means

A failure is not always catastrophic. The GM has two options on a failure:

**Hard failure** — the attempt simply does not succeed. The character
did not pick the lock, did not convince the official, did not make the
jump calculation in time.

**Success at cost** — the character may achieve their goal, but something
else suffers. The lock opens, but the tool breaks. The official is
convinced, but someone noticed the conversation. The jump calculation
is made, but the margin is thin. This is always the player's choice to
accept. The GM offers it; the player decides.

Success at cost is a useful tool for keeping the narrative moving without
removing consequence from failure.

> **Roleplay note:** Failure is not the end of a scene — it is the
> beginning of a different one. Encourage players to describe what their
> character tries and what goes wrong, rather than treating failure as
> a narrative full stop. The most interesting moments at the table often
> begin with a failed roll.

## Specialties

A specialty is a focused application of a skill that reflects specific
training, background, or experience. Specialties do one of three things:

**Remove a penalty** that would normally apply to this specific
application of the skill.

**Add a bonus die** in specific circumstances.

**Enable an attempt** that would otherwise be unavailable — a roll
that the character simply could not make without the relevant knowledge.

Specialties are tied to specific skills. A character may not have more
specialties in a single skill than their rating in that skill.

When a specialty applies, state it before rolling. The bonus or removal
applies to that roll.

---

## The Opacity Rule

Without the relevant **Exposure** specialty, alien emotional states,
intentions, and social signals are opaque.

A character can observe alien behaviour — the Vashori's tail is moving,
the Skharri's ears are back, the Vaelkhi has gone very still — but the
meaning is inaccessible. **Read rolls for emotional or intentional
content against an unfamiliar species automatically fail.** Manipulate
rolls against unfamiliar species take a **penalty die** from operating
without feedback.

Exposure does not make aliens simple. It makes them *legible*.

This applies symmetrically. A Shekhari without Exposure: Human finds
human emotional states as opaque as a human without Exposure: Shekhari
finds theirs. The capacity to read another species is earned, not assumed.

> **Roleplay note:** The Opacity Rule is not a punishment — it is a
> description of reality. A character who develops Exposure to a
> crewmate's species over the course of a campaign has done something
> real and earns the mechanical benefit accordingly. That is a character
> arc with mechanical expression.

## Pushing a Roll

After seeing a failed or unsatisfactory result, a character may **push**
the roll once. Pushing represents digging deeper — extra effort, ignoring
pain, taking risks, or simply refusing to accept the result.

To push:

1. Lock out any dice showing a **1**. These dice are set aside and
   cannot be rerolled.
2. Reroll all remaining dice that did not show a **6**.
3. Count sixes across all dice — including the locked sixes from
   the first roll and the new results.

A push always has a **narrative consequence**. Before the reroll,
the GM describes what pushing costs — a tool strained beyond its
rating, a social situation made more tense, physical exhaustion that
will matter later, a margin of safety that no longer exists. The
consequence applies regardless of the result of the push.

On a pushed failure — no sixes after both rolls — something goes
**meaningfully wrong**. This is not the same as a simple failure.
The GM should treat a pushed failure as a scene-changing event,
not merely an unsuccessful attempt.

Each roll may only be pushed once.

> **Roleplay note:** Pushing should feel like something. Encourage
> players to describe what their character is doing differently on
> the push — what they are risking, what they are ignoring, what
> they are spending. A push that is just "I roll again" is a missed
> opportunity.

## Levels of Success

Count every six in the final pool (after pushing, if pushed):

**0 sixes — Failure.**
The attempt does not succeed. See *What Failure Means* above.

**1 six — Bare success.**
The goal is achieved, but the margin is thin. The GM may introduce
a minor complication, a cost, or a condition on the success. In
opposed rolls, bare success may not be enough.

**2 sixes — Clean success.**
The goal is achieved without significant complication. Standard result.

**3 sixes — Strong success.**
The goal is achieved with extra effect. In combat, additional damage
or a tactical advantage. In social situations, more information, a
better outcome, or an additional concession. In technical work, faster
completion or better quality.

**4+ sixes — Outstanding success.**
The goal is achieved with exceptional effect. The GM should treat
this as a scene-defining moment in the relevant skill area — not just
better, but qualitatively different.

Levels of success matter most in **social and information skills**,
where the difference between a bare success and an outstanding one
is the difference between knowing something happened and understanding
why. In **combat**, additional sixes translate to additional effect
as described in the Combat chapter. In **technical tasks**, they
affect speed and quality.

> **Roleplay note:** Encourage players to describe their successes
> at the level they achieved, not just at the minimum. A character
> who rolls four sixes on Read has not merely detected a lie —
> they have read the room completely and understand what is actually
> happening. That deserves a richer description than "I succeed."

## Opposed Rolls

When two characters act directly against each other — one attempting
to deceive, the other attempting to detect the deception; one attempting
to outrun the other; one attacking, the other defending — both roll
their relevant skill pools simultaneously.

**The character with more sixes wins.**

On a tie, the **acting character wins** — the attempt succeeds.

If neither character rolls any sixes, the situation is unresolved —
neither attempt succeeded, and the GM advances the scene accordingly.

**Common opposed pairs:**

| Acting | Resisting |
|--------|-----------|
| Manipulate | Read |
| Stealth | Scan |
| Fight (attack) | Fight (defence) |
| Shoot | Move (evading) |
| Command | Manipulate (resisting orders) |

In opposed rolls, levels of success matter for **degree** rather than
binary outcome. A character who wins with three sixes against an
opponent's one has not merely succeeded — they have succeeded
convincingly, with consequences for the loser's position.

## Extended Tasks

Some tasks cannot be resolved in a single roll. An **extended task**
has a difficulty threshold — a total number of successes needed across
multiple rolls — representing work that takes time and sustained effort.

**Setting a threshold:**

| Difficulty | Threshold | Typical example |
|------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Simple | 3 | Minor repair, basic research question |
| Moderate | 6 | Significant repair, investigating an unknown |
| Hard | 9 | Major overhaul, complex negotiation |
| Extreme | 12+ | Rebuilding from scratch, first contact protocol |

Each roll represents a meaningful period of work — a scene, not a
moment. Successes accumulate across rolls until the threshold is reached.

**Failures** on extended tasks do not subtract from accumulated
successes but introduce **complications** — a setback, a new
obstacle, or a cost that must be addressed. A pushed failure on
an extended task introduces a serious complication that may change
the nature of the task.

**Interruption** — if an extended task is interrupted before
completion, accumulated successes may be preserved (at the GM's
discretion) if the character can return to the task under similar
conditions. If conditions have changed significantly, the GM may
require starting over or reducing the accumulated total.

> **Roleplay note:** Extended tasks are an opportunity for the table
> to breathe. Each roll is a scene, not merely a dice result. What
> is happening during the repair? What complications arise during
> the negotiation? What does the research reveal along the way that
> was not the goal but matters anyway? The dice provide structure;
> the players and GM provide texture.

## Difficulty

Most rolls have no fixed difficulty — you roll and count sixes, and
the result speaks for itself. Difficulty is expressed through **pool
modification** rather than target numbers:

**Favourable conditions** — bonus die added to the pool.

**Standard conditions** — pool as built, no modification.

**Difficult conditions** — one penalty die.

**Very difficult conditions** — two penalty dice.

**At the limit of possibility** — three or more penalty dice, or a
pool reduced so far that success requires a push or outstanding luck.

**Impossible** — do not call for a roll. If something cannot be done,
say so and explain why. Rolls are for uncertain outcomes, not certain
ones.

This approach keeps the table moving — there are no target numbers
to look up, no difficulty charts to consult. The GM builds the pool
modification from the situation and the player rolls.

## The Consequence Die

The **consequence die** is a black D4, rolled separately from the
main pool. It is not a penalty die — it does not reduce the pool.
It is a signal that the situation will cost something regardless of
success or failure.

The GM introduces a consequence die when:

- The situation has active opposition that cannot be overcome by
  skill alone
- The character is attempting something in a context that is
  fundamentally hostile to their survival
- The narrative has reached a moment where something must go wrong,
  and the only question is what

The consequence die is rolled alongside the main pool. Its result
determines **what the situation costs**, regardless of the main
roll's outcome:

| D4 | Consequence |
|----|-------------|
| 1 | **Minimal cost.** Something minor is used up, worn, or spent. A charge, a supply, a small advantage. |
| 2 | **Complication.** Success or failure comes with a condition attached that must be dealt with. |
| 3 | **Collateral.** Someone or something else takes the cost. Equipment, a bystander, a relationship. |
| 4 | **Full cost.** The thing the situation warned about happens. The danger was real. |

The consequence die result is interpreted by the GM in the context
of the specific situation. A consequence die in a firefight means
something different from one in a negotiation with a Vardhek diplomat.

**The consequence die is not a punishment.** It is a tool for creating
moments where the universe is not neutral — where the situation has
teeth regardless of how well the character performs. It should be
used deliberately and sparingly, not as a default escalation.

> **Roleplay note:** When the GM places a black D4 on the table,
> the players know something will cost them. This is a moment of
> acknowledged tension, not a hidden threat. The consequence die
> works best when its introduction is visible — "this situation
> has a consequence die" — so that the player can choose whether
> to push forward or find another approach. It is information, not
> punishment.

## Conditions

Characters can accumulate **conditions** that affect their capability.
Conditions come from injury, environmental exposure, psychological
stress, and the consequences of pushed rolls.

Each condition imposes one penalty die on relevant rolls until it
is resolved. Resolution depends on the condition — a physical injury
requires medical treatment and rest; environmental exposure requires
warmth, food, or shelter; psychological stress requires time and
circumstances.

**Condition track:** A character can sustain up to four conditions
before they are incapacitated. Incapacitation means they cannot act
meaningfully until at least one condition is resolved.

Specific conditions — wounds, hypothermia, vacuum exposure, Skharri
honour-debt — are detailed in their relevant chapters. The general
rule is: each condition is one penalty die on relevant rolls, and
four conditions is the limit before the character is out of the scene.

## Group Rolls

Some tasks benefit from multiple characters working together. When
the group attempts a task collectively:

**One character leads** — they make the primary roll using their
full pool.

**Each assisting character** contributes one bonus die if they have
at least Skill 1 in the relevant skill. Assistance beyond three
characters provides no additional benefit — too many people and
someone is in the way.

Group rolls are appropriate for: coordinated physical tasks (Labor),
combined fire (Shoot), crew emergency response (Command + relevant
skills), extended research (Science).

Group rolls are **not** appropriate for tasks that are inherently
individual: piloting a ship, conducting a negotiation, reading a
person's intentions.

---

## When Not to Roll

**Do not call for a roll when:**

The outcome is certain. If a character with Tech 3 and appropriate
tools is repairing a simple system with plenty of time and no pressure,
they repair it. The roll is for uncertainty, not for procedure.

The outcome does not matter. If the result of failure would not
change anything at the table, the roll is wasted time.

The character has no meaningful chance. If the pool, after all
modifications, is zero or negative, tell the player the attempt
is not possible in current conditions. Rolls are not for the
appearance of fairness when the outcome is predetermined.

The moment calls for roleplay, not resolution. Not every social
interaction is a Manipulate roll. Not every observation is a Scan
roll. Players who engage with the world through description and
roleplay should be rewarded with information and outcomes directly,
reserving rolls for moments of genuine uncertainty with meaningful
stakes.

> **Design note:** The most common mistake in YZE-adjacent games is
> rolling too often. The dice are most powerful when they are saved
> for moments that matter. A table that rolls for everything loses
> the texture that makes individual rolls significant. A table that
> rolls only when the outcome is genuinely uncertain — and the result
> genuinely consequential — finds that each roll carries weight.
