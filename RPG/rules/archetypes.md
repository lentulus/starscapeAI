# Archetypes

## Overview

An archetype is a character's formation — the combination of background and
expertise that shaped who they are before the campaign begins. It is not a
job title or a character class. It is the answer to: *where did you come
from, and what did life teach you to do?*

Archetypes work in two layers:

**Background** — the broad environment and life-context that shaped the
character. Six backgrounds cover the realistic spread of how people end up
aboard a ship or in a special operations context in this setting.

**Expertise** — the specific competency the character developed within that
background. Four expertises per background, chosen at character creation.
Expertise is expressed primarily through specialties.

A character is described as *Background / Expertise*: a Spacer/Engineer, a
Military/Intelligence operative, a Citizen/Academic. The combination
generates a character with a specific formation, specific skills, and a
specific relationship to the universe — without prescribing personality
or locking out anything not on the sheet.

## The Opacity Rule

Without the relevant **Exposure** specialty, alien emotional states,
intentions, and social signals are opaque.

A character can observe alien behaviour — the Vashori's tail is moving,
the Skharri's ears are back, the Vaelkhi has gone very still — but the
meaning is inaccessible. Read rolls for emotional or intentional content
against an unfamiliar species **automatically fail**. Manipulate rolls
against unfamiliar species take a **penalty die** from operating without
feedback.

Exposure does not make aliens simple. It makes them *legible*.

This applies symmetrically: a Shekhari without Exposure: Human finds human
emotional states as opaque as a human without Exposure: Shekhari finds
theirs. The capacity to read another species is earned, not assumed.

> **Roleplay note:** The Opacity Rule is not a punishment — it is a
> description of reality. Louis Wu had spent enough time around Kzinti to
> read them. Most humans hadn't, and treated them accordingly: as
> unpredictable aliens whose internal states were guesswork. A character
> who develops Exposure to a crewmate's species over the course of a
> campaign has done something real. That is a character arc with mechanical
> expression.

## Exposure Specialties

Every character receives one Exposure specialty at character creation,
reflecting the species they grew up around, worked alongside, or spent
formative years with. Exposure comes from environment, not career —
a Bureaucrat posted to a multi-species administrative hub has the same
opportunity for Exposure as a Colonist on a mixed-species frontier world.

Characters from demonstrably multi-species environments may take two
Exposure specialties at GM discretion.

Exposure specialties are tied to the **Read** and **Manipulate** skills.
They do two things: they lift the Opacity Rule for that species, and they
grant a bonus die in specific high-stakes social situations with that species.

**Available Exposure specialties:**

**Exposure: Human** — you can read human emotional states, social cues,
and body language accurately. You know what a human looks like when they
are lying, when they are frightened, and when they have made a decision
they haven't announced yet. Required for non-human PCs to function
socially with human crew at full capacity.

**Exposure: Shekhari** — you understand the mercantile intelligence and
the loyalty transition. You know when a Shekhari is still calculating and
when they have actually committed. Harder to be manipulated by one; easier
to tell when you have genuinely earned their trust.

**Exposure: Golvhaan** — you understand that the patience is not slowness
and the curiosity is genuine. You know how to give them space to be
effective, and you can read the difference between a Golvhaan who is
content and one who is deeply unhappy about something and processing it
quietly. Bonus die on Command when coordinating with Golvhaan.

**Exposure: Vashori** — you can read their emotional state accurately
and know which topics run hot. You catch the pre-escalation signals
before the volatility fires, and you know which cooling approaches work.
Bonus die on Read and reduces the chance of accidentally triggering the
emotional volatility response.

**Exposure: Vaelkhi** — you understand choth culture, the God of Ythri,
and the hunt-patience. You know not to rush them and what constitutes
disrespect versus what is merely alien. You can read the difference
between a Vaelkhi who is planning and one who is genuinely alarmed.

**Exposure: Skharri** — you understand the honour-economy. You know what
constitutes a formal challenge, what requires a response, and how to
phrase things so the scream-and-leap instinct does not fire unnecessarily.
You can read the ear and tail positions that precede violence. You know
the difference between a Skharri who respects you and one who is
tolerating you.

**Exposure: Nakhavi** — you are practiced with translation-layer
communication and can read chromatophore affect well enough to catch
what the translation drops. You understand the distributed cognition
well enough not to mistake arm-independent behaviour for confusion. You
know what the lifespan means to them and how to be present in a
conversation with someone for whom time has a different weight.

**Exposure: Kraathi** — you understand the G'naak doctrine in its full
depth and know how to navigate mixed interactions without triggering
the frame collapse. You know what the cafeteria menu means. You know
which topics are safe in formal diplomatic contexts and which will bring
the crusade into the room regardless of setting. Bonus die on Administer
when dealing with Kraathi formal institutions.

> **Note on Vardhek, Kreeth, and Nhaveth:** Exposure to these species
> functions differently.
>
> *Vardhek* — Exposure: Vardhek is available but confers understanding
> of the Long View's logic rather than emotional legibility. Vardhek
> emotional states are readable with normal Read; what is opaque without
> Exposure is the *strategic context* behind their behaviour. Bonus die
> on Administer when dealing with Roidhunate institutions and on Read
> when trying to identify what position the Long View assigns to the
> current situation.
>
> *Kreeth* — there are no Kreeth emotional states to read. Exposure:
> Kreeth is a survival specialty: you understand their tactical
> coordination, you know how to disrupt Queen-contact, and you know
> what severed units do. It is available to characters with relevant
> Military backgrounds.
>
> *Nhaveth* — the Nhaveth handout substitutes for Exposure. A character
> who possesses that knowledge can attempt identification rolls at
> reduced difficulty. Full Exposure: Nhaveth does not exist as a
> standard option because the species' true nature is not widely known.

## Archetype Template

For reference when adding new archetypes:

```
## [Background] / [Expertise]

> *One-line essence*

**Formation:** One paragraph. Who is this person and what shaped them.
Roleplay texture, not biography. What do they notice that others don't?
What do they assume that others don't?

**Career skills:** Three skills, each bumped one step at character creation.

**Specialties:** Two or three specialties from career. Plus one Exposure
specialty chosen from the list above.

**Starting gear:** Small characterful package. Not exhaustive.

**Connections:** Who this character knows and can call on. One sentence
each, specific enough to use.

> **At the table:** Roleplay prompt. How this archetype thinks under
> pressure. What they care about. What they miss.
```

## Background: Spacer

*Life defined by ships, stations, vacuum, and the Belt. You did not grow
up on a planet, or if you did, you left it early enough that it no longer
feels like home. Home is a pressurised hull and a view of stars.*

Spacers share a formation regardless of expertise: the instinctive
awareness of hull sounds, the habit of checking air supplies, the
ability to move in zero-G without thinking about it, the particular
claustrophobia of planetary surfaces that never quite goes away. They
know that space is trying to kill them and have made their peace with it.

In a multi-species setting, Spacers are among the most likely to have
genuine Exposure to non-human species — ships and stations mix species
in ways planetary surfaces often don't. A Spacer who has never worked
alongside at least one non-human crewmember is unusual.

> **Background specialty (all Spacers):** *Zero-G Movement* — no penalty
> die on Move rolls in microgravity or zero-G environments. Spacers move
> through freefall the way groundsiders walk.

---

### Spacer / Pilot

> *The ship goes where I take it. Everything else is someone else's problem.*

**Formation:** You learned to fly before you learned most other things,
and flying is still how you think about the universe — in terms of vectors,
approach angles, and what the ship will and won't do. You have opinions
about every vessel you have ever flown, strong opinions about the ones
you have been asked to fly that you considered inadequate, and a specific
affection for one ship that probably no longer exists. Pilots are not
reckless; they are precise about risk in a way that looks like recklessness
to people who don't understand what they're calculating.

**Career skills:** Operate +2, Navigate +1, Scan +1

**Specialties:**
- *Atmospheric Flight* — no penalty die when transitioning between
  space and atmosphere or operating in weather conditions
- *Combat Manoeuvring* — bonus die on Operate rolls when manoeuvring
  under fire or in close-quarters ship combat
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Flight suit (Gear 2), personal navigation tools,
ship's berth (assumed)

**Connections:** A mechanic at a specific port who keeps quiet about
what they've seen; a former commanding officer who still answers calls

> **At the table:** Pilots notice the ship first and people second.
> They have strong feelings about being a passenger. They assess every
> docking approach, every thruster firing, every unfamiliar vessel for
> what it can and can't do. Under pressure they get quieter, not louder —
> the concentration required for difficult manoeuvres does not leave
> room for noise.

---

### Spacer / Engineer

> *The ship is alive. I'm the one who keeps it that way.*

**Formation:** You understand ships the way other people understand
faces — not as collections of systems but as wholes with personalities,
tendencies, and moods. You know what a healthy drive sounds like and
what the vibration pattern at 0300 means before the diagnostic does.
Engineers develop a particular relationship with the ships they serve:
protective, occasionally frustrated, and deeply invested in a way that
looks disproportionate to people who haven't learned to depend on
machinery the way you have.

**Career skills:** Tech +2, Labor +1, Scan +1

**Specialties:**
- *Drive Systems* — bonus die on Tech rolls involving propulsion,
  jump drives, and power systems
- *Jury Rigging* — once per session, may attempt a repair that should
  require parts or time that aren't available. Roll Tech; on success,
  the fix holds for the scene. On failure, it holds for one action
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Tool kit (Gear 3), engineering coveralls, diagnostic
equipment

**Connections:** A parts dealer at a major station who occasionally
has things that aren't on the manifest; another engineer on a different
ship who shares technical problems over long-range comms

> **At the table:** Engineers notice what's wrong before anyone asks.
> They have a background awareness of system states that surfaces as
> apparently random observations — "that coolant pressure is low" in
> the middle of a conversation about something else entirely. They find
> inaction during a crisis almost physically painful; there is always
> something to fix, adjust, or prepare. They are often the last person
> to leave a failing ship.

---

### Spacer / Scout

> *I go first. That's the job.*

**Formation:** You are trained and practiced at going into unknown
situations and coming back with information. This requires a particular
combination of skills — navigating without charts, observing without
being observed, and making quick accurate assessments of situations
that may be actively trying to kill you. Scouts develop a specific
kind of self-sufficiency that is not the same as isolation: you know
how to work alone, but you also know that the information you gather
is only valuable if you get it back to someone.

**Career skills:** Scan +2, Move +1, Navigate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Hazard Recognition* — bonus die on Scan rolls to identify
  environmental dangers, ambushes, or tactical problems before
  they develop
- *Silent Running* — bonus die on Stealth rolls in unfamiliar
  environments; no penalty die for moving at speed while stealthy
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Scout suit (Gear 2, light, good sensor suite),
personal sidearm (Gear 2), survival kit

**Connections:** A cartographer at a survey organisation who trades
information; a contact in whatever organisation sent them out who
may or may not still be reliable

> **At the table:** Scouts enter every room noting exits first.
> They are quiet by habit and observant by training — they notice
> things other characters miss and have learned to trust those
> observations even when they can't immediately explain them. They
> are often the first to say "something is wrong here" and the last
> to be able to explain why clearly. They are comfortable with
> uncertainty in a way that some other archetypes are not.

---

### Spacer / Gunner

> *Point me at it. I'll handle the rest.*

**Formation:** You operate ship weapons systems and heavy personal
weapons — the kind of hardware that requires training, maintenance,
and a specific temperament to use effectively. Gunners are not soldiers;
they are technicians of applied violence at scale. They understand
weapon systems the way Engineers understand engines: as things with
personalities, optimal operating conditions, and failure modes that
need to be known before they occur. They are often quieter and more
precise than their role suggests.

**Career skills:** Shoot +2, Tech +1, Scan +1

**Specialties:**
- *Shipboard Weapons* — bonus die on Shoot rolls operating fixed
  or turreted ship weapon systems; can perform field maintenance
  on ship weapons without a Tech roll in normal conditions
- *Fire Control* — bonus die on Scan rolls when operating targeting
  systems or coordinating fire with another gunner
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Personal sidearm (Gear 3), weapon maintenance kit,
targeting visor (Gear 1)

**Connections:** An armourer at a military installation who can source
unusual munitions; a former gunnery crew member now working somewhere
relevant

> **At the table:** Gunners assess threats in terms of range, cover,
> and what their equipment can actually do about it — practical rather
> than aggressive. They are often the person who says "that won't work
> from here" when the plan involves shooting something at a distance
> the plan hasn't accounted for. They maintain their equipment obsessively
> and notice when other people's weapons are poorly kept.

## Background: Military

*You served. The details vary — whose military, what branch, what theatre,
what war — but the formation is consistent: you learned to function under
authority, under fire, and under conditions designed to break you. Some of
what that training built is still useful. Some of it you are still
working around.*

Military characters share a formation that persists long after service
ends: situational awareness as a constant background process, the habit
of assessing threat before anything else, a specific relationship to
hierarchy that is either comfortable or actively resisted depending on
what the service did to them, and the knowledge of what violence actually
looks like at close range.

In a multi-species setting, Military service is one of the most likely
sources of genuine Exposure — mixed-species units exist, and fighting
alongside someone or against them produces a specific kind of knowledge.

> **Background specialty (all Military):** *Threat Assessment* — when
> entering a new situation, the character may ask the GM one question
> about the tactical situation that will be answered honestly. This
> represents the military habit of reading environments for danger
> as automatic background processing.

---

### Military / Infantry

> *Two things matter: accomplish the mission and bring the people home.
> In that order, mostly.*

**Formation:** Ground combat, small unit tactics, and the particular
competence of someone who has learned to function effectively when
everything is going wrong. Infantry are not specialists — they are
generalists under fire, trained to do whatever the mission requires
with whatever is available. This produces a specific kind of practical
resourcefulness that is different from the improvisation of an Engineer
or a Scout: it is the resourcefulness of someone who has learned that
the plan never survives contact and built their skills accordingly.

**Career skills:** Fight +1, Shoot +2, Endure +1

**Specialties:**
- *Small Unit Tactics* — bonus die on Command rolls when leading
  a group of six or fewer in a tactical situation; bonus die on
  Scan for tactical situation awareness
- *Suppression and Cover* — bonus die on Shoot rolls for suppression
  fire; no penalty die for firing while moving
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Combat armour (Gear 2), assault weapon (Gear 3),
sidearm (Gear 2), field kit

**Connections:** Former unit members scattered across the setting —
at least one in a useful position; a veterans' network that provides
informal support and information

> **At the table:** Infantry characters assess every room for cover
> before they assess it for anything else. They are often the person
> who moves to a better position without being asked, who notices the
> exits, who sits with their back to the wall. Under pressure they
> become more focused rather than more emotional — the training kicks
> in and the noise drops out. They find extended periods of inaction
> genuinely difficult.

---

### Military / Special Operations

> *You won't see me coming. That's the point.*

**Formation:** Specialised, high-risk operations requiring skills that
go beyond standard military training: infiltration, extraction, direct
action against high-value targets, operating without support in hostile
territory. Special Operations characters are self-sufficient by
necessity and precise by training — they do not use more force than
the mission requires, because excess attracts attention. They have
done things they do not discuss and developed capabilities that are
not on any official record.

**Career skills:** Stealth +2, Fight +1, Scan +1

**Specialties:**
- *Infiltration* — bonus die on Stealth in actively secured
  environments; can assess security systems through observation
  before engaging them (Scan roll, no penalty for unfamiliar systems)
- *Direct Action* — bonus die on Fight in close quarters; when
  initiating combat from stealth, first attack automatically
  achieves surprise regardless of other factors
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Light stealth suit (Gear 3), compact sidearm (Gear 2),
multi-tool with classified functions, no identifying documentation

**Connections:** A handler or former handler whose loyalty is uncertain;
a network of safe contacts in specific locations that may or may not
still be active

> **At the table:** Special Operations characters are quiet in a
> specific way — they observe before they engage, they position
> before they act, and they do not telegraph their capabilities.
> They find it professionally uncomfortable to walk into situations
> without having assessed them first. They have a specific relationship
> to authority: they have operated with enormous autonomy and they
> remember it. Taking orders from someone they don't respect is
> genuinely difficult.

---

### Military / Naval

> *The ship is the weapon. The crew is the weapon. My job is both.*

**Formation:** Officers and senior ratings of spacecraft military
operations — not the pilot or the gunner specifically, but the person
responsible for the whole vessel functioning as a combat system. Naval
characters understand command, tactical coordination, and the specific
challenge of making decisions at range where communication is delayed
and outcomes are binary. They have a formal relationship to hierarchy
that most other Military subtypes have modified or discarded; they
believe in chain of command because they have seen what happens without it.

**Career skills:** Command +2, Operate +1, Navigate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Fleet Tactics* — bonus die on Navigate and Scan rolls in multi-
  vessel situations; can coordinate the actions of up to three
  vessels as a single Command action
- *Damage Control* — bonus die on Command rolls when directing crew
  response to ship damage or emergency; crew under their command
  do not take morale penalties from the first critical system failure
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Officer's sidearm (Gear 2), formal uniform (opens
doors in military contexts), encrypted comms device

**Connections:** Former subordinates now serving elsewhere; a flag
officer who remembers their service favourably; a contact in naval
intelligence

> **At the table:** Naval characters think in terms of systems and
> teams rather than individual action. They notice when the crew is
> not functioning as a unit before any individual crew member does.
> They are comfortable giving orders and uncomfortable taking them
> from people whose competence they have not verified. They have a
> specific horror of situations that are out of control — not fear,
> but a professional revulsion at chaos that other archetypes may
> find excessive.

---

### Military / Intelligence

> *Information is the only weapon that wins wars before they start.*

**Formation:** Military intelligence: gathering, analysis, and acting
on information in ways that are not always visible. Intelligence
characters are trained to observe, to lie convincingly, to build
and maintain cover identities, and to assess whether the information
they are receiving is accurate, incomplete, or deliberately misleading.
They have a specific relationship to truth — they know what it is
worth and what it costs, and they are not sentimental about either.

**Career skills:** Read +2, Manipulate +1, Administer +1

**Specialties:**
- *Cover Identity* — can establish and maintain a false identity
  that will withstand casual scrutiny; bonus die on Manipulate
  rolls while operating under cover
- *Source Assessment* — bonus die on Read rolls when evaluating
  whether information or a person is reliable; can identify
  deliberate deception with a Read roll even when the deception
  is professionally executed
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** False documentation (quality varies), recording
device (concealed), personal sidearm (Gear 2, unregistered)

**Connections:** A current or former handler with their own agenda;
a source in an unexpected location who provides information for
reasons that have never been fully explained

> **At the table:** Intelligence characters listen more than they
> speak, and when they speak they choose their words with visible
> precision. They are often the person who asks the question no
> one else thought to ask, or who notices that someone answered
> a different question from the one they were asked. They find it
> genuinely difficult to trust, not because they are paranoid but
> because their training has given them an accurate picture of how
> often people lie and why.

## Background: Colonist

*You are from somewhere that was still being built. The frontier does
not have to be wilderness — it can be a new habitat, a recently settled
moon, a world that humanity or another species is still figuring out —
but it shares the quality of being a place where the infrastructure
has not caught up with the need, and where people improvise constantly.*

Colonists share a formation of practical self-sufficiency: you fix
things because there is no one else to fix them, you know your neighbours
because isolation makes community necessary, and you have a relationship
to your environment — whatever that environment is — that more settled
people often lack. You know what your world smells like, what its seasons
do, and what the particular dangers of your specific corner of the
galaxy are.

The species composition of a colony is one of the most formative facts
about a Colonist character. A human who grew up on a world with a
significant Vashori population has Exposure: Vashori as part of their
formation. A Golvhaan who grew up in a mixed-species habitat has
human Exposure as a lived reality. A colony world with Skharri settlers
and human settlers produces humans who understand the honour-economy
from childhood — and Skharri who have learned that humans are not
simply prey.

> **Background specialty (all Colonists):** *Environmental Adaptation* —
> the character has deep familiarity with one specific environment type
> (chosen at character creation: high-G, low-G, hostile atmosphere,
> extreme temperature, aquatic-adjacent, etc.). No penalty die for
> physical or Wits rolls in that environment type.

---

### Colonist / Frontier

> *Out here you solve your own problems. There is no one else.*

**Formation:** The frontier colonist's expertise is practical
competence across a wide range: keeping equipment running, surviving
when it doesn't, growing or finding food, building what's needed
with what's available. This is not glamorous and not specialised —
it is the deep generalism of someone whose situation has demanded
they be competent at everything because incompetence at anything
has consequences. Frontier colonists often find settled populations
faintly absurd: all these people, and no one knows how to fix
anything?

**Career skills:** Tech +1, Endure +1, Labor +1

**Specialties:**
- *Field Improvisation* — bonus die on Tech rolls using improvised
  or inappropriate materials; can attempt repairs without proper
  tools at no additional penalty
- *Wilderness Survival* — bonus die on Endure rolls in hostile
  environments; can find food and water in environments that
  support life without a roll under normal conditions
- One Exposure specialty of choice (reflecting colony species mix)

**Starting gear:** Multi-tool (Gear 2), survival kit (comprehensive),
practical clothing rated for the home environment, personal weapon
appropriate to the home world (Gear 2)

**Connections:** The colony itself — people who will help if you
come back and ask; a local official who owes a favour

> **At the table:** Frontier colonists notice infrastructure.
> They assess every new environment for its practical qualities:
> what grows here, what the atmosphere is doing, where the water
> is, what could go wrong and how quickly. They find urban and
> station environments simultaneously impressive and wasteful.
> They are often the most practically capable person in the room
> and the least likely to mention it.

---

### Colonist / Technical

> *Someone has to keep the lights on. I'm that someone.*

**Formation:** The technical specialist of a colony — the person
responsible for the systems that keep the settlement viable. Water
reclamation, atmospheric processing, power generation, communications,
medical equipment maintenance. Technical colonists are not generalists
like their Frontier counterparts; they have deep expertise in specific
systems, usually the systems their colony most critically depended on.
They also have the colonist's underlying self-sufficiency — a technical
specialist who cannot improvise does not survive long on the frontier.

**Career skills:** Tech +2, Science +1, Labor +1

**Specialties:**
- *Life Support Systems* — bonus die on Tech rolls involving
  atmospheric, water, and power systems; can diagnose life support
  failures faster than standard (one round instead of one scene)
- *Systems Integration* — bonus die on Tech rolls connecting or
  interfacing disparate systems, including alien technology; reduced
  penalty die when working with unfamiliar system architectures
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Specialised tool kit (Gear 3), diagnostic scanner,
technical manuals for home colony systems

**Connections:** A supplier network for colony technical components;
a colleague at another colony facing similar problems

> **At the table:** Technical colonists see every station and ship
> as a collection of systems with interdependencies, failure modes,
> and maintenance schedules. They notice when things are poorly
> maintained before anything has failed. They find it very difficult
> to leave a failing system without attempting to fix it, even when
> leaving is the correct tactical decision.

---

### Colonist / Medical

> *They sent one doctor for four hundred people. That was me.*

**Formation:** Colonial medicine is not the medicine of a well-equipped
hospital. It is the medicine of limited supplies, improvised equipment,
and conditions that are not in any textbook because no one has been
here long enough to write one. Colonial medical practitioners are
generalists by necessity — they handle everything from childbirth to
trauma to alien-contact diseases to the psychological consequences of
isolation. They are also, quietly, among the most important people
in any community they work in, and they know it.

**Career skills:** Science +2, Read +1, Manipulate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Field Medicine* — bonus die on Science rolls for medical
  treatment without proper facilities; can treat conditions
  under fire or in hostile environments without penalty die
- *Xenomedicine* — has working knowledge of the medical needs
  of species present in their home colony; bonus die on Science
  rolls treating those species; no penalty die for unfamiliar
  alien physiology (basic treatment only)
- One Exposure specialty of choice (reflecting colony species mix)

**Starting gear:** Medical kit (Gear 3, comprehensive for field use),
pharmacological supplies, diagnostic equipment

**Connections:** A medical supply network; a colleague at a proper
medical facility who can be contacted for consultation

> **At the table:** Colonial medics notice health. They observe
> people the way Scouts observe environments — assessing, cataloguing,
> identifying what is wrong before anyone mentions it. They have a
> specific horror of preventable death and a specific competence
> around inevitable death that other archetypes sometimes find
> unsettling. They ask direct questions about symptoms that social
> convention might suggest are too personal.

---

### Colonist / Administrator

> *Forty people, three factions, one water supply, and no rulebook.
> I wrote the rulebook.*

**Formation:** The person who made the colony function as a society
rather than just a collection of survivors. Colonial administrators
deal with resource allocation, conflict resolution, external relations
(with the sponsoring organisation, with neighbouring settlements, with
whatever species share the world), and the unglamorous work of making
decisions that affect everyone with information that is always
incomplete. They have learned that there is no perfect decision,
only better and worse ones made under time pressure.

**Career skills:** Administer +2, Command +1, Read +1

**Specialties:**
- *Resource Management* — bonus die on Administer rolls involving
  allocation, logistics, or supply chain; can identify resource
  shortfalls before they become critical with a single roll
- *Conflict Mediation* — bonus die on Manipulate and Read rolls
  when mediating between parties in conflict; can de-escalate
  a situation that has not yet turned violent with a Manipulate
  roll even without leverage
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Personal comms (encrypted), colony documentation
and records (partial, relevant), sidearm (Gear 1, administrative
rather than combat)

**Connections:** The sponsoring organisation — a contact who can
be reached but whose interests may not align; neighbouring settlement
contacts; a legal adviser in the nearest proper jurisdiction

> **At the table:** Colonial administrators have an instinct for
> group dynamics and resource flow. They notice when a group is
> making a decision badly — too fast, without the right information,
> with the wrong people in the room — and they find it genuinely
> difficult not to intervene. They are often the person who asks
> "what happens after this?" when everyone else is focused on the
> immediate problem.

## Background: Citizen

*You are from somewhere established — a world, a station, a habitat
that has been civilised long enough to have stopped thinking about it.
You did not grow up in a crisis. You grew up in a place with history,
infrastructure, and the assumption that tomorrow would be roughly like
today.*

This is not a background of disadvantage. Citizens come from
environments that invest in education, that have institutions, that
have the accumulated knowledge of generations. What they often lack
is the specific self-sufficiency of Colonists or the specific intensity
of Military or Spacer formation — they have not been tested in the
same way, and they know it.

Citizens are the most common background for non-human PCs from
established species. A Vashori scholar, a Golvhaan historian, a
Shekhari who grew up in a port city — these are Citizens. Their
species' civilisation has had time to produce people who are shaped
by something other than survival or service.

> **Background specialty (all Citizens):** *Institutional Knowledge* —
> bonus die on Administer rolls when dealing with the institutions
> of their home world or species. They know how things work where
> they come from, who actually has authority, and which rules are
> enforced and which are not.

---

### Citizen / Academic

> *The question is more important than the answer. The answer just
> tells you what to ask next.*

**Formation:** The scholar, researcher, or specialist whose expertise
was developed in an institutional context — a university, a research
organisation, a species' knowledge-preservation body. Academic citizens
have deep knowledge in specific fields and the research skills to
extend that knowledge into new areas. They also have the particular
blind spots of institutional formation: they are very good at the
questions their field has defined and sometimes poor at the questions
it hasn't.

**Career skills:** Science +2, Administer +1, Read +1

**Specialties:**
- *Research Methodology* — bonus die on Science rolls when
  investigating an unknown phenomenon with time to apply systematic
  method; can identify what is known versus unknown about a situation
  faster than other characters
- *Specialist Knowledge* — deep expertise in one specific field
  (chosen at character creation: xenobiology, history, physics,
  linguistics, archaeology, etc.); bonus die on all Science rolls
  within that field; can attempt rolls that would be unavailable
  to non-specialists
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Research equipment relevant to specialty, personal
library (digital, comprehensive within field), academic credentials

**Connections:** A network of colleagues in the field; an institution
that may want their findings; a rival whose work overlaps inconveniently

> **At the table:** Academics notice when something doesn't fit
> the established model. They ask questions that other characters
> find tangential and that frequently turn out to be central. They
> find the absence of information as interesting as its presence.
> They are sometimes poor at recognising when the time for research
> has ended and the time for action has begun.

---

### Citizen / Technical

> *I didn't build it. But I understand it completely, which is almost
> the same thing.*

**Formation:** The technical specialist of an established world or
station — someone whose expertise was developed in a context with
resources, training, and the accumulated knowledge of a mature
technical tradition. Citizen technicians have deeper formal training
than their Colonist counterparts and narrower practical experience.
They know more about how things should work and sometimes less about
what to do when they don't.

**Career skills:** Tech +2, Science +1, Operate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Advanced Systems* — bonus die on Tech rolls involving high-
  technology equipment, prototype systems, or alien technology
  that has been formally studied; can read technical documentation
  in unfamiliar system architectures with a Science roll
- *Precision Engineering* — bonus die on Tech rolls requiring
  fine work; can perform technical tasks in half the normal time
  at no penalty when conditions are adequate
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** High-quality tool kit (Gear 3), technical
credentials, specialised diagnostic equipment

**Connections:** A former employer or institution; a colleague
working on a related problem; a supplier of high-end technical
components

> **At the table:** Citizen technicians notice quality. They
> assess equipment automatically — whether it is well-maintained,
> what generation it is, whether it has been modified and how well.
> They find poorly maintained equipment offensive in a mild but
> persistent way. They are often the most formally educated person
> in the room and sometimes the least practically flexible.

---

### Citizen / Medical

> *Medicine is applied science. The application is the hard part.*

**Formation:** A medical practitioner from an established medical
system — trained properly, with access to equipment and colleagues
and the accumulated knowledge of a mature medical tradition. Citizen
medics know what good medicine looks like and how to practise it.
They are also shaped by their institutional context: they have
expectations about facilities, supplies, and conditions that field
medicine constantly violates.

**Career skills:** Science +2, Read +1, Empathy — wait, Empathy
is not a skill. Science +2, Read +1, Manipulate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Clinical Medicine* — bonus die on Science rolls for medical
  treatment when adequate facilities are available; can diagnose
  conditions that field medics would miss with proper equipment
- *Xenomedicine* — formal training in the medical needs of species
  encountered in their practice; bonus die on Science rolls treating
  those species; can attempt treatment of species outside their
  training at standard difficulty (field medics take a penalty die)
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Medical kit (Gear 3, high quality), pharmacological
supplies (comprehensive), diagnostic equipment (advanced)

**Connections:** A hospital or medical institution; a pharmaceutical
supplier; a colleague in a relevant specialty

> **At the table:** Citizen medics notice health in the same way
> Colonial medics do, but they also notice the absence of adequate
> facilities in a way that Colonial medics have long since accepted.
> They find field conditions frustrating rather than simply limiting.
> They are often better than Colonial medics at conditions they have
> seen before and worse at conditions they haven't.

---

### Citizen / Law and Administration

> *The rules exist for reasons. Knowing the reasons tells you when
> the rules are wrong.*

**Formation:** Someone who worked within legal or administrative
systems — as a lawyer, a government official, a corporate administrator,
a judicial functionary. They understand how institutions make decisions,
where the actual authority lies (which is rarely where the org chart
says it is), and how to navigate systems that are designed to be
navigated by people who know how they work. They are also shaped by
the particular ethics of institutional work: the gap between what
the rules say and what is right is a gap they have spent time in.

**Career skills:** Administer +2, Manipulate +1, Read +1

**Specialties:**
- *Legal Framework* — bonus die on Administer rolls involving
  legal processes, contracts, or jurisdictional questions; can
  identify the legal exposure of a situation and the options
  available within the law
- *Institutional Navigation* — bonus die on Manipulate rolls
  when working within or around bureaucratic systems; knows
  which procedures can be expedited, which officials have
  discretion, and which rules are genuinely enforced
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Legal credentials, personal comms, formal clothing
appropriate to institutional contexts, documentation kit

**Connections:** A judge or official who owes a professional
courtesy; a colleague in private practice; a contact in a
regulatory body

> **At the table:** Law and Administration characters notice
> authority — who actually has it, who thinks they have it,
> and the gap between the two. They find extralegal situations
> interesting rather than uncomfortable: knowing the law well
> includes knowing where it doesn't reach. They ask about
> jurisdiction in situations where other characters are asking
> about more immediate problems, and they are sometimes right
> that jurisdiction matters more.

## Background: Bureaucrat

*You worked inside large organisations — governments, corporations,
interstellar bodies, species-level administrative structures. You
understand how institutions actually function, which is quite different
from how they are supposed to function, and you have the specific
competences of someone who has navigated complexity from the inside.*

Bureaucrats are not paper-pushers. They are people who understand
power as it is actually exercised: through information flow, resource
allocation, personnel decisions, and the management of competing
interests. They know that the person with the title is frequently
not the person with the authority, and they know how to find the
person with the authority.

In a multi-species setting, senior Bureaucrats are among the most
likely to have formal Exposure to multiple species through their
institutional work — interstellar bodies require it.

> **Background specialty (all Bureaucrats):** *Organisational Reading* —
> bonus die on Administer rolls when assessing an unfamiliar
> organisation's structure, identifying who actually has authority,
> or understanding how a decision was made. Bureaucrats can read
> institutions the way Scouts read terrain.

---

### Bureaucrat / Government

> *The government is not the state. The state is not the law. I know
> the difference.*

**Formation:** Someone who worked within government — not necessarily
senior, but experienced enough to understand how policy becomes
action, how decisions are made and unmade, and where the pressure
points are. Government bureaucrats have a specific relationship to
power: they have been close to it without having it, which produces
either cynicism or sophistication, and sometimes both.

**Career skills:** Administer +2, Read +1, Command +1

**Specialties:**
- *Policy and Process* — bonus die on Administer rolls involving
  governmental processes, interstellar treaties, or political
  decision-making; understands the legislative and executive
  structures of their home government in detail
- *Political Intelligence* — bonus die on Read rolls when assessing
  the political motivations behind a decision or action; can identify
  what a political actor actually wants versus what they say they want
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Government credentials (current or former),
encrypted communications device, formal clothing, access to
classified databases (limited, depending on current status)

**Connections:** A current official who prefers their relationship
with this character remain informal; a former colleague now in
a different position; a journalist who pays for information

> **At the table:** Government bureaucrats see politics in
> everything. They ask who benefits, who loses, and who made
> the decision to frame every situation they encounter. They
> find purely tactical thinking incomplete — there is always
> a political dimension that other characters are missing.
> They are often right, and sometimes this is useful, and
> sometimes this is just exhausting.

---

### Bureaucrat / Corporate

> *It is not personal. It is never personal. That's what makes it
> so effective.*

**Formation:** Someone who worked within corporate structures —
trading companies, manufacturing concerns, resource extraction
operations, interstellar commercial enterprises. Corporate
bureaucrats understand profit motive as the actual driver of
institutional behaviour in a way that government bureaucrats
often don't, and they understand that corporate loyalty is
a transactional relationship that runs in both directions until
it doesn't.

**Career skills:** Administer +2, Manipulate +1, Scan +1

**Specialties:**
- *Commercial Intelligence* — bonus die on Administer rolls
  involving commercial law, contracts, trade regulations, and
  corporate structure; can assess the financial health and actual
  priorities of a commercial entity with an Administer roll
- *Negotiation* — bonus die on Manipulate rolls in formal
  negotiation contexts; understands the difference between
  stated positions and actual interests and can exploit the gap
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Corporate credentials (current or former),
encrypted comms, formal clothing, commercial database access

**Connections:** A former employer with unresolved business;
a competitor who is occasionally useful; a financial institution
contact

> **At the table:** Corporate bureaucrats assess situations
> for their commercial implications with a speed other characters
> find slightly unsettling. They notice who is paying for what
> and what that means. They are often the person who points out
> that the apparently altruistic offer has a business model behind
> it that the other characters haven't found yet. They are not
> cynical — they are accurate.

---

### Bureaucrat / Intelligence Agency

> *I know things I cannot tell you. Some of them are about you.*

**Formation:** Civilian intelligence — not military intelligence,
but the agencies that gather information on behalf of governments,
corporations, or other large actors through means that are not
military. Agency bureaucrats understand information as a commodity:
how it is gathered, how it is assessed, how it is used, and how
it is used against you. They have a specific relationship to
truth that is more complicated than most people's.

**Career skills:** Read +2, Administer +1, Manipulate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Information Analysis* — bonus die on Read and Scan rolls
  when assessing information sources, identifying disinformation,
  or synthesising fragmentary intelligence into a coherent picture
- *Asset Management* — bonus die on Manipulate rolls when
  recruiting, maintaining, or directing human sources; understands
  the psychology of why people provide information and how to
  exploit it ethically or otherwise
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** False documentation (high quality), recording
equipment (concealed, high quality), personal sidearm (Gear 2,
unregistered), no official credentials

**Connections:** A handler whose agenda is not entirely clear;
a source in an unexpected location; a former asset who now
knows too much

> **At the table:** Intelligence agency characters listen
> before they speak and remember everything. They find other
> characters' willingness to volunteer information about themselves
> both useful and faintly alarming. They are often the person
> who says "we don't know enough yet" when the group is preparing
> to act, and sometimes they are right, and sometimes waiting
> is the wrong call and they know it.

---

### Bureaucrat / Diplomatic Corps

> *My job is to prevent the thing that happens when my job fails.*

**Formation:** Someone who worked in formal diplomatic contexts —
representing one species, government, or faction to another, managing
relationships across the full range from routine trade negotiations
to crisis management at the edge of open conflict. Diplomatic corps
characters are trained in a specific set of social skills that are
different from the street-level Manipulate of a trader or spy: they
understand protocol, they know how to use formal structures as tools,
and they have experience with the particular challenge of
representing interests that are not their own.

**Career skills:** Manipulate +2, Administer +1, Read +1

**Specialties:**
- *Protocol* — bonus die on Manipulate rolls in formal diplomatic
  contexts; knows the correct form for interactions with most
  major species and governments; no penalty die for unfamiliar
  formal contexts (can improvise correctly)
- *Crisis Management* — bonus die on Manipulate and Command rolls
  when managing a situation that is actively deteriorating; can
  buy time in a crisis with words when other options have failed
- One Exposure specialty of choice (Diplomatic Corps characters
  may take two Exposure specialties reflecting their posting history)

**Starting gear:** Diplomatic credentials (current or former),
formal clothing (multiple, species-appropriate), encrypted comms,
translation technology (high quality)

**Connections:** A current posting or former posting with
ongoing relationships; a colleague in another delegation; a
contact in the foreign ministry of a relevant species

> **At the table:** Diplomatic Corps characters notice when
> communication is failing before anyone else does. They catch
> the moment when a negotiation changes direction, when someone
> has said something that landed wrong, when a situation that
> seemed stable has shifted. They find violence representing
> a failure — not a moral failure necessarily, but a professional
> one — and they will work very hard to avoid it. Sometimes
> this is the right call. Sometimes the situation called for
> violence two scenes ago.

## Background: Merchant

*Trade. The movement of goods, information, and value across the
distances between worlds and species. You are part of the commercial
infrastructure that makes interstellar civilisation function — not
its government, not its military, not its institutions, but the
network of exchange that underlies all of those.*

Merchants come in many forms — from the captain-owner of a battered
free trader to the senior factor of a major commercial house — but
they share a formation: comfort with uncertainty, a specific
relationship to risk and reward, and the practical competence of
people who have to make things work in places far from any support
structure. They also, characteristically, know people. Merchants
run on relationships.

In a multi-species setting, Merchants are among the most likely to
have broad Exposure simply because trade does not respect species
boundaries. A merchant who has never dealt across species lines
is operating in a very limited market.

> **Background specialty (all Merchants):** *Market Reading* —
> bonus die on Administer rolls when assessing the commercial
> value of goods, services, or information; can identify
> arbitrage opportunities (price differences between markets)
> with a single roll.

---

### Merchant / Free Trader

> *My ship, my cargo, my route, my decision. That's the whole deal.*

**Formation:** The independent operator — captain, owner, and
usually crew of a vessel that goes where the profit is and stays
independent because independence is the point. Free traders have
the Spacer's comfort with ships and vacuum combined with the
Merchant's commercial instincts and a specific quality that neither
background alone produces: the ability to make every decision
themselves, in full knowledge that every decision is their
responsibility. They are often the most self-sufficient characters
in the setting and the most resistant to taking orders.

**Career skills:** Operate +1, Administer +1, Manipulate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Route Knowledge* — bonus die on Navigate rolls in known
  regions; has detailed knowledge of a specific region of space
  including unofficial routes, hazards, and the people who
  operate there
- *Deal Making* — bonus die on Manipulate rolls in commercial
  negotiations; can identify the actual bottom line of a
  negotiating counterpart with a Read roll
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Ship (assumed, with debt attached), personal
sidearm (Gear 2), trading contacts database, cargo manifest tool

**Connections:** A banker or financier holding paper on the ship;
regular customers at several ports; a competitor who is sometimes
a colleague

> **At the table:** Free traders think about their ship constantly.
> It is their home, their livelihood, and their freedom, and
> threats to it are threats to everything. They assess every
> situation for its commercial angle without necessarily acting
> on it — it is simply how they see the world. They find
> employers and authorities tolerable when the pay is right
> and irritating when it isn't.

---

### Merchant / Factor

> *I represent interests. Which interests depends on the day.*

**Formation:** The commercial agent — someone who acts on behalf
of others in commercial contexts, negotiating, sourcing, placing,
and managing transactions across multiple parties. Factors are
the Van Rijn tradition: deep commercial knowledge, broad relationship
networks, and the specific competence of someone who makes their
living knowing what everyone wants and connecting them to it. They
are not tied to a single employer or a single ship; they operate
in the space between parties.

**Career skills:** Administer +2, Manipulate +1, Read +1

**Specialties:**
- *Commercial Network* — once per session, can contact a
  source anywhere in known space for commercial information,
  goods availability, or market conditions; the contact may
  want something in return
- *Contract Law* — bonus die on Administer rolls involving
  commercial contracts, trade agreements, or commercial disputes;
  can identify problematic contract terms that other characters
  would miss
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Commercial credentials, encrypted comms,
formal and informal clothing, letter of credit (limited)

**Connections:** A major commercial house that uses their
services; a rival factor; contacts at multiple ports across
their operating region

> **At the table:** Factors are always working. They notice
> commercial opportunity the way Scouts notice tactical
> opportunity — automatically, as background processing.
> They know who everyone is and what they want, or they
> find out. They are often the person who can get the thing
> the group needs, for a price that is negotiable, through
> channels that are not entirely official.

---

### Merchant / Broker

> *Information is cargo. Cargo is information. Same business.*

**Formation:** The information merchant — someone who trades
in intelligence, contacts, and knowledge as readily as in
physical goods. Brokers know things and know people who know
things, and they have built a career on the value of that
network. They are distinct from intelligence agency operatives
in that their motivations are commercial rather than political,
and their methods are relationship-based rather than covert —
though the line between a well-connected broker and a
well-placed intelligence asset is not always clear.

**Career skills:** Read +2, Administer +1, Scan +1

**Specialties:**
- *Information Network* — bonus die on Read and Scan rolls
  when gathering information through contacts; once per session,
  can identify a contact who knows something relevant to the
  current situation
- *Discretion* — bonus die on Manipulate rolls when assuring
  parties that information will be handled confidentially;
  has a professional reputation for not selling the same
  information twice (whether or not this is true)
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Encrypted comms (high quality), contact
database (extensive), recording equipment, personal sidearm
(Gear 1, rarely used)

**Connections:** Three contacts in different sectors who
provide information regularly; a buyer for sensitive
information whose identity is protected

> **At the table:** Brokers are always listening. They find
> other characters' information security habits interesting
> and often inadequate. They know things about the people
> they work with that those people have not told them directly,
> and they are careful about when and whether to reveal this.
> They find violence crude — not morally, but commercially.
> Violence is bad for the information business.

---

### Merchant / Smuggler

> *The law says what's legal. The market says what's needed.
> I serve the market.*

**Formation:** The independent operator who moves goods that
someone, somewhere, has decided should not be moved — whether
that's weapons, contraband, controlled technology, refugees,
or information that someone powerful wants suppressed.
Smugglers have the Free Trader's self-sufficiency and the
Broker's relationship network, plus a specific set of skills
that come from operating in the gap between what is permitted
and what is needed. They are not criminals in any meaningful
moral sense — the laws they break vary wildly in legitimacy
— but they operate outside official frameworks and have
developed the skills that requires.

**Career skills:** Operate +1, Stealth +1, Manipulate +1

**Specialties:**
- *Hidden Cargo* — bonus die on Stealth rolls to conceal
  goods or people; knows the standard search patterns of
  most major port authorities and how to design concealment
  around them
- *Clean Passage* — bonus die on Manipulate rolls when
  dealing with port authorities, customs officials, and
  checkpoints; can identify the right approach to a given
  official (bribable, bypassable, or must-be-avoided) with
  a Read roll
- One Exposure specialty of choice

**Starting gear:** Modified ship (assumed, with concealed
compartments), false documentation (Gear 2), personal sidearm
(Gear 2), bribery fund (modest)

**Connections:** A port official who looks the other way;
a regular customer who needs things moved; a legitimate
merchant who uses their services and pretends not to

> **At the table:** Smugglers notice security. They assess
> every port, every checkpoint, every official for its
> implications for their operation — past, present, or
> future. They are often the most practically useful person
> in the group when things go wrong with authorities and
> the most professionally uncomfortable when things go right
> with them. They find bureaucratic legitimacy mildly amusing
> and occasionally useful as cover.

## Character Creation Summary

1. Choose your **species** (see Species chapter)
2. Choose your **background** (Spacer / Military / Colonist /
   Citizen / Bureaucrat / Merchant)
3. Choose your **expertise** within that background
4. Apply career skill bumps from your expertise
5. Choose your **Exposure specialty** from the list (Diplomatic
   Corps may choose two)
6. Note your **starting gear** and **connections**
7. Apply your species' attribute adjustment and biological
   specialties
8. Distribute base attribute points (see Character Creation
   chapter)
9. Name your character and establish their specific history
   within the background framework

## Adding New Archetypes

When a player requests a background or expertise not covered here,
use this checklist:

- Does it fit an existing background, or does it need a new one?
- What three skills does this career train systematically?
- What two or three specialties distinguish this expertise from
  adjacent ones?
- What starting gear characterises this career?
- What connections does this career naturally produce?
- What does this character notice that others don't?
  (The roleplay prompt test — if you can't answer this,
  the archetype isn't distinct enough yet)

New expertises within existing backgrounds can be added at any
time. New backgrounds should be rare — the six here cover the
realistic spread of how people end up in this kind of work.
