# Conditions

## Overview

Conditions represent the accumulated cost of violence, environmental
stress, psychological pressure, and sustained hardship. They are the
mechanical expression of the fact that the universe is trying to kill
you and sometimes partially succeeds.

Conditions work on two parallel tracks: **physical** and
**psychological**. Each track has its own capacity, its own named
conditions, and its own recovery requirements. Both tracks use the
same underlying mechanic — each condition imposes a penalty die on
relevant rolls — but they accumulate from different sources and
resolve in different ways.

A character is **incapacitated** when either track reaches its
capacity. Physical incapacitation means the body has given out.
Psychological incapacitation means the mind has. Both are serious.
Neither is automatically fatal.

---

## The Two Tracks

### Physical Track

**Hit Capacity** = Strength + Endure skill

A character with Strength 3 and Endure 2 has a Hit Capacity of 5.
They can sustain five physical conditions before incapacitation.

Physical conditions come from: combat wounds, vacuum exposure,
environmental extremes, toxic atmospheres, physical exhaustion,
disease, and injury from accident or structural failure.

### Psychological Track

**Stress Capacity** = Wits + Empathy (average, rounded up)

A character with Wits 3 and Empathy 2 has a Stress Capacity of 3
(average 2.5, rounded up). They can sustain three psychological
conditions before breaking.

Psychological conditions come from: sustained fear, witnessing
atrocity, isolation, the death of crewmates, moral injury, the
specific horrors of the deep dark between stars, and the
accumulated weight of a life lived at the edge.

### Tracking Conditions

Conditions are recorded on the character sheet as they accumulate.
Each named condition occupies one slot on the relevant track.
When a track is full, the character is incapacitated on that track.

Record the specific condition, not just a tick. *Hurt (gunshot,
left shoulder)* is more useful at the table than a blank box
with a mark in it. The specific nature of the condition informs
how it was treated, how it affects the character's behaviour,
and what recovery looks like.

---

## Physical Conditions

Physical conditions are acquired in order of severity — Winded
before Hurt, Hurt before Wounded, Wounded before Critical —
unless a single hit is severe enough to skip levels. A devastating
hit (four or more sixes in combat) may impose Wounded directly.
The attacker or the GM specifies when a condition skips levels.

### Winded

*Minor physical stress. The body is protesting but functional.*

**Sources:** A glancing hit, brief vacuum exposure, pushing
physical limits without adequate rest, minor chemical exposure,
a fall that did not break anything.

**Effect:** Penalty die on **Endure** and **Labor** rolls.

**Recovery:** Rest. Given an hour of genuine rest with no
physical demands, Winded clears automatically. No medical
attention required.

> **Roleplay note:** Winded is the condition that accumulates
> quietly. Characters who push themselves repeatedly — multiple
> pushed rolls in a session, sustained physical effort, a long
> EVA — may find themselves Winded before anything dramatic
> has happened. It is the setting's way of saying the body
> keeps score.

---

### Hurt

*Genuine injury. Something is damaged that will not simply
rest away.*

**Sources:** A solid combat hit, significant vacuum exposure,
a bad fall, toxic atmosphere without adequate protection,
extended operation in a hostile environment without recovery.

**Effect:** Penalty die on all **physical skill rolls**
(Fight, Endure, Labor, Shoot, Stealth, Move).

**Recovery:** Rest and basic medical treatment. A character
with the Medic specialty (Science) can downgrade Hurt to
Winded with a successful Science roll and appropriate
supplies. Without medical attention, Hurt requires a full
night's rest to clear. With treatment, it clears after
a few hours.

> **Roleplay note:** Hurt is visible. Other characters notice.
> A Hurt character moves differently, reaches for things
> more carefully, flinches. The specific nature of the injury
> — the location, the cause — should inform how the character
> carries themselves for the rest of the session.

---

### Wounded

*Serious injury. Without treatment, this gets worse.*

**Sources:** A strong combat hit, serious vacuum exposure,
structural injury (caught in a hull breach, crushed by
falling equipment), severe toxic or thermal exposure.

**Effect:** Penalty die on **all rolls** without exception.

**Special:** A Wounded character who does not receive
medical attention within a scene (approximately one hour
of game time) acquires an additional Hurt condition as
the wound worsens. A character who is already at capacity
when this happens is in immediate danger.

**Recovery:** Requires proper medical treatment — a Science
roll at no penalty with adequate supplies, or a field
treatment that downgrades to Hurt with a penalty die on
the roll. Full recovery from Wounded requires rest after
treatment; the condition clears after a long rest (six
or more hours) following successful treatment.

> **Roleplay note:** A Wounded character is in real trouble
> and should feel it. This is the condition that forces
> decisions — do we press on or do we address this now?
> The one-hour clock before worsening is a genuine pressure.
> Use it to create urgency rather than ignoring it for
> narrative convenience.

---

### Critical

*Life-threatening. Immediate intervention required.*

**Sources:** A devastating combat hit, extended vacuum
exposure without suit, catastrophic environmental failure,
multiple accumulated wounds without treatment.

**Effect:** Penalty die on **all rolls**. Additionally,
the character must make an **Endure roll at the start
of each scene** while Critical. On a failure, they
acquire another Hurt condition. If this would exceed
their Hit Capacity, they are dying.

**Dying:** A character who exceeds their Hit Capacity
while Critical is dying. They have a number of rounds
equal to their Strength before death unless stabilised.
Stabilisation requires a successful Science roll (Medic
specialty removes the penalty die) against difficulty 2
— two or more sixes needed.

**Recovery:** Requires significant medical intervention —
proper facilities, time, and a Science roll at difficulty 2.
On success, Critical downgrades to Wounded. Full recovery
from Critical takes days of rest and follow-up treatment.
Some Critical wounds leave permanent effects at the GM's
discretion — scars, reduced function, trauma that does
not entirely clear.

> **Roleplay note:** Critical is the condition that changes
> the session. When a character goes Critical, everything
> else stops mattering as much. The crew's response — who
> goes to them, who keeps fighting, what they sacrifice to
> get them out — is character definition. Do not rush past it.

## Psychological Conditions

The named psychological conditions — Rattled, Stressed,
Traumatised, Breaking — describe the **mechanical effect**
of psychological distress. What that distress actually looks
like, how it manifests in behaviour and expression, varies
profoundly by species.

A Rattled human looks different from a Rattled Skharri. A
Breaking Golvhaan is not the same experience as a Breaking
Vashori. The mechanics are identical; the phenomenology is
not. The **Species Psychological Profiles** section that
follows the condition descriptions gives the GM and players
the texture they need to make psychological conditions feel
real rather than generic.

Psychological conditions do not always accumulate in strict
order — a single sufficiently traumatic event can impose
Traumatised directly. The GM determines the severity of the
psychological impact of a specific event and imposes the
appropriate condition.

Repeated exposure to the same type of stressor may eventually
reduce its impact — a character who has processed a specific
type of horror develops a callus. This is a roleplay
development, not an automatic mechanical effect.

### Rattled

*Shaken. The normal confidence of a competent person has
been temporarily disrupted.*

**Sources:** A near-miss, a sudden unexpected threat, the
first serious combat, witnessing something disturbing,
an uncomfortable revelation about the situation.

**Effect:** Penalty die on **Wits** rolls.

**Recovery:** A moment of calm and grounding. Given ten
minutes away from the stressor — not necessarily safe,
just not actively threatening — Rattled clears. The
personal item (once per session) clears Rattled along
with any other stress condition if used.

> **Roleplay note:** Rattled is the most common psychological
> condition and the one most easily dismissed. Do not dismiss
> it. A character who is Rattled has been reminded that the
> universe can reach them. Play that for a moment before
> it clears.

---

### Stressed

*Sustained pressure is accumulating. The baseline has shifted.*

**Sources:** Extended periods under threat, repeated dangerous
situations without recovery, unresolved interpersonal conflict,
the psychological weight of command decisions with bad outcomes,
sustained isolation.

**Effect:** Penalty die on **Wits** and **Empathy** rolls.

**Recovery:** Requires genuine rest and social connection.
A full night's sleep in relative safety clears Stressed —
but only if the stressor has been removed or addressed.
Stress that continues to accumulate cannot be slept away.

A crewmate who takes time to actively support a Stressed
character — a real conversation, not a mechanical action —
allows the Stressed character to make a **Read roll**. On
a clean success (two or more sixes), the Stressed condition
clears. This requires genuine roleplay; it is not a free
recovery mechanic.

The personal item removes one psychological condition once
per session; Stressed is the most common target.

> **Roleplay note:** Stressed is the condition that reveals
> character relationships. Who notices? Who makes time? A crew
> that looks after each other's psychological state functions
> better under pressure — and the game rewards that attention
> mechanically as well as narratively.

---

### Traumatised

*Something has happened that will not simply resolve with rest.*

**Sources:** Witnessing atrocity, the death of someone close,
a profound moral injury, surviving something that should not
have been survivable, discovering something that cannot be
unknown.

**Effect:** Penalty die on **all rolls**.

**Special:** A Traumatised character has a **specific trigger**
— a type of situation, a sensory input, a context — that
the GM and player establish when the condition is acquired.
When the character encounters that trigger, they must make
a Wits roll. On a failure, they acquire a Rattled condition
immediately (in addition to Traumatised) as the trauma
reasserts itself.

**Recovery:** Cannot be slept away. Requires time, safety,
and active processing — either through roleplay (a significant
conversation with a trusted crewmate or the personal item
used in a meaningful scene) or through downtime between
missions. The GM may require a specific narrative event
before Traumatised will clear.

> **Roleplay note:** Trauma is permanent until it is worked
> through. The trigger mechanic is not a punishment — it is
> an invitation to explore what happened and what it means.
> Work with the player on what the trigger is and what
> encountering it looks like at the table.

---

### Breaking

*The edge. Everything is becoming too much.*

**Sources:** Accumulated stress without recovery, repeated
trauma, sustained impossible pressure, watching the crew
fall apart.

**Effect:** Penalty die on **all rolls**. Additionally,
once per scene the GM may call for a **Wits roll** when
the character faces a significant stressor. On a failure,
the character's response to the stressor is determined
by the GM in consultation with the player. This is not
control removed from the player — it is the GM and player
collaborating on what a person at the edge actually does.

**Recovery:** Cannot recover while the stressor continues.
Requires removal from the situation — genuine safety, not
just tactical safety — and significant time. Breaking clears
only after a period of downtime and active support from
trusted people.

> **Roleplay note:** Breaking is rare and should be treated
> with care. A character who reaches Breaking has been through
> something real. Work with the player to make it meaningful
> rather than merely penalising.
>
> If a player is uncomfortable with the Breaking mechanic
> for any reason, the GM should remove it and treat the full
> Stress Capacity track as incapacitation without the
> additional behavioural effects.

## Species Psychological Profiles

The condition names and mechanics above apply to all species.
What the conditions *look like* — how distress manifests in
behaviour, expression, and action — varies profoundly by
species. These profiles give the GM and players the texture
to make psychological conditions feel authentic rather than
generic.

When a psychological condition is imposed on a non-human
character, refer to their profile here. The mechanical effect
is unchanged; the roleplay expression follows the profile.

---

### Human

Humans are the baseline against which other species'
psychological expressions are measured — and it is worth
stating explicitly what that baseline is, because human
psychological distress is not a neutral default.

**Rattled:** Visible disruption of the competent surface.
Humans show shock — a brief freeze, a change in breathing,
a loss of the smooth social performance that most humans
maintain. They recover quickly but the moment was real.

**Stressed:** Humans under sustained pressure become either
withdrawn and short-tempered, or overly focused on task
to the exclusion of everything else. Both are avoidance.
The stress is often visible in physical tells — disrupted
sleep, changed appetite, a tension in the body that other
characters can read with Scan.

**Traumatised:** Humans are variable. Some go quiet. Some
become hypervigilant. Some replay the event compulsively
in conversation, looking for the moment where they could
have done something different. The trigger mechanic for
humans is often sensory — a smell, a sound, a visual
detail from the event.

**Breaking:** Human psychological collapse tends toward
one of two poles: shutdown (the person stops engaging,
stops making decisions, stops being present) or flooding
(everything comes out at once, uncontrolled and
overwhelming). Which pole depends on the person. Work
with the player to establish which direction their
character breaks.

---

### Shekhari

Shekhari psychological distress is characterised by
*withdrawal and cold calculation*. Emotional display is
not the Shekhari default; distress makes them less
expressive, not more.

**Rattled:** The Shekhari goes quiet. The sharp-tongued
commentary stops. They are assessing — running numbers
on the situation, on their options, on whether this crew
is still a viable context for them. Other characters who
know them well will recognise the quiet as alarming.
Characters without Exposure: Shekhari will likely not
notice anything is wrong.

**Stressed:** The mercantile calculus runs constantly
and audibly. A Stressed Shekhari starts pricing things —
the risk of the current situation, the cost of staying,
the value of various outcomes. They may begin to pull
back from crew commitments, not dramatically, but in
small ways that accumulate. They are protecting themselves
against a loss they are beginning to think is likely.

**Traumatised:** The Shekhari has identified this experience
as a data point about the world that changes their model.
Something they assumed was safe or manageable has been
revealed as otherwise. The trigger is the category of
thing that revealed this — not the specific event but
what it meant. A Traumatised Shekhari is very difficult
to read; the distress is almost entirely internal.

**Breaking:** A Breaking Shekhari makes a unilateral
decision. They have concluded that the current situation
is not survivable on the current terms, and they act
on that conclusion without consultation. This is not
cowardice — it is the Shekhari's deepest adaptive
mechanism firing at full intensity. They will do what
they need to do to survive. The crew may or may not
be part of that plan. If the loyalty transition has
happened — if the Shekhari has genuinely committed to
this crew — Breaking looks different: the unilateral
decision is in service of protecting the crew rather
than themselves. Either way, they act alone.

---

### Golvhaan

Golvhaan psychological distress manifests as *progressive
withdrawal of engagement*. The curiosity that defines them
goes away first. By the time a Golvhaan is visibly distressed,
they have been struggling for some time.

**Rattled:** The questions stop. A Golvhaan who has been
asking questions — about the situation, about the other
species in the room, about what is happening — goes quiet
when Rattled. The patient attention is still present;
the engagement has pulled back. Characters without
Exposure: Golvhaan will almost certainly not notice this.
Characters who know them well will find the silence
louder than anything else.

**Stressed:** The Golvhaan becomes very still. The
deliberateness that is always part of their character
becomes pronounced — they are spending significant
processing capacity on something they are not
discussing. They continue to function, continue to
help, but there is a distance in it. If asked directly,
a Stressed Golvhaan will generally answer honestly —
they do not dissemble about their internal state —
but they may not have found the words yet.

**Traumatised:** A Traumatised Golvhaan has encountered
something that has fundamentally contradicted their
model of how the universe works. Their xenophilia —
their genuine curiosity and openness — is the source
of their vulnerability here. They trusted; something
responded to that trust in a way that caused damage.
The trigger is almost always relational — a specific
type of betrayal or cruelty from a species or person
they had reason to trust. Recovery requires that trust
to be rebuilt, which takes considerable time.

**Breaking:** This is rare and significant. A Golvhaan
at Breaking has passed through a profound stillness
and come out the other side into something that does
not look like distress at all — until it becomes
obvious that the patience has failed. A Breaking
Golvhaan acts with complete deliberate commitment
in a direction determined by whatever finally broke
through the stillness. This may be protective, may
be catastrophically destructive of whatever caused
the distress, or may be a withdrawal so complete
that they simply leave and do not return. None of
these are impulsive. All of them have been building
for a long time.

### Vashori

Vashori psychological distress is *immediate and visible*
from the earliest stages. The same emotional bandwidth
that makes Vashori warm and volatile in normal circumstances
means that distress cannot be concealed. They feel it
loudly and they show it.

**Rattled:** Obvious and immediate. The Vashori's emotional
regulation — already active management rather than passive
calm — becomes visibly effortful. They are managing something
and everyone can see it. The recovery is also rapid; Vashori
process fast and the Rattled condition clears more easily
for them than for most species. A moment of genuine calm
is often enough.

**Stressed:** The management effort becomes the dominant
feature of the Vashori's behaviour. They are working very
hard to maintain function and the work is showing. The
emotional volatility mechanic becomes more likely to fire
— the Wits roll to avoid an uncontrolled reaction takes
a penalty die while Stressed. Small things that would
normally be managed may break through the surface.

**Traumatised:** Vashori feel trauma intensely and carry
it intensely. The emotional system that processes quickly
in normal circumstances finds some things genuinely
unprocessable — and those things sit, heavy and present,
in a way that contradicts the species' usual rapid
emotional flow. A Traumatised Vashori is simultaneously
more emotionally reactive in general (the system is
overloaded) and specifically stuck on the traumatic
content. The trigger is almost always emotional in
nature — a specific feeling, a specific type of betrayal
or loss. Recovery requires the emotional processing to
actually complete, which means the Vashori needs to
be able to feel it fully with support, not suppress it.

**Breaking:** Emotional flooding. Everything that has
been managed, processed, and held together comes apart
simultaneously. A Breaking Vashori does not shut down —
they are overwhelmed by the entirety of what they are
feeling, without the capacity to filter, prioritise,
or moderate. This is not rage or despair specifically;
it is everything at once. The GM and player should work
out what this looks like for this specific character —
some Vashori at Breaking become completely non-functional;
some channel the flooding into a single overwhelming
emotional commitment to one thing.

---

### Vaelkhi

Vaelkhi psychological distress manifests as *hypervigilance
and progressive isolation*. The choth instinct — the
deep need for the extended clan — is the wound that most
psychological damage reopens.

**Rattled:** The Vaelkhi becomes watchful in a way that
goes beyond their usual hunt-patience. Every position
in the room is assessed. Every exit is noted. They
withdraw slightly from the group — not dramatically,
but the physical distance increases. They are scanning
for the threat that produced the rattle, and they have
not yet determined whether it has passed.

**Stressed:** The group participation decreases
noticeably. A Stressed Vaelkhi moves toward solo
action — making decisions independently, positioning
independently, handling things without consultation.
This is not aggression toward the crew; it is the
absence of choth making itself felt. They are alone
in a way that the crew cannot entirely address, because
the crew is not a choth. Characters who understand
Vaelkhi will recognise this as distress; characters
without Exposure: Vaelkhi may read it as the Vaelkhi
simply preferring to work alone.

**Traumatised:** The choth absence becomes acute. A
Traumatised Vaelkhi is conscious of being fundamentally
without their social structure in a way that normal
circumstances allow them to set aside. The trigger is
often isolation-related — being separated from the crew,
being in a situation where they had to act completely
alone, or something that reminded them viscerally of
the permanence of having left the choth. Recovery
requires the crew to function, however imperfectly,
as a substitute social structure — not a choth, but
something that holds.

**Breaking:** The God of Ythri logic at its darkest.
A Breaking Vaelkhi begins to seek the decisive moment
— not the scream-and-leap impulsiveness of the Skharri,
but a deliberate, patient identification of the action
that ends things cleanly. This may be directed outward
(a commitment to a high-risk action against the source
of the distress) or inward (a philosophical acceptance
of death that removes the fear of risk entirely and
with it the survival instinct that would otherwise
function as a check). The GM and player should work
together on this carefully. A Breaking Vaelkhi is
not suicidal in the human sense — they are applying
the God of Ythri's logic to an impossible situation.
That is a distinction worth preserving in play.

---

### Skharri

Skharri psychological distress manifests through the
*honour-economy*. Every psychological condition, for a
Skharri, is mediated by how it interacts with their
standing — their own standing in their eyes, and the
standing of those around them.

**Rattled:** The honour threshold drops. A Rattled
Skharri is acutely sensitive to perceived slights —
things that would normally be processed and set aside
now register as challenges requiring a response. The
scream-and-leap Wits roll difficulty does not change
mechanically, but the triggers multiply. The GM should
note more situations as potential triggers for the
Wits roll while a Skharri is Rattled.

**Stressed:** The honour-economy is all-consuming.
A Stressed Skharri is running a continuous internal
accounting of their position — what has happened,
what it means for their standing, what is owed and
to whom. Social situations become minefields because
every interaction is being assessed for its honour
implications. The Skharri may become visibly
uncomfortable in situations that are ambiguous
in honour terms — mixed-species social settings
where the rules are unclear, situations where
their competence has been questioned, contexts
where they cannot establish their position.

**Traumatised:** The Skharri has experienced something
that damaged their honour-position in a way they have
not been able to address. This may be a defeat, a
failure to protect someone, a situation where the
honour-instinct fired and the outcome was wrong, or
a betrayal by someone whose honour-standing they
respected. The trigger is honour-adjacent — a
situation that resembles the one that caused the
damage. A Traumatised Skharri may withdraw from the
crew to avoid situations where their damaged position
becomes visible. Recovery requires the honour-debt
to be acknowledged and addressed — not necessarily
avenged, but addressed in terms the Skharri can
accept as settling the account.

**Breaking:** A sustained, cold, deliberate fury
looking for a target. This is not the scream-and-leap.
A Breaking Skharri has identified the source of the
damage and committed to addressing it with full
deliberate focus, regardless of the consequences
to themselves or the tactical situation. This is
more dangerous than the impulsive honour-instinct
precisely because it is considered. A Breaking
Skharri who has identified a target will pursue
it with the patience and commitment that defines
the species at its most capable — and they are
no longer weighing the cost.

---

### Nakhavi

Nakhavi psychological distress manifests as *cognitive
fragmentation* — the distributed intelligence begins
to lose coherence. The arms, partially autonomous in
normal function, become more independent as the central
brain's integrating capacity degrades.

**Rattled:** Visible arm-conflict. The semi-autonomous
ganglia pull in different directions — an arm reaching
toward something while the character is moving away,
two arms working at cross-purposes, a visible hesitation
in physical tasks as the distributed system disagrees
with itself. Characters with Exposure: Nakhavi recognise
this immediately. Characters without it may notice that
something seems off with the Nakhavi's movement without
being able to identify what.

**Stressed:** The translation layer becomes unreliable.
A Stressed Nakhavi's communication degrades — the
device that converts their chromatophore display to
speech begins to introduce errors, not from equipment
failure but from the source signal becoming less
coherent. They know what they want to say; the path
from cognition to chromatophore display to translation
is losing fidelity. They may begin to communicate
less, not from withdrawal but from frustration with
the degraded channel.

**Traumatised:** The distributed system fixates.
One aspect of the traumatic experience — a specific
sensory input, a specific decision point, a specific
moment — loops in the system, returned to repeatedly
by the distributed cognition in the way that a
committee returns to an unresolved question. The
Nakhavi is aware of this and finds it distressing
precisely because the distributed cognition is
their greatest strength and it is working against
them. The trigger is almost always the specific
input that is looping. Recovery requires the loop
to be interrupted — a new context for the memory
that allows the system to file it rather than
return to it.

**Breaking:** Full cognitive dissociation. The arms
act independently, the central brain unable to
coordinate. The Nakhavi is present but the
integration that makes them a single coherent
intelligence has temporarily failed. From the
outside, this is alarming — a Nakhavi whose arms
are all doing different things, whose chromatophore
display is producing conflicting signals, whose
communication is fragmented and contradictory.
The Nakhavi themselves are experiencing something
like a chorus of voices that have stopped being
a conversation. Recovery from Breaking requires
physical stillness, quiet, and time — the system
needs to be allowed to resynchronise without
additional input. A crewmate who simply stays
present and calm, without demanding communication
or decision, is providing the most useful support.

The 18-year lifespan gives Nakhavi psychological
distress a specific weight that other species do
not experience. Time spent incapacitated by
psychological conditions is time that cannot be
recovered. A Nakhavi who reaches Breaking and
requires downtime to recover is acutely aware of
what that downtime costs in terms of their remaining
lifespan. The GM and player should acknowledge this
without making it mechanical — it is texture,
not an additional penalty.

## Environmental Conditions

Environmental conditions are a third category — neither
purely physical nor purely psychological, but the body's
response to a hostile environment. They differ from named
conditions in one important way: **they resolve when the
environmental cause is removed**, without requiring recovery
time or medical treatment.

While active, an environmental condition occupies a slot on
the physical track and imposes the same penalty die as a
named physical condition. When the environment is addressed
— warmth restored, atmosphere corrected, pressure normalised
— the slot clears immediately.

If a character is already at maximum physical conditions when
an environmental condition would be imposed, it cannot be
absorbed — the character is incapacitated.

### Common Environmental Conditions

**Cold (Vashori):** A Vashori in an environment below 285 K
without adequate thermal protection is Cold. Penalty die on
all physical and Wits rolls. Below 275 K, two penalty dice —
this occupies two slots on the physical track. Clears
immediately when adequate warmth is restored.

**Hungry (Shekhari, Vaelkhi):** A Shekhari who has not eaten
within six hours of strenuous activity, or a Vaelkhi who has
missed two feedings, is Hungry. Penalty die on all physical
and Wits rolls. Clears when the character eats adequately.
Subsequent missed feedings add additional slots.

**Decompression:** An unsuited character exposed to hard vacuum
acquires one Decompression condition per round. Unlike other
environmental conditions, Decompression does **not** clear
when the environment is restored — it has caused physical
damage that requires treatment. Treat as Hurt conditions
once the character is back in atmosphere.

**Gravity stress:** A character operating outside their species'
comfortable gravity range for extended periods (more than a
few hours) without appropriate support acquires Gravity Stress.
Penalty die on physical rolls. Clears with rest in appropriate
gravity.

**Atmospheric:** A character breathing an unsuitable atmosphere
(wrong composition, toxic contaminants, insufficient pressure)
without protection acquires one condition per scene of exposure,
treated as Hurt once they are in safe atmosphere.

## Interplay Between Tracks

Physical and psychological conditions are tracked separately
but interact in play:

**Physical exhaustion affects psychological resilience.**
A character who is Wounded takes a penalty die on Stress
Capacity checks at the GM's discretion — the body's distress
makes the mind more vulnerable.

**Psychological conditions affect physical performance.**
The penalty dice from psychological conditions apply to all
rolls, including physical ones. A Traumatised character is
impaired in combat as well as in social situations.

**Both tracks can fill simultaneously.** A character who is
Wounded and Stressed is carrying conditions on both tracks.
Four conditions on either track causes incapacitation on
that track — they do not combine into a single total.

## Recovery

### Physical Recovery

| Condition | Without treatment | With treatment |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|
| Winded | 1 hour rest | Clears immediately |
| Hurt | Full night's rest | Few hours after Science roll |
| Wounded | Worsens without treatment | Long rest after Science roll (difficulty 1) |
| Critical | Dying without treatment | Days of rest after Science roll (difficulty 2) |

**Science rolls for treatment** use the Medic specialty where
applicable. Without the Medic specialty, treat the roll as one
difficulty level harder. Without adequate supplies, treat the
roll as one difficulty level harder again.

**Field medicine** — treating conditions under fire or without
proper facilities — imposes a penalty die on the Science roll.

**Extended recovery** — some Critical wounds leave effects that
persist beyond the condition clearing. The GM determines these
on a case-by-case basis: reduced function in an injured limb,
scarring, recurring pain under stress. These are narrative
consequences, not additional mechanical conditions.

### Psychological Recovery

| Condition | Recovery requirement |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Rattled | Ten minutes away from stressor, or personal item |
| Stressed | Full night's rest (stressor removed), or crewmate support + Read roll, or personal item |
| Traumatised | Significant narrative event + time; GM determines |
| Breaking | Downtime, safety, active support; GM determines |

**The personal item** removes one psychological condition once
per session. It can remove any psychological condition, including
Traumatised, if used in a meaningful scene that connects the
item to what happened. The GM and player should agree on what
that scene looks like before the condition clears.

**Downtime recovery** — between missions, psychological conditions
recover faster. A full downtime period (several days of genuine
rest and safety) clears one Traumatised or Breaking condition
if the player and GM agree the character has had the space to
process it. For Nakhavi, the GM should acknowledge the lifespan
cost of this downtime in the fiction, even if it carries no
additional mechanical penalty.

## The Condition Sheet

Conditions are tracked on the character sheet in two rows:

**Physical:** [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ] [ ] (Hit Capacity slots)
**Psychological:** [ ] [ ] [ ] (Stress Capacity slots)

Each filled slot records the specific condition and its cause.
Environmental conditions are marked with an (E) to distinguish
them from injury and trauma.

When a track is full, write **INCAPACITATED** across it.
Physical incapacitation: the character cannot act, move,
or fight. Psychological incapacitation: the character
cannot function socially, make decisions, or maintain
composure. In either case, they need help.

## Quick Reference — Conditions

### Physical Track (Hit Capacity = Strength + Endure)
| Condition | Effect | Recovery |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Winded | Penalty die: Endure, Labor | 1 hour rest |
| Hurt | Penalty die: all physical skills | Night's rest or treatment |
| Wounded | Penalty die: all rolls; worsens without treatment | Treatment + long rest |
| Critical | Penalty die: all rolls; Endure roll each scene or worsen | Significant treatment + days |

### Psychological Track (Stress Capacity = Wits + Empathy avg, rounded up)
| Condition | Effect | Recovery |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| Rattled | Penalty die: Wits | 10 min calm, or personal item |
| Stressed | Penalty die: Wits, Empathy | Rest + stressor removed, or support + Read roll, or personal item |
| Traumatised | Penalty die: all rolls; trigger mechanic | Significant narrative event + time |
| Breaking | Penalty die: all rolls; Wits roll vs stressors | Downtime + safety + support |

### Environmental Conditions
Occupy physical track slots. Clear when cause is removed.
Exception: Decompression — treated as Hurt once in atmosphere.

### Personal Item
Once per session: remove one psychological condition.
Requires a moment of quiet connection with the item.
