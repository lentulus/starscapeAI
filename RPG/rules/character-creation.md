# Character Creation

## Overview

A character is built in nine steps. The steps are designed to be taken
in order — each one builds on the last — but experienced players may
find it natural to move between them as the character concept develops.
What matters is that all nine steps are complete before play begins.

The process takes approximately 30-45 minutes for a first character,
less for subsequent ones. The GM should be present or available during
character creation — connections, exposure specialties, and starting
history all benefit from knowing what the campaign will look like.

---

## Step 1 — Concept

Before touching the sheet, write one paragraph describing who this
character is and why they are here.

The paragraph should answer three things:

- **Species and background** — what shaped them before the campaign
- **The defining quality** — the one thing that is most true about
  them as a person, not as a set of skills
- **Why here, why now** — what has brought them to this crew or
  this situation

This paragraph is the foundation. Everything in the subsequent steps
should be consistent with it, and when the steps produce something
that contradicts it, the concept paragraph wins. Mechanics serve
character, not the other way around.

> **Example:** *Sheshal is a Vashori who spent twelve years as a
> colonial medical officer on a human-majority frontier world,
> the only non-human doctor for four hundred people. She is
> defined by the particular competence of someone who has handled
> every emergency alone. She is here because the colony's
> sponsoring corporation dissolved, the colony was abandoned,
> and she has nowhere else to go — but she has her kit, her
> training, and an anger about what happened that has not cooled.*

---

## Step 2 — Species

Choose your species from the Species chapter. Read the full entry,
not just the mechanics section — the roleplay section is as important
as the stat block, and the *At the table* note tells you what the GM
and other players will expect.

Record the following:

**Attribute adjustment** — noted in Step 5 when attributes are
distributed, but keep it in mind now. It will take one attribute
above the normal creation maximum.

**Biological specialties** — record these now. They are separate
from career specialties and do not count against specialty limits.

**Environmental constraints** — note any conditions under which
your species takes penalty dice. These are active throughout play.

**Size** — note if your species is Small or Large. This affects
Fight, Labor, Stealth, and equipment as described in the Species
chapter.

### Advanced Species

If choosing Skharri or Nakhavi, confirm with the GM that you have
read and understood the full species entry including the GM notes.
These species are rewarding but require specific commitment from
the player. The GM should confirm that the player has a clear
answer to why this character is functioning in a mixed crew before
play begins.

### Non-Human Characters and Exposure

Non-human characters require Exposure: Human to read human emotional
states at full capacity. Without it, the Opacity Rule applies to
your human crewmates. This is not a disadvantage — it is a realistic
starting point for a character who has not spent significant time
among humans. Many non-human characters will take Exposure: Human
as their starting Exposure specialty (Step 4). Those who do not
have an interesting roleplay situation from session one.

---

## Step 3 — Background and Expertise

Choose your **background** from the six available:

- **Spacer** — life defined by ships, stations, vacuum, and the Belt
- **Military** — served in organised armed forces
- **Colonist** — from a frontier or developing world
- **Citizen** — from an established settled world or habitat
- **Bureaucrat** — worked inside large institutions
- **Merchant** — commercial life, trade, and negotiation

Then choose your **expertise** within that background. Full
descriptions of all backgrounds and expertises are in the
Archetypes chapter.

Record the following from your chosen archetype:

**Background specialty** — the specialty shared by all characters
of this background (e.g., *Zero-G Movement* for all Spacers,
*Threat Assessment* for all Military). This does not count against
specialty limits.

**Career skill bumps** — three skills, each bumped by the noted
amount. Apply these in Step 6 after distributing base skill points,
not before.

**Career specialties** — two or three specialties from the expertise
entry. Record these alongside your biological specialties.

**Starting gear** — the characterful package from the expertise
entry. This is not exhaustive — it represents what the career
typically provides. Personal additions are made in Step 8.

**Connections** — record the connections from the expertise entry.
Discuss with the GM how these connect to the campaign's setting.
A connection that has no relevance to where the campaign takes
place is a missed opportunity; work with the GM to ground them
in the specific region and political situation of the starting point.

---

## Step 4 — Exposure

Choose one **Exposure specialty** from the list in the Archetypes
chapter.

Exposure reflects which non-human (or human, for non-human characters)
species you grew up around, worked alongside, or spent enough formative
time with to understand at a social level. It lifts the Opacity Rule
for that species and grants specific social bonuses detailed in the
species entry.

**Remember the Opacity Rule:** Without the relevant Exposure specialty,
Read rolls for emotional or intentional content against that species
automatically fail, and Manipulate rolls take a penalty die.

Exposure comes from environment and experience, not from career. Any
background can produce any Exposure. A Military/Infantry character who
served in a mixed-species unit has Exposure to whichever species they
served alongside. A Citizen/Academic who studied xenobiology has
Exposure to the species they researched.

**Characters from demonstrably multi-species environments** may take
two Exposure specialties at the GM's discretion. Discuss this during
character creation rather than claiming it after the fact.

**Diplomatic Corps characters** automatically receive two Exposure
specialties as noted in the Archetypes chapter.

## Step 5 — Attributes

Distribute **14 points** across the five attributes.

| Attribute | Minimum | Maximum (creation) | Maximum (with species) |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Strength | 2 | 4 | 5 |
| Agility | 2 | 4 | 5 |
| Wits | 2 | 4 | 5 |
| Empathy | 2 | 4 | 5 |
| Intellect | 2 | 4 | 5 |

No attribute may be below 2 or above 4 at creation, before the
species adjustment is applied.

**Apply the species adjustment last.** If your species grants
Agility +1 and you have distributed 4 points to Agility, your
final Agility is 5 — one above the creation maximum, which is
correct. The species adjustment can take one attribute to 5;
no attribute may exceed 5 by any means.

Humans receive no species adjustment. Their 14 points are their
final attributes.

### What Attributes Mean

Distribute points according to the concept established in Step 1,
not according to optimisation. A character whose concept is a
patient scholar should not have Strength 4 unless that is part
of who they are.

**Strength** — raw physical power, endurance, and the capacity
for sustained physical effort. The body as a tool.

**Agility** — speed, coordination, and controlled movement.
The body as a precision instrument.

**Wits** — in-the-moment mental sharpness. Situational awareness,
reaction speed, and the ability to process and act under pressure.

**Empathy** — understanding and influencing others. The social
and emotional intelligence to model what another being is
thinking and feeling.

**Intellect** — applied knowledge. What you know, how deeply
you understand it, and whether you can reason through novel
problems from principles.

---

## Step 6 — Skills

Distribute **8 points** across the fifteen skills.

No skill may exceed **3** at creation.
No skill may be below **0** (unrated skills cost nothing and
remain at 0).

After distributing base points, apply the **career skill bumps**
from Step 3. Career bumps may take a skill above 3, up to a
maximum of 4. A skill may not exceed 4 at creation by any means.

### The Fifteen Skills

**Strength:** Fight, Endure, Labor
**Agility:** Shoot, Stealth, Move
**Wits:** Navigate, Operate, Scan
**Empathy:** Manipulate, Read, Command
**Intellect:** Tech, Science, Administer

Full descriptions of each skill are in the Skills chapter.

### Distribution Guidance

Eight points is enough to be competent in a few areas and
aware in others. A focused character might put 3 points in
their primary skill, 2 in a secondary, and spread the remainder.
A generalist might distribute more evenly.

Unrated skills (0) are not impossible — a character can attempt
almost anything using attribute dice alone. They are simply
areas where the character has no specific training. An unrated
skill should correspond to something the concept suggests this
character genuinely has not been trained in.

Career skill bumps represent what the background systematically
developed. They are not negotiable — a Spacer/Pilot has Operate
development because that is what the career does. If the career
bumps conflict with the concept, reconsider either the concept
or the archetype.

## Step 7 — Derived Values

The following values are derived from attributes and will be
used in the Conditions and Combat chapters.

**Hit Capacity** — the number of physical conditions a character
can sustain before incapacitation.

```
Hit Capacity = Strength + Endure skill
```

A character with Strength 3 and Endure 2 has Hit Capacity 5.
This is the number of physical conditions they can carry before
being incapacitated. Most characters will have Hit Capacity
between 3 and 7.

**Stress Capacity** — the number of psychological conditions
a character can sustain before breaking.

```
Stress Capacity = Wits + Empathy (average, rounded up)
```

A character with Wits 3 and Empathy 2 has Stress Capacity 3.
Psychological conditions — fear, isolation, sustained horror,
the consequences of the things that happen in space — are tracked
separately from physical ones.

**Carry** — the amount of significant gear a character can
carry without penalty.

```
Carry = Strength + Labor skill
```

Carry is an abstract value, not a weight limit. Each significant
item of gear occupies one Carry slot. Personal items, clothing,
and incidentals do not count. A character who exceeds their
Carry capacity takes a penalty die on Move rolls.

> **Playtest note:** These derived values are the most likely
> candidates for adjustment. If characters feel too fragile,
> increase the base values. If combat feels too slow because
> characters absorb too much, reduce them. Track which end
> of the scale causes problems first.

## Step 8 — Gear and Personal Item

### Starting Gear

Record the starting gear package from your archetype (Step 3).
This represents what your background has provided — equipment
appropriate to your career and expertise.

All starting gear is assumed to be in functional condition.
Gear ratings reflect quality and condition combined; gear that
is poorly maintained degrades in rating over time.

### Additional Personal Gear

Beyond the career package, a character may start with up to
**three additional items** of personal gear. These should be
consistent with the concept and background — a former colonial
medic might carry personal medical supplies beyond their kit;
a military veteran might have kept a specific sidearm from
their service.

Additional items should have a story. *Why does this character
have this?* The answer does not need to be dramatic, but it
should exist.

### The Personal Item

Choose **one item** that is specifically and personally
significant to the character. This is not necessarily the most
valuable or most useful item they carry. It is the one that
matters to them.

The personal item has a mechanical function beyond its gear
rating:

**Once per session**, a character who spends a quiet moment
with their personal item — handling it, thinking about what
it represents, the context in which they use it — may remove
one stress condition. This requires a moment of genuine calm,
not a combat action.

The personal item is not indestructible. If it is lost,
damaged, or destroyed, the character loses this recovery
option until the item is recovered or a new one takes its
place over time. A new personal item cannot be designated
immediately — it takes sessions of play for an object to
accumulate that weight.

> **Examples:** A worn photograph. A tool that belonged to
> someone who taught them. A specific piece of music on an
> ancient storage medium. A child's drawing on a colony world
> that no longer exists. The item should cost nothing and
> mean everything.

## Step 9 — Name, History, and the Three Questions

### Name

Choose a name appropriate to the species and background.
Non-human characters use names from their species' culture —
the species entries provide flavour for naming conventions
without prescribing specific names.

Human characters from different planetary or station cultures
will have names reflecting those cultures. A character from
a long-established Belt habitat may have a name quite different
from someone from a core world.

### History

Write two or three sentences of specific history. Not biography —
specific history. The moment that changed something, the decision
that cannot be undone, the thing that happened that is still
happening in some sense.

This is distinct from the concept paragraph in Step 1, which
described who the character is. History describes what made
them that way.

### The Three Questions

Answer these three questions in writing before the first session.
They do not go on the sheet — they go in a note that the player
keeps.

**What do you want?**
Not what your character wants in general — what do they want
*right now*, at the start of the campaign? Something specific
and achievable in principle, even if not easily. The desire
that will shape their decisions in the first few sessions.

**What are you running from or toward?**
Something behind them or ahead of them that has momentum.
A thing that will arrive eventually regardless of what the
character does. This is the GM's invitation to bring that
thing into the campaign at the right moment.

**Who outside this crew matters to you?**
One person, named, with a relationship defined. They may be
alive or dead, near or far, in contact or lost. They are the
human (or alien) anchor — the reason the character's choices
have weight beyond the immediate situation.

Share these answers with the GM. They are not secret. They are
the starting material for the campaign's personal layer.

---

## Step 10 — Connections (Group)

After all characters are created, the group takes one additional
step together: **establishing connections between characters**.

Each character identifies one relationship to another PC:

- Someone they have worked with before (how did that go?)
- Someone they owe something to, or who owes something to them
- Someone they do not entirely trust but need
- Someone whose presence makes them more capable than they
  would be alone

These connections do not need to be reciprocal and should not
all be positive. A crew with complicated internal relationships
is more interesting than one that is uniformly friendly from
session one.

> **Roleplay note:** The internal crew dynamic is one of the
> richest veins of roleplay in a ship-based campaign. Who trusts
> whom, who has history, who is still forming an opinion —
> these relationships develop through play, but starting with
> one established connection per character gives the first
> session something to work with beyond the immediate situation.

## The Completed Character

A completed character has:

- [ ] Concept paragraph (Step 1)
- [ ] Species recorded with attribute adjustment, biological
      specialties, and environmental constraints (Step 2)
- [ ] Background and expertise recorded with background specialty,
      career skill bumps, career specialties, starting gear,
      and connections (Step 3)
- [ ] Exposure specialty recorded (Step 4)
- [ ] Five attributes distributed, species adjustment applied (Step 5)
- [ ] Skills distributed, career bumps applied (Step 6)
- [ ] Hit Capacity, Stress Capacity, and Carry calculated (Step 7)
- [ ] Starting gear recorded, personal item identified and described
      (Step 8)
- [ ] Name, history, and three questions written (Step 9)
- [ ] One inter-PC connection established (Step 10)

---

## Specialty Summary

A completed character has the following specialties, from
different sources:

| Source | Count | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Biological (species) | 1–2 | From species entry; no limit |
| Background | 1 | Shared by all characters of this background |
| Career (expertise) | 2–3 | From expertise entry |
| Exposure | 1–2 | From Exposure list; 2 for Diplomatic Corps or multi-species environments |
| **Total** | **5–8** | Before any development in play |

This is enough specialties to feel specific and capable without
feeling complete. There should be obvious gaps — things the
character cannot do as well as they would like, situations where
another character is clearly better placed. Those gaps are where
growth happens.

## Designer's Notes — Playtest Values

The following values are most likely to require adjustment during
playtesting. Watch for the symptoms described and adjust accordingly.

**Attribute array (14 points, min 2, max 4):**
- *Too weak:* Characters feel identical because the spread is too
  narrow. Solution: increase to 15 or 16 points.
- *Too strong:* Characters start feeling like finished products
  with no room to grow. Solution: reduce to 13 points or tighten
  the maximum to 3.

**Skill points (8 points, max 3 at creation):**
- *Too few:* Characters feel incompetent outside their narrow
  specialty, which reduces roleplay options. Solution: increase
  to 10 points.
- *Too many:* Characters start with no obvious growth direction.
  Solution: reduce to 6 points or lower the creation maximum to 2.

**Career skill bumps:**
- *Too significant:* The career bumps overwhelm the base point
  distribution and all characters of the same archetype feel
  similar. Solution: reduce bumps to +1 total across three skills
  rather than the current distribution.
- *Too minor:* Career feels decorative rather than formative.
  Solution: add a fourth skill bump or allow one bump to reach +2.

**Hit Capacity:**
- *Characters die too easily:* Add Endure skill value to the
  formula or increase the base value by 1.
- *Combat drags because characters absorb too much:* Remove the
  skill component — use Strength alone.

**Stress Capacity:**
- *Stress conditions accumulate too fast:* Increase the formula
  or add a flat +1.
- *Stress conditions never feel threatening:* Reduce the formula
  or make specific stress conditions harder to remove.

**Personal item (once per session, remove one stress condition):**
- *Too powerful:* Change to once per long rest rather than once
  per session.
- *Rarely used:* The situations calling for calm are too infrequent.
  Consider allowing its use in any moment of genuine quiet, even
  briefly during a scene.

---

## Quick Reference — Character Creation Steps

1. **Concept** — one paragraph, who and why
2. **Species** — attribute adjustment, biological specialties,
   environmental constraints
3. **Background and expertise** — background specialty, career
   skills, career specialties, gear, connections
4. **Exposure** — one specialty; two for Diplomatic Corps or
   multi-species environments
5. **Attributes** — 14 points, min 2, max 4; apply species
   adjustment last
6. **Skills** — 8 points, max 3; apply career bumps after,
   max 4 total at creation
7. **Derived values** — Hit Capacity, Stress Capacity, Carry
8. **Gear and personal item** — career package, up to three
   personal items, one significant personal item
9. **Name, history, three questions** — share with GM
10. **Group connections** — one inter-PC relationship each
