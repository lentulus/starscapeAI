# Nhaveth: What You Know

*This document contains information your character has acquired through
research, experience, or reliable sources. It is not common knowledge.
Handle it accordingly.*

---

You know what the Nhaveth are: neural parasites, native to a standard
habitable world, who evolved alongside a sub-sapient hominid species
called the Kethara. An adult Nhaveth controls a Kethara host completely —
the parasite's intelligence directs all behaviour; the host provides the
body. In natural form the Nhaveth is approximately 0.6 m, limbless,
physically fragile. You will almost certainly never see one in natural
form. What you will see is the host.

The Kethara you encounter is not the Kethara. The intelligence behind
those eyes is ancient, patient, and assessing you.

---

## What To Look For

Most people who interact with a Nhaveth-controlled host never know what
they are dealing with. The control is complete and the Nhaveth have
had centuries to learn how to make the host appear natural.

But the co-evolutionary relationship between Nhaveth and Kethara is not
perfect, and certain tells survive even in a well-controlled host.
None of these is conclusive on its own. Together, in a host that shows
several, they are damning.

---

### The Blink

Kethara blink at a normal biological rate, and — like most species —
they blink reflexively in response to sudden stimuli: a loud sound, a
fast movement, something unexpected. The reflex is fast and automatic.

A Nhaveth-controlled host blinks slightly too infrequently at rest —
the parasite's attention management suppresses unnecessary reflexes.
More importantly, the **startle blink** is delayed. If something
unexpected happens in the room, a Kethara blinks immediately. A
Nhaveth-controlled host blinks a fraction of a second later, because
the signal has to travel through the parasite's neural web before
the motor response fires.

This is subtle. You will not see it in a single glance. You will see
it if you are watching, and if something unexpected happens while
you are watching.

*Detection: Scan, difficulty 2 with this knowledge. Requires an
unexpected event occurring during observation.*

---

### The Asymmetry

The Nhaveth neural web sits slightly off-centre in the host skull —
the parasite's attachment point is anatomically consistent but not
perfectly bilateral. Under normal conditions this produces no visible
effect. Under physical or cognitive stress, **fine motor asymmetry
emerges**: one hand steadier than the other, weight distribution
favouring one side, slightly uneven grip strength if you can observe
physical tasks closely.

This is most visible in situations requiring precise bilateral
coordination — handling small objects with both hands, climbing,
writing. A Kethara under stress is symmetrically stressed. A Nhaveth-
controlled host under stress shows the asymmetry.

*Detection: Scan or Read, difficulty 2 with this knowledge. Requires
a context where fine motor coordination is observable.*

---

### The Temperature

The Nhaveth organism generates a localised metabolic heat signature at
the base of the host's skull, where the neural web attaches. This is
a small but consistent elevation above the surrounding tissue — not
feverish, not obvious to casual contact, but detectable.

Close physical contact at the neck and upper spine will reveal this
to a character paying attention. A thermal sensor at close range (within
a metre) will show it clearly. Standard bioscan equipment used for
medical purposes will flag it as an anomaly, though not necessarily
identify its cause.

*Detection: Read (physical contact) or Tech (sensor), difficulty 2
with this knowledge. Requires close proximity.*

---

### The Recall Gap

Nhaveth controlling a host have access to the host's memories — the
co-evolutionary relationship ensures basic memory integration. But the
integration is not perfect, and there is a measurable difference between
how a Nhaveth retrieves memories and how a native consciousness does.

Ask a person about their childhood — a specific memory, a specific
name, a specific place. They answer with the natural fluency of someone
accessing their own experience. A Nhaveth-controlled host answers the
same question with a **fractional pause** — not long, not obvious in
a single instance, but consistent. The pause is retrieval, not
recollection. Questions about the immediate present are fluent. Questions
about personal history that the Nhaveth was not present for take
slightly longer.

This is nearly impossible to catch in casual conversation. In a formal
interview, or in a situation where you are asking pointed personal
questions while watching closely, it is detectable.

*Detection: Read, difficulty 2 with this knowledge. Requires a
conversation with personal historical content, and a character who
is watching for it specifically.*

---

## What To Do With This

Detection is not confrontation. A character who has identified a
Nhaveth-controlled host has identified a situation that requires
careful handling, not an enemy to be immediately attacked.

Consider:

- What does this Nhaveth want from this interaction?
- Which court do they represent?
- Are other Nhaveth present, possibly undetected?
- What happens to the host if the Nhaveth is exposed or killed?
  (The Kethara is an innocent victim of a parasitic relationship,
  not a collaborator. Killing the host to get the parasite is a
  choice with moral weight.)
- Who else in the room knows what you know?

The Nhaveth has been doing this for a very long time. Proceed
accordingly.
