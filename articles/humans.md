*"When we made contact, there were eleven of them waiting. They had each arrived independently. None of them could speak for the rest. All of them claimed they could."*

---

The first difficulty with the Humans is definitional. Every other species in this region of space arrives at first contact as something coherent — a civilisation, a court, a hive, an empire, a polity that can be addressed, negotiated with, and held to commitments. The Humans do not. What you encounter when you encounter them is a fragment: one government, one bloc, one ideology, one era's dominant faction, speaking as though it represented the whole and managing not to. Behind it, in every direction, are the others — competitors, enemies, dissidents, splinter states, successor governments, revolutionary movements, and schismatic branches of movements that themselves splintered thirty years ago. There is no centre. There has never been a centre. Trying to treat them as a unified species is a persistent analytical error that has cost every contact civilisation that attempted it.

They are, physically, unremarkable. Bilateral, bipedal, endothermic, mid-range in mass and height, short-lived by the standards of their neighbours. They have no unusual sensory capability, no exoskeletal armour, no biological weapons, no collective cognition. In any direct physical comparison with most of the species they share this region of space with, they are not the largest, not the fastest, not the most resilient. What they are, when the comparison is made at the level of technology adoption and doctrinal evolution, is by a considerable margin the fastest.

A Human polity that encounters a new technology does not study it for a generation and then cautiously implement selected elements. It takes it apart, reverses what it can reverse-engineer, and produces a variant adapted to its own strategic requirements before the original manufacturer has finished publishing the technical specifications. This has happened, in documented history, in domains ranging from propulsion to weapons to agriculture to communication. The speed is not the product of any particular Human genius — their individual cognitive capability is moderate — but of the competitive pressure that their perpetual internal conflict generates. A polity that is slower than its neighbours to adopt a new capability will be replaced by those neighbours. This has happened often enough in Human history that the survivors are those for whom fast adoption became institutional reflex.

The second consequence of their fragmentation is that their expansion is uncoordinated and therefore uncontainable in the way that a directed expansion can be contained. The Vardhek expand according to a program; the program can, in principle, be disrupted by disrupting the program's institutional home. The Kraathi expand according to a crusade; the crusade follows legible logic that a determined adversary can anticipate and position against. The Humans expand because multiple competing entities, each responding to local resource pressure and each modelling expansion as a solution to that pressure, push outward simultaneously and independently. There is no throat to cut. There is no program to subvert. A polity that defeats one Human faction discovers that three others have been establishing presence in the systems it was defending while the conflict occupied its attention.

Their xenophilia score, expressed as a species average, is meaningless. What the average conceals is a variance so wide as to make the number useless for prediction. On contact with any given Human polity, you may encounter genuine curiosity, instrumental openness, wary transactionalism, or hostility framed in ideological terms that took decades to develop and will take decades to shift. All of these dispositions are represented simultaneously in different Human governments, in different human institutions within those governments, and sometimes in different individuals within the same institution. The species cannot offer a consistent diplomatic face because there is no diplomatic face that is not contested internally.

Their memory is short relative to their lifespans, which are short relative to their neighbours. A Human with a grievance will pursue it with genuine intensity for perhaps a decade before the political conditions that generated the grievance have changed enough that pursuing it becomes structurally awkward. Their institutional memory is longer but imprecise — embedded in records that are selectively maintained, selectively cited, and reinterpreted by successive administrations to support whatever position those administrations currently require. They do not have the Nhaveth's six-century personal recall or the Vardhek's generational ledger. They half-remember, which has its own dangers: enough memory to sustain resentment, not enough to accurately reconstruct what generated it.

The strategic assessment that emerges from all of this is uncomfortable in its honesty. The Humans are not the most capable species in the region by most individual metrics. They are also very probably the least containable. Their fragmentation, which reads initially as a weakness, is structurally protective: an adversary that defeats a Human state has defeated one fragment while the rest continue. Their adaptability means that even deep setbacks produce tactical evolution rather than collapse. Their expansionism, uncoordinated and therefore unpredictable, fills vacuum regardless of who is theoretically managing the vacuum.

The first large-scale conflict in this region of space is probably a Human war. The most persistent ongoing instability in this region of space will almost certainly be Human. The question any adjacent civilisation has to answer is not whether Human expansion will arrive, but which of its many independent manifestations will arrive first, and whether those manifestations are currently at war with each other or between wars.
