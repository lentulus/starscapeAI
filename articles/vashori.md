*"I am not offended. I am simply — yes. I am offended. But I find you fascinating anyway. Please continue."*

---

The Vashori are scaled. This is the first thing, and it matters more than it sounds, because scaled bipeds from hotter worlds carry an unconscious association in the human mind with cold affect, deliberate movement, predatory patience. The Vashori will spend the first ten minutes of any encounter systematically dismantling every one of those assumptions, and they will do it while being genuinely delighted to meet you.

They run warm in the emotional sense even though they are ectotherms in the biological one. The combination is arresting. Their bodies need heat — below roughly seventeen degrees Celsius they slow down, become uncharacteristically deliberate, lose the quick-fire energy that makes them so engaging. But when the temperature is right, they are among the most expressive, reactive, and communicatively alive species any human diplomat will encounter. They get enthusiastic. They get offended. They get enthusiastic again. They will tell you a story with their whole body. They find the human tendency toward emotional restraint slightly baffling and slightly endearing.

The key insight is that their emotionality is not volatility in the dangerous sense. A Vashori who flares up at a perceived slight will, in most cases, have cooled significantly before the next sentence. The flare is genuine, the reconciliation is genuine, and the fact that both happen quickly does not mean either is shallow. What it means is that you need to be able to read the cycle rather than simply react to the peak. A human negotiator who responds to the flare as though it were a settled position has misread the room.

They are carnivores, which surprises people who expect the diplomatic, scholarly species to be herbivores. Apex reptiloid ancestry, protein-dependent, though flexible enough to adapt to most diets with some effort. They do not share the Kraathi moral horror at other species' eating habits and have no particular view on what you choose to eat, which makes cross-cultural meals considerably less fraught than they are with several other species.

Their civilisation is old and stable. They have not been empire-builders in any sustained way — their expansionism is genuinely modest, focused on trade networks rather than territorial acquisition, and their long history reflects a preference for sophisticated commercial relationships over the kind of dominance that requires continuous military investment. They are capable of war. They simply find it a poor return on the capital.

This makes them excellent partners and, on the whole, low-risk neighbours. The complication is their memory. Vashori remember slights with an intensity that their rapid emotional cycling can disguise. They will forgive — they forgive relatively easily, by the standards of honour-culture species — but forgiveness and forgetting are different things, and a Vashori who has been genuinely wronged will carry the fact of it for a very long time, surfacing at inconvenient moments with the full fresh weight of the original injury. They do not hold grudges ostentatiously. They hold them quietly, and you may not know you are being held accountable until the accounting arrives.

Reproductively they are deliberate: small clutches of carefully tended eggs, long maturity periods, the considered investment pattern of a species that lives well past a century and structures its generational expectations accordingly. They do not replenish quickly after losses. This shapes their risk calculus in ways that are not always visible in their affect — the cheerful, curious exterior sits on top of a civilisation that has learned, across its long history, that certain kinds of damage cannot be repaired in any reasonable timeframe.

The honest summary is that the Vashori are the species most humans will get along with most easily, which is either a straightforward diplomatic advantage or a reason to pay closer attention to the fine print, depending on how carefully you have read their history.
