*"The host you are looking at is not who you are talking to."*

---

The first thing to understand is that the body across the table from you is not the Nhaveth. The body is a Kethara — large, strong, bipedal, bearing the insignia of whichever court its passenger currently controls. The Kethara is roughly the physical dimensions of a large human, well-muscled, with the slightly vacant affect of a sub-sapient organism whose higher cognitive functions have been comprehensively replaced. The Kethara's own personality, such as it ever was, is not present. What is present, coiled around the Kethara's brainstem in a configuration that took millions of years of co-evolution to achieve, is roughly half a kilogram of vermiform neural tissue that is watching you with considerably more attention than the host's expression conveys.

This is the Nhaveth.

In natural form they are limbless, approximately the length of a forearm, with a recessed fringe of micro-manipulators useful for fine work but inadequate for anything requiring reach or force. They do not interact with the world in natural form if they can avoid it. Everything — diplomacy, warfare, labour, negotiation — is conducted through a Kethara host. The hosts are, from the Nhaveth perspective, operational platforms. They are replaced when they age, when they are damaged, when a better specimen is available. A senior Nhaveth may have occupied dozens of bodies across their life.

Their effective lifespan, through sequential host transfer, extends to roughly six centuries.

This is the second thing to understand.

A Nhaveth elder has six hundred years of first-hand memory. Not institutional memory, not historical record — personal, lived, bodily memory of every alliance made and broken, every court that rose and fell, every betrayal committed against them or by them. They were present. They remember the face of the individual who did it, the specific words that were used, the context that made it feel reasonable at the time, and every subsequent event that retroactively confirmed how right they were to resent it. Their grievance memory is absolute in a way that no species with a normal lifespan can quite match, because normal lifespans provide a natural mechanism for moving on. The Nhaveth have no such mechanism.

The social structure is courts — factions organised around a dominant Nhaveth whose host carries the court's insignia. Each court is a tyranny: the dominant Nhaveth directs everything, the hierarchy within is absolute and physically enforced through the parasitic relationship itself. Between courts there is essentially perpetual warfare, territorial jostling, and the patient accumulation of leverage for eventual confrontations that may be decades in preparation. No court has ever unified the species. The dynamics that would be required for unification — trust, shared interest, the subordination of individual advantage to collective purpose — are precisely the dynamics that Nhaveth history has made impossible.

What they want, at the civilisational level, is host population access. More Kethara populations to parasitise means more larvae successfully matured means more Nhaveth. This is the engine of their expansion, and it explains both their territorial aggression and their relationship to non-Nhaveth species: other sophonts are categorised as host-stock (potentially exploitable), labour (useful but subordinate), or threats (to be managed). The category of peer does not exist in their taxonomy. It was not adaptive.

Interacting with them requires holding several uncomfortable facts simultaneously. The charming, articulate entity presenting as a Kethara host is genuinely intelligent, genuinely capable of sophisticated negotiation, and entirely likely to honour an agreement precisely as long as honouring it serves the court's interests. The emotional affect they project through the host is managed rather than spontaneous — centuries of experience with other species have produced sophisticated models of what expressions produce useful responses. They are not incapable of pragmatic co-operation. They are incapable of the kind of trust that makes co-operation durable beyond mutual interest.

And if you wrong them, in any form, in any context, in any negotiation across any number of years — they will remember. Not as a policy. As a fact that is simply present in their memory, as vivid as the day it happened, with a six-century lifespan still ahead of them to find the right moment.
