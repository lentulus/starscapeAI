*"They attacked before the analysis was complete. They always attack before the analysis is complete."*

---

The Skharri are large, furred, and built along lines that the human visual system parses immediately and accurately as predatory. Twice human mass on average, digitigrade legs that carry them at unsettling speed, tawny coat over a frame that is all fast-twitch muscle and dense bone. The face is recognisably felinoid in a way that triggers something old and instinctive in the human threat-response. This is, in the case of the Skharri, an appropriate response.

They are obligate carnivores. Fresh meat, strongly preferred — scavenged food is a cultural insult, synthesised food is tolerated with visible distaste, and the dietary narrowness compounds with a social structure that makes any acknowledgment of weakness or limitation close to unthinkable. They eat what predators eat. They live as predators live. The civilisational superstructure built on top of that baseline has refined and ritualised the underlying drives rather than moderating them.

The defining concept is honour, and it operates in ways that are both deeply familiar and deeply dangerous to human observers because they look like recognisable values — courage, loyalty, pride, the keeping of one's word — while producing behavioural outcomes that are reliably catastrophic at the strategic level. The problem is the scream-and-leap instinct: when honour demands action, the Skharri act, and honour demands action before analysis is complete. This is not a failure of intelligence. Skharri strategic capacity is considerable when they can deploy it. The difficulty is that the biological and cultural pressure to respond to perceived dishonour is faster than strategic analysis, and it has started wars that no subsequent strategic assessment would have recommended.

They have lost several interstellar conflicts by this mechanism. They know they have lost them by this mechanism. They have not changed. Tradition-bound honour cultures do not change quickly, and to change this particular response would require acknowledging that the instinct itself is a liability, which would itself be experienced as dishonour. The loop is closed.

Within a pride, the Skharri are impressive. Loyalty to the patriarch and family unit runs deep enough to be described without exaggeration as fierce. The hierarchy is absolute — rank determines everything, and nameless males, those who have not accumulated sufficient honour to earn a formal designation, have essentially no social standing — but within that hierarchy the bonds are genuine and the willingness to sacrifice for the group is total. A Skharri fighting for their pride is not performing courage. They simply do not have an off switch for it.

The inter-pride politics are another matter. Clans compete for rank and territory with a bitterness that reflects the same competitive drives operating at the group level rather than the individual one. The Skharri civilisation is not unified; it is a collection of intensely loyal small units in continuous low-grade competition with each other. This is both a structural weakness and a structural strength: it is hard to collapse something that is already distributed, but it is also hard to coordinate something that is constitutionally competitive.

Every species they have encountered has been categorised rapidly as prey, slave, or grudging temporary ally. The question of which category you occupy is largely settled by whether you can defeat them in a war and what the cost of doing so turns out to be. They do not hate other species. They do not recognise other species as peers. The distinction is meaningful.

Their memory for grievance is almost total. A Skharri lives for two centuries and does not forgive. Defeats are not filed away as historical events; they are carried as active debts requiring eventual settlement. The interstellar conflicts they have lost are not, in Skharri civilisational terms, over. They are paused. Anyone who has concluded from the current absence of Skharri military activity that the matter has been resolved has misunderstood the timescale on which the accounting is being kept.
