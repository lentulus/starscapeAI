*"No, you go ahead and underestimate me. It's fine. It's actually better if you do."*

---

The Shekhari are small. Cat-sized, standing perhaps to a human's waist on their hind legs, with a prehensile tail they use as a third hand when the geometry requires it. Most encounters happen looking down at them, or across a table that was designed for larger species, and the physiological gap produces a specific cognitive error in nearly every species that makes first contact with them: the assumption that small means manageable.

It does not mean manageable. It means they have had to be better than you at nearly everything that matters, for longer than your civilisation has been spacefaring, just to occupy the same commercial and diplomatic spaces that larger species take for granted. They are acutely aware of how they read to others and have made a productive decision to treat that misreading as an asset rather than an insult. By the time you have corrected your initial assessment, you are already in an agreement that you entered with the wrong frame.

They are traders in the most fundamental sense — not merchants as a profession, but a species that has organised its entire civilisational project around the flow of goods, information, and access between parties who did not previously know they needed each other. Wide-ranging, persistent, and extraordinarily adaptable to alien social contexts, a Shekhari on a foreign world spends the first few days mapping the informal power structures, the actual decision-makers behind the official ones, and the gaps in the local supply chain that no one has bothered to fill yet. This is not espionage; it is commerce. The line between the two is one they prefer to keep blurry.

They are not warmly curious about other species in the way the Nakhavi or Golvhaan are. The Shekhari interest in you is real, but it is the interest of someone trying to understand what you need and what you have, in order to identify where the deal is. If there is no deal — if your species produces nothing they want and wants nothing they can provide — the relationship does not go cold, exactly, but it becomes notably thinner. They will remember you. They will not prioritise you.

Their dietary flexibility is exceptional, which makes them useful on long voyages and in foreign environments where other species would struggle. They are omnivores with a genuinely wide range and no strong preferences, which means provisioning them is simple and cross-cultural meals are never a diplomatic incident. They eat what is available. This is a minor thing, and it is not minor — extended multispecies operations have run aground on subtler logistics than feeding preferences.

Socially, they are individualists without being antisocial. Family structures exist, but individual mobility is high and the emotional weight placed on group membership is light. They cooperate readily when incentives align and defect cleanly when they do not — not through treachery, but through the simple calculus that loyalty which costs more than it returns is not loyalty, it is a bad investment. They do not fracture groups dramatically. They leave them. A Shekhari who has decided an arrangement is no longer working quietly optimises their position and exits when the exit is cheap.

Their memory for being cheated is long and commercial rather than violent. They do not pursue vengeance in the way an honour-culture species would. They adjust their pricing. They route around you. They make themselves available to your competitors. They ensure, through patient and entirely legal commercial pressure, that the cost of having wronged them accumulates over years until it is simply more expensive to have them as an adversary than as a partner. This is, in practice, more effective than most of the alternatives.

The useful summary is that the Shekhari are excellent partners, reliable within the scope of a mutual interest, adaptable enough to be comfortable almost anywhere, and professionally pleasant in the kind of way that makes it easy to forget they are conducting a continuous, sophisticated assessment of every interaction. Which is, of course, exactly what they intend.
