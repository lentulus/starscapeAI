*"We have time. Sit. Explain it properly."*

---

The first thing you notice is the size. A Golvhaan standing in a standard docking bay makes the bay feel small. Four heavy legs carry a body mass that would challenge a freight loader, and above that mass rises a humanoid torso, long-necked, scaling-hided, with two arms that move with the unhurried deliberateness of something that has never once needed to hurry. Head height is around two and a quarter metres. The tail alone is longer than a human is tall. You do not crowd a Golvhaan. You arrange yourself around them, and they find this entirely unremarkable.

They come from a high-gravity world, which explains the build. The musculature is denser than it looks, the bones heavier, the whole frame engineered by evolution for a planet that pulled harder than Earth does. In our gravity they move with something close to ease. They find low-gravity environments mildly amusing, in the way that a strong swimmer finds shallow water. They will never say so directly.

What surprises most humans on first contact is the warmth. Given the scale and the physical confidence, you expect either indifference or menace. You get neither. Golvhaan are genuinely, uncomplicatedly curious about other species — not curious in the way a researcher studies a specimen, and not curious in the way a merchant sizes up a counterparty, but curious in the way a person is curious about a stranger at a dinner table who turns out to be fascinating. They will ask you questions. They will listen to the answers. They are not performing interest; they are interested.

This does not make them soft. A Golvhaan who decides a situation requires direct action is a significant physical and strategic event. They simply arrive at that decision slowly, deliberately, and having considered most of the available alternatives. The same patient temperament that makes them excellent diplomats makes them dangerous opponents when patience is finally exhausted, because by that point every option except the one they are about to take has already been ruled out.

They are omnivores in the most thorough sense — they will eat almost anything organic, in the enormous quantities that a body of that mass requires, without notable preference or fussiness. Provisioning a Golvhaan on a long voyage means bringing enough, not bringing anything particular. This makes them practical crew on extended expeditions. It also means they are entirely unbothered by alien food cultures in a way that species with strict dietary requirements are not.

Socially, they are clan-oriented but not claustrophobically so. Individual Golvhaan range widely, pursue their own purposes, and reintegrate with family groups across long absences without drama. Their clan structures have leadership, but the leadership is earned rather than inherited, and a Golvhaan of any rank is essentially impossible to intimidate through status alone. You cannot threaten something that masses seven hundred kilograms into deference. You can only earn its respect, which takes longer but lasts considerably longer too.

They disagree with each other frequently and resolve it verbally, over time. They are constitutionally disinclined toward the kind of internal political fracture that tears other civilisations apart. Not because they are uniformly of one mind — they are not — but because the temperament that produces patience in diplomacy also produces patience in disagreement. Schisms require momentum. Golvhaan arguments tend to run out of momentum before they reach that point.

Their one genuine strategic vulnerability is demographic. They bear one child at a time, raise that child slowly over decades, and live long enough that the pace feels natural to them. To a human observer it looks precarious. A war that kills a significant fraction of the Golvhaan population does not just kill individuals — it kills potential births, and the recovery from that loss plays out across generations. They are aware of this. It is, arguably, one of the reasons they are so disinclined toward war.

In human terms, the closest cultural analogue is a very old, very large, very relaxed person who has seen enough of the world to find almost everything in it interesting and almost nothing in it threatening — and who, if you were unwise enough to make them feel actually threatened, would turn out to be the most formidable thing in the room.
