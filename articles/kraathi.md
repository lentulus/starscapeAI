*"The question is not whether you eat meat. The question is only whether we have confirmed it."*

---

The Kraathi are large. This needs to be said before anything else, because the scale shapes everything about how an encounter with them unfolds. Centauroid — four-legged lower body carrying a horse's worth of mass, upright humanoid torso above that, head at roughly two metres. They travel in groups as a matter of biological necessity rather than social preference, and those groups tend to fill whatever space is available. A delegation of Kraathi does not sit across a table from you. It is present in the room in a way that makes the geometry of the room a secondary consideration.

They are herbivores. Obligate herbivores, which in their case means something more absolute than dietary preference — handling meat is a profound taboo, consuming it is unthinkable, and the smell of a carnivore's recent meal registers somewhere between distasteful and threatening on the Kraathi sensory scale. This is not, for them, a cultural sensitivity to be accommodated politely. It is the foundation of their entire civilisational project.

The Kraathi call carnivores *G'naak* — roughly, eaters of the dead — and they have decided, at the species level, as a matter of settled multi-generational policy, that the *G'naak* must be eliminated from the galaxy. This is not a fringe position within their civilisation. It is not the policy of a faction or a historical period. It is the central organising purpose of the Kraathi's expansion into space, backed by a military program prosecuted across thousands of worlds over a timeframe that makes human history look recent. They are not angry about it. They are not conflicted about it. They are methodical about it.

What this means for any initial contact scenario is that the first relevant question is dietary. If your species eats meat, you are not a potential trading partner or diplomatic counterpart in Kraathi terms. You are a target that has not yet been addressed. The Kraathi will not communicate this in the opener; they will establish it through inquiry, and the answer will determine every subsequent interaction.

This sits in arresting contrast with their social character in other respects. Among themselves, and with herbivore species, the Kraathi are thoroughly communal, deeply loyal, and genuinely supportive of the herd-group in ways that humans find almost touching. The herd dependency is biological — a Kraathi separated from companions is not merely lonely but cognitively and emotionally compromised in measurable ways. They are not built for isolation. They are built for togetherness, and within the herd that togetherness is warm, hierarchical in the way that large social animals are hierarchical, and remarkably stable.

They are also deeply conservative. Ancient traditions are maintained without modification across timescales that most civilisations would describe as geological. They do not adopt foreign practices, foreign technology, or foreign ideas readily. What worked three thousand years ago works now. The Long View is not unique to the Vardhek; the Kraathi version of it is simply aimed at a different target and prosecuted with less patience — they are not strategically subtle, but they are strategically persistent.

Their one significant military and diplomatic constraint, from a human analyst's perspective, is that their adaptability is genuinely limited. Their doctrine does not evolve quickly. A species or coalition that can stay out of direct confrontation long enough to develop a decisive technological or tactical asymmetry can, in principle, survive a Kraathi campaign. The difficulty is that the timescales involved are long, the Kraathi's territorial and population advantages are substantial, and their social cohesion means they do not fracture under prolonged pressure in the way that more factional species do.

The closed question, for any carnivorous species in this region of space, is simply: how long before the Kraathi arrive, and how much time is there to prepare?
